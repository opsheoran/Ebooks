1.1 Introduction: The Philosophy of Inference

The history of humanity is, in many ways, the history of learning from limited information. Whether it is a cook tasting a spoonful of sauce to judge the seasoning of the whole pot, or a scientist analyzing a few grams of soil to determine the fertility of a thousand-acre field, the core principle remains the same: the part represents the whole.

In Statistics, this "part-representing-the-whole" logic is formalized as Sampling Theory. It is the mathematical bridge that allows us to move from the known (the sample) to the unknown (the population). While complete enumeration (Census) was once considered the only "truth," modern pioneers like A.L. Bowley and Jerzy Neyman proved that a scientifically selected sample could often provide more accurate and timely information than a full count.

The Inductive Nature of Sampling

Sampling is an inductive process. We move from particular observations to general laws. The goal of this unit is to master the vocabulary and the logic that makes this transition mathematically sound and practically useful.

Without sampling, modern society would come to a halt. National governments use it to track unemployment; pharmaceutical companies use it to test life-saving drugs; and agricultural scientists use it to develop resilient crop varieties. In each case, the stakes are high, and the logic must be flawless.

1.2 Core Terminology & Fundamental Concepts

Before we can apply sampling techniques, we must speak the language of Statistics fluently. These definitions are the building blocks of everything that follows.

1.2.1 Population (The Universe)

The Population (denoted by \(N\)) is the aggregate of all possible units under investigation. It must be defined precisely in terms of content, units, extent, and time.

Example of Population

  • Target Population: All farmers in Haryana growing Basmati rice in the year 2026.
  • Observation Unit: An individual farmer.

1.2.2 Sample (The Subset)

A Sample (denoted by \(n\)) is a finite part of the population selected with the objective of investigating the characteristics of the whole. A sample is a proxy for the population.

1.2.3 Parameters vs. Statistics

This is a critical distinction that many students find confusing at first. Remember: Parameters come from the Population, while Statistics come from the Sample.

Characteristic Population (Parameter) Sample (Statistic)
Mean (Average) \(\mu\) (Mu) \(\bar{x}\) (X-bar)
Variance \(\sigma^2\) (Sigma Squared) \(s^2\) (s squared)
Proportion \(P\) \(p\)

Parameter

A parameter is a numerical constant derived from the entire population. It is usually unknown and we try to estimate it using sample data.

1.2.4 Estimator and Unbiasedness

An Estimator is a mathematical formula (a rule) used to calculate an estimate of a population parameter based on sample data.

An estimator \(t\) is Unbiased for parameter \(\theta\) if:
\(E(t) = \theta\)

If the expected value of our statistic matches the true population parameter exactly over many repetitions, our method is "fair" or unbiased. If \(E(t) - \theta = B(t) \neq 0\), we have a Bias in our method.

1.2.5 Standard Error (S.E.)

The standard deviation of the sampling distribution of a statistic is called its Standard Error. It tells us how much we expect the sample result to vary from one sample to another.

1.2.6 Sampling Unit and Sampling Frame

A Sampling Unit is the individual element (person, farm, house) that can be selected. The Sampling Frame is the list of all these units. Without a complete and up-to-date frame, your whole survey may be invalid (this is called Frame Bias).

1.3 The Absolute Need for Sampling

Why do we settle for a sample when we could, in theory, look at everyone? There are several compelling reasons that make sampling not just a choice, but a necessity.

1.3.1 Resource Constraints

Studying an entire population requires enormous Money, Time, and Personnel. For most research projects, a census is simply unaffordable. Sampling provides "adequate" information at a fraction of the cost.

1.3.2 Destructive Testing

In many fields, the process of observation destroys the unit. In such cases, a census would mean the total destruction of the population.

Examples of Destructive Testing

  • Testing the life of electric bulbs (they must burn until they fail).
  • Testing the breaking strength of a steel cable.
  • Determining the fat content of a container of milk.
  • Testing the effectiveness of a new batch of firecrackers.

1.3.3 Infinite Populations

If the population is infinite (like the number of stars or the fish in the sea), a census is physically impossible. Sampling is the only scientific way to study such phenomena.

1.3.4 The Accuracy Paradox

Surprisingly, a well-conducted sample is often more accurate than a census. Why? Because in a census, the volume of work is so massive that it's hard to supervise staff, leading to large non-sampling errors (mistakes in counting, lying respondents, entry errors). In a sample, we can use highly trained staff and better equipment, reducing these human errors significantly.

1.4 Census vs. Sample Survey: A Rigorous Comparison

The choice between a Census and a Sample depends on the objective of the study. Let's compare them across several dimensions.

Basis Census (Complete Enumeration) Sample Survey
Scope Each unit of the population is studied. Only a subset (fraction) is studied.
Economics Very expensive; requires massive budget. Economical; saves time and money.
Accuracy Results are theoretically exact but prone to human error. Results are estimates but errors are mathematically controlled.
Time Factor Takes months or years (e.g., National Census). Results available in days or weeks.
Supervision Difficult to supervise millions of workers. Easy to maintain high quality and supervision.

Limitations of Sampling

While sampling is great, it does have limits. It cannot be used if every unit must be known (e.g., creating a voter ID list for every citizen). It also requires expertise in statistical theory to avoid misleading results.

1.5 The 11 Principal Steps in a Sample Survey

Conducting a professional survey is a complex administrative and scientific task. Following these 11 steps ensures the results are reliable and valid.

  1. Statement of Objectives: Clearly state what you want to find. Ambiguity at this stage ruins the whole project.
  2. Definition of Population: Who exactly is being studied? "Farmers" is too vague; "Rice farmers in Hisar with >5 acres" is precise.
  3. Determination of Sampling Units and Frame: Identify the individual elements and get a list (Frame) of them.
  4. Selection of Sample Design: Decide how to pick the units (Simple, Stratified, Systematic, etc.) and determine the size (\(n\)).
  5. Method of Measurement: Will you use personal interviews, telephone calls, or mailed questionnaires?
  6. Questionnaire/Schedule Construction: Design the questions carefully to avoid bias and ensure clarity.
  7. Organization of Field Work: Hire and train investigators. Provide them with maps and clear instructions.
  8. Data Scrutiny and Editing: Check for "outliers" (impossible values) and missing data once the forms come back.
  9. Analysis of Data: Apply mathematical formulas to calculate means, variances, and confidence intervals.
  10. Reporting and Conclusions: Present the findings in a clear, readable report with graphs and tables.
  11. Information Gained for Future Surveys: Every survey is a lesson. Note what worked and what didn't for the next team.

1.6 Fundamental Principles of Sampling

Why does sampling work? It is not magic; it is based on two powerful mathematical principles.

1.6.1 Principle of Statistical Regularity

This principle stems from the Law of Large Numbers. It states that a moderately large number of items chosen at random from a large group are almost sure, on average, to possess the characteristics of the large group.

In simple words: Nature tends to follow a pattern. If you pick enough units randomly, the "random" fluctuations will cancel each other out, leaving you with the true average.

1.6.2 Principle of Validity

A sampling design is valid if it allows us to calculate an estimate of the sampling error from the sample itself. This is only possible in Probability Sampling.

1.6.3 Principle of Optimization

This principle focuses on efficiency. We aim to either:
(a) Minimize the cost for a given level of accuracy, OR
(b) Maximize the accuracy for a given fixed budget.

1.7 Taxonomy of Sampling Methods

The "Method of Selection" is the most important decision in any survey. We classify them into three categories:

1.7.1 Subjective (Judgment/Purposive) Sampling

The choice of sample units depends entirely on the discretion and judgment of the investigator. There is no mathematical rule.

  • Merits: Fast, easy, and can be used for very small pilot studies.
  • Demerits: Highly biased; no way to measure error; results cannot be generalized to the population.

1.7.2 Probability (Random) Sampling

Every unit in the population has a pre-assigned probability of being selected. This is the foundation of scientific statistics.

  • Merits: Eliminates personal bias; allows calculation of sampling error; provides valid results.
  • Demerits: Requires a sampling frame; more complex to organize.

1.7.3 Mixed Sampling

A combination of subjective and probability sampling. Often used in large-scale national surveys to balance cost and accuracy.

1.8 Data Collection Tools: Questionnaire & Schedule

How do we actually "get" the data from the people? We use two main tools:

1.8.1 The Questionnaire

A list of questions sent to the respondent (usually by email or post). The respondent reads and fills it out themselves.

  • Merits: Very cheap; large geographic coverage; respondent has time to think.
  • Demerits: High non-response rate; only for literate people; no way to clarify doubts.

1.8.2 The Schedule

A list of questions carried by an investigator. The investigator asks the questions and fills the answers themselves.

  • Merits: High response rate; can be used for illiterate respondents; investigator can clarify questions.
  • Demerits: Very expensive (travel + salary); investigator bias might creep in.

Part A: Subjective Deep-Dive (20 Q)

1. Define "Sampling Frame" and explain the consequences of using an inaccurate frame.
Detailed Solution: A sampling frame is an exhaustive list, map, or directory of all sampling units in the population. Consequences of inaccuracy: (a) Omission: Legitimate units are missed (under-coverage). (b) Inclusion: Non-existent units are included (over-coverage). (c) Duplication: Some units are listed twice, giving them a higher chance of selection. All these lead to serious Non-sampling bias.
2. Elaborate on the "Accuracy Paradox"—why a sample can be more accurate than a census.
In a census, the scale of operations is so large that we must employ thousands of unskilled workers, leading to massive Non-sampling errors. In a sample, we use a small, elite group of highly trained investigators and sophisticated equipment. Even though a sample has sampling error, its non-sampling error is so low that the Total Error (Sampling + Non-sampling) is often less than the total error of a census.
3. State and explain the "Principle of Statistical Regularity".
Derived from the mathematical theory of probability, this principle states that a moderately large number of items chosen at random from a large group will, on average, possess the same characteristics as the whole group. It is based on the idea that random fluctuations in individual units cancel each other out as the sample size grows.
4. Distinguish between a Parameter and a Statistic with 3 examples.
A Parameter is a characteristic of the population (fixed), while a Statistic is a characteristic of the sample (variable).
Examples:
1. Population Mean (\(\mu\)) vs Sample Mean (\(\bar{x}\)).
2. Population Variance (\(\sigma^2\)) vs Sample Variance (\(s^2\)).
3. Population Proportion (\(P\)) vs Sample Proportion (\(p\)).
5. Why is Destructive Testing the strongest argument for sampling?
If the testing process destroys the item (like checking the lifespan of a candle or the strength of an eggshell), a census would result in 100% loss of the population. Sampling is the only way to gather data while leaving the majority of the population intact for use.
6. List the 11 steps in a sample survey as per standard protocol.
1. Objectives 2. Population Definition 3. Unit/Frame identification 4. Selection of Design 5. Method of measurement 6. Questionnaire design 7. Fieldwork organization 8. Data scrutiny 9. Statistical analysis 10. Reporting 11. Archiving for future use.
7. Compare and contrast Questionnaire and Schedule methods.
Questionnaire: Sent by mail/post; respondent fills it; cheap; low response rate; requires literacy.
Schedule: Carried by investigator; investigator fills it; expensive; high response rate; works for anyone.
8. Define "Unbiased Estimator" mathematically.
An estimator \(t\) is unbiased for parameter \(\theta\) if the expected value of its sampling distribution is exactly equal to the parameter: \(E(t) = \theta\). This implies that in the long run, the average of all possible estimates will hit the target.
9. What are the major demerits of Subjective (Judgment) Sampling?
1. Results are highly influenced by investigator bias. 2. No mathematical basis for calculating error. 3. Sampling distribution is unknown, so confidence intervals cannot be made.
10. Explain the "Principle of Optimization" in sampling.
It refers to the trade-off between Cost and Precision. The goal is to obtain the maximum information (minimum variance) for a fixed budget, or to achieve a required level of precision at the minimum possible cost.

Part D: True or False (20 Q)

1. A sample can be more accurate than a census in large scale surveys. [True / False]
Answer: True
Reason: Better control over non-sampling errors using a smaller, trained team.
2. Parameters vary from sample to sample. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: Parameters are fixed constants of the population. Statistics vary from sample to sample.
3. Purposive sampling is more scientific than Random sampling. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: Random sampling has a mathematical basis and eliminates personal bias.
4. Non-sampling errors occur only in sample surveys. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: They occur in both census and sample surveys due to human mistakes.

Answer: True
