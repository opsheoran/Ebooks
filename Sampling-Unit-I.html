1.1 Introduction to Sampling

In our daily lives, we often use sampling without even realizing it. When a cook tastes a spoonful of soup to check the salt, they are sampling. When a doctor takes a few drops of blood to test for an illness, they are sampling. We don't need to drink the whole pot of soup or drain all the blood to know the quality or health status.

In Statistics, Sampling Techniques provide the scientific framework for this common-sense approach. It allows us to study a small part of a group (the sample) and make very accurate guesses about the whole group (the population).

Why do we use Sampling?

Imagine you want to know the average income of every person in India. Interviewing 1.4 billion people would be impossible—it would take decades and cost a fortune. Sampling allows us to get a very close answer by interviewing just a few thousand people in a systematic way.

1.2 Key Definitions & Terminology

Population (N)

The total set of all possible observations or units under study. If we are studying the height of students in a college, every single student in that college is part of the Population.

Sample (n)

A smaller group or subset selected from the population. If we pick 50 students from the college to measure their height, those 50 students form the Sample.

Parameter vs. Statistic

Parameter: A value calculated from the whole population (like the average height of ALL students). It is a fixed constant.

Statistic: A value calculated from the sample (like the average height of just the 50 students picked). It varies from sample to sample.

Finite vs. Infinite Population

Finite: The number of students in your university. You can count them. (e.g., N = 10,000).

Infinite: The number of stars in the sky or the number of fish in the ocean. It is impossible to count them all.

Sampling Frame

A list or map that identifies every unit in the population. For example, the college roll-call list is a Sampling Frame for studying the students.

1.3 Census vs. Sample Survey

A Census is a complete count—studying every single member of the population. A Sample Survey studies only a selected portion.

Comparison

Feature Census Sample Survey
Cost Very High Relatively Low
Time Takes a long time Very quick results
Accuracy Higher (in theory) High (if done scientifically)
Destructive Testing Impossible The only way

Example of Destructive Testing: If you want to test how much weight a bridge can hold before it breaks, or how long a bulb lasts before it fuses, you cannot test every unit (Census), because you would destroy everything. Sampling is the only way here.

1.4 Principal Steps in a Sample Survey

Planning a survey is like building a house. You need a clear plan. Here are the 11 essential steps:

  1. Define Objectives: What do you want to find out? (e.g., "I want to know the average yield of wheat per acre").
  2. Define Population: Who or what are you studying? (e.g., "All farmers in Hisar").
  3. Sampling Unit & Frame: What is the smallest piece? (e.g., "One individual farm"). Where is the list of farms?
  4. Data Collection Tool: Will you use a Questionnaire (filled by respondent) or a Schedule (filled by investigator)?
  5. Selection of Design: Will you use Simple, Stratified, or Systematic sampling?
  6. Training: Train the people who will go to the field to collect data.
  7. Field Work: Actually going out and collecting the data.
  8. Data Scrutiny: Checking for errors or missing information in the collected data.
  9. Analysis: Using statistical formulas to calculate results.
  10. Reporting: Writing the final results clearly for others to read.

1.5 Types of Sampling

There are three broad ways to pick a sample:

1. Subjective (Judgment) Sampling

The researcher uses their own personal "judgment" to pick the units. There is no mathematical rule. This is often biased but useful for quick, informal studies.

2. Probability Sampling

This is the gold standard. Every unit in the population has a known mathematical chance of being selected. This eliminates personal bias. Examples include Simple Random Sampling and Stratified Sampling.

3. Mixed Sampling

A combination of both. For example, picking which colleges to study (Judgment) and then picking students from those colleges using a random rule (Probability).

1.6 Practice Exercises

Part A: Subjective Questions

1. Distinguish between Population and Sample with examples.
2. Why is sampling mandatory in destructive testing? Explain.
3. What are the merits and demerits of the Questionnaire method?

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions

Q1. Any numerical value calculated from the sample is called:
(a) Parameter    (b) Statistic    (c) Bias    (d) Error
Correct Answer: (b) Statistic
Explanation: Values from the population are parameters; values from the sample are statistics.
Q2. The list of all sampling units in the population is known as:
(a) Sample Space    (b) Sampling Frame    (c) Sampling Design    (d) Census
Correct Answer: (b) Sampling Frame

Part C: Fill in the Blanks

1. An estimator is said to be ___________ if its expected value is equal to the population parameter.
Answer: Unbiased
2. Sampling error can be reduced by ___________ the sample size.
Answer: Increasing

Part D: True or False

1. Census survey is more economical (cheaper) than sample survey. [True / False]
Answer: False
Explanation: Sample surveys are much cheaper as they study fewer units.
2. Probability sampling eliminates personal bias. [True / False]
Answer: True
