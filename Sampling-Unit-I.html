1.1 Introduction: The Science of Data and Sampling

Statistics is fundamentally the science of data. Data consists of numerical values containing critical information about a phenomenon under study. We use statistical tools on these datasets to draw statistical inferences. These inferences are crucial for decision-making across almost every sector of human activity.

For instance, governments rely on statistical data for policy formulation and welfare schemes. Marketing firms conduct consumer surveys to enhance their products and services. To acquire this data, organizations conduct what we call Sample Surveys. Around the globe, esteemed agencies like the National Sample Survey Organization (NSSO) in India, Statistics Canada, the World Health Organization (WHO), and the Food and Agricultural Organization (FAO) regularly undertake massive sample surveys to understand population trends, health crises, and agricultural outputs.

The Role of Sampling Theory

Sampling theory provides the mathematical tools and precise techniques for data collection. It ensures that the process is objective and that the collected data accurately reflects the true nature of the population, keeping the underlying objectives of the study firmly in focus.

Fundamentally, there are two contrasting ways to obtain information about a population:

  1. Complete Enumeration (Census): Collecting data from every single unit in the entire population.
  2. Sample Surveys: Collecting information from merely a fraction of the total population.

While a census attempts to cover everyone, sample surveys focus on a subset. Some surveys are conducted on a routine, continuous basis, such as economic indicators or agricultural acreage estimations. Others are ad-hoc or need-based, such as assessing consumer satisfaction immediately following the inauguration of a new shopping mall. Both methods serve distinct purposes depending on the availability of resources and the required precision.

1.2 Exhaustive Terminology & Fundamental Concepts

To master sampling techniques, one must first become perfectly fluent in the vocabulary of statistics. Clear definitions prevent ambiguity during survey planning and mathematical derivations.

1.2.1 Sampling Unit

An element, or a group of elements, on which observations can be taken is called a sampling unit. The definition of a sampling unit is strictly dictated by the objective of the survey.

Examples of Sampling Units

If the objective is to determine the total income of households in a city, the sampling unit is the household. Conversely, if the objective is to determine the income of a specific demographic, the sampling unit is the individual person.

In health surveys studying blood sugar levels, the sampling unit is the person from whom blood samples and health history are obtained.

1.2.2 Population (The Universe)

The Population is the aggregate collection of all sampling units in a specified region at a particular point in time or over a specific period. The total number of sampling units in the population is the population size, traditionally denoted by the capital letter N.

Populations can be classified based on their size:

  • Finite Population: A population with a countable number of units. E.g., the number of patients registered in a specific hospital during 2025, or the total number of students enrolled in M.S. University.
  • Infinite Population: A population with an uncountable or hypothetically limitless number of units. E.g., the number of stars in the observable universe, or the outcomes of tossing a coin indefinitely.

1.2.3 Sample and Sample Space

A Sample is a subset of one or more sampling units selected from the population according to a clearly defined procedure. It consists only of a portion of the population units. The number of units in the sample is denoted by n.

The Sample Space refers to the totality of all possible sample points or outcomes consistent with the chosen method of sampling. For instance, when tossing a single coin, the sample space is \( S = \{Head, Tail\} \). Any subset of this sample space is formally termed an Event.

1.2.4 Parameters vs. Statistics

It is vital to distinguish between values that describe the population and values that describe the sample.

Parameter

Any numerical constant computed from the entire population is called a parameter. Parameters are generally unknown and are the targets of our estimation. They are conventionally denoted by Greek letters (e.g., Population Mean \(\mu\), Population Variance \(\sigma^2\)).

Statistic

Any mathematical function of the sample values—which does not contain any unknown population parameters—is called a statistic. Statistics are calculated from the data we collect and are used to estimate the unknown parameters. They are conventionally denoted by Roman letters (e.g., Sample Mean \(\bar{x}\) or \(\bar{y}\), Sample Variance \(s^2\)).

1.2.5 Estimator, Estimate, and Unbiasedness

An Estimator is a specific mathematical rule or formula applied to a sample to guess the value of a population parameter. Since it depends on the random sample selected, an estimator is a random variable and has a probability distribution. The specific numerical value obtained by applying this rule to a particular sample is called an Estimate.

An estimator \(t\) is said to be an Unbiased Estimator for the parameter \(\theta\) if:
\(E(t) = \theta\)

Here, \(E(t)\) represents the expected value (long-run average) of the estimator across all possible samples. If \(E(t) \neq \theta\), the estimator is biased. The amount of bias is defined as \( Bias = B(t) = E(t) - \theta \).

1.2.6 Standard Error (S.E.) and Mean Square Error (MSE)

The positive square root of the variance of an estimator's sampling distribution is called the Standard Error of the estimator. It measures the average dispersion of the estimates around their expected value.

The Mean Square Error (MSE) is a more comprehensive measure of an estimator's accuracy, as it incorporates both the variance and the bias. The MSE of an estimator \(t\) with respect to parameter \(\theta\) is the expected value of the squared difference between them:

\(MSE(t) = E[(t - \theta)^2]\)
It can be proven that: \(MSE(t) = Variance(t) + [Bias(t)]^2\)

1.2.7 Sampling Frame and Representative Sample

The Sampling Frame is the practical tool used to access the population. It comprises the actual list, map, or directory of all the units in the population to be surveyed, complete with identification particulars. Examples include a university's student roll list, a city's telephone directory, or an automobile registration database.

A Representative Sample is one in which all the salient features, diversities, and proportions of the population are accurately reflected. For example, if a population consists of 30% males and 70% females, a truly representative sample must exhibit nearly the identical gender ratio.

1.3 The Absolute Need for Sampling

One might intuitively believe that to get accurate information, we must examine every single unit in the population. However, sampling methods have been extensively and successfully used for a vast diversity of situations. Complete enumeration is frequently impossible or highly impractical due to several compelling reasons.

1.3.1 Severe Resource Constraints

Conducting a full census demands astronomical amounts of money, highly trained personnel, and specialized equipment. In most practical research scenarios—whether in a university department or a corporate marketing division—resources are strictly limited. Sampling allows us to achieve our objectives within realistic budget and manpower constraints by focusing on a fraction of the population.

1.3.2 Destructive Experimentation

In industrial quality control and agricultural research, testing often destroys the unit being tested. This is known as destructive testing.

Examples of Destructive Testing

  • Finding out the germination percentage of a batch of seeds (the seeds are planted and consumed).
  • Evaluating the lethality of a new insecticide on pests.
  • Testing the breaking tension of steel ropes or chalks.
  • Checking the lifespan of an electric tube or bulb.
  • Testing the quality of chemical salts or explosives.

If we attempted complete enumeration in these scenarios, we would destroy the entire stock we are trying to evaluate, leaving nothing to sell or use! Here, sampling is not merely an option; it is the only method available.

1.3.3 Infinite or Hypothetical Populations

When a population is hypothetically infinite, a complete count is logically impossible. For example, if we are tossing a coin to determine its bias, the process can continue indefinitely. The population of "all possible coin tosses" is infinite. Similarly, estimating the number of leaves in a dense jungle or stars in the galaxy necessitates sampling. Sampling is the only scientific method for estimating parameters of an infinite universe.

1.3.4 The Factor of Time and Urgency

Data is often highly perishable; its utility degrades rapidly if not collected and analyzed within a strict time limit. A census survey takes an immense amount of time to conduct, verify, and tabulate. If the government needs immediate data on crop damage following a sudden flood to distribute relief funds, a census would take months, rendering the relief useless. Sampling provides rapid, actionable results when time is of the essence.

1.3.5 Greater Accuracy and Quality Control

This point often surprises beginners. A sample can yield more accurate results than a complete census. In a census, the massive volume of data collection requires hiring thousands of temporary, often underqualified enumerators. This leads to massive non-sampling errors (recording errors, fake interviews, measurement mistakes). In a sample survey, we can afford to hire a small group of highly qualified, expertly trained personnel. The intense supervision and use of sophisticated measuring instruments possible in a small sample survey drastically reduce non-sampling errors, often making the sample's estimate more accurate than the flawed census count.

1.4 Census vs. Sample Survey: A Critical Comparison

We must formally differentiate between complete enumeration and sample enumeration.

Census Survey: The total count of all units of the population for a certain characteristic. An example is the decennial population census conducted by the Government of India.

Sample Survey: When only a mathematically selected part of the population is examined, and conclusions are drawn about the whole.

Limitations of Census compared to Sampling

  • Less Time Requirement: There is considerable saving in time and labor since only a fraction of the population is examined in a sample survey. The results can be processed and analyzed much faster.
  • Reduced Cost: Sampling results in massive reductions in cost. The total expense expected for a sample survey is generally a small fraction of the cost of a complete census.
  • Greater Accuracy of Results: As discussed, the deployment of highly trained staff, better supervision, and sophisticated equipment on a small sample yields highly reliable data with minimal observational errors.
  • Greater Scope: Because sampling saves time and money, a survey can be highly intensive. A researcher can afford to ask 50 detailed questions to 1,000 people (sample) rather than asking just 2 basic questions to 1,000,000 people (census). Detailed, intensive inquiries are only possible through sampling.
  • Error Estimation: In probability sampling, it is mathematically possible to determine the extent of sampling errors and establish confidence limits, providing a measurable degree of reliability.

1.5 The 11 Principal Steps in a Sample Survey

Conducting a large-scale sample survey is a massive administrative and scientific undertaking. It requires meticulous planning. The sponsors of the survey must navigate the following eleven sequential steps.

  1. Objectives of the Survey: The very first step is to define clear, concrete, and unambiguous objectives. The statistician must be well-versed in the exact issues to be addressed. Often in complex surveys, initial objectives are forgotten, leading to the collection of irrelevant data. Objectives must align with available resources.
  2. Defining the Population to be Sampled: The aggregate of objects must be defined rigorously. If studying "farmers," one must define whether it means farm owners, tenant farmers, or agricultural laborers. The geographical boundaries and time frame must be specified.
  3. The Frame and Sampling Units: The population must be divided into distinct, non-overlapping sampling units that cover the entire population. A physical map or list (the frame) must be constructed or procured. A faulty frame leads to severe non-response and coverage errors.
  4. Data to be Collected: Decide precisely what variables are relevant. Researchers must resist the temptation to collect too much data "just in case," as excessively long surveys lead to respondent fatigue and lower data quality.
  5. The Questionnaire or Schedule: Construction of the inquiry tool requires deep skill. Questions must be unambiguous, courteous, non-offending, and to the point. Detailed instructions for filling out the forms must be prepared for the enumerators.
  6. Method of Collecting Information: Choose between methods like the Interview Method (investigator visits personally) or Mailed Questionnaire Method (sent via post/email). Other methods include physical observation or web-based inquiries.
  7. Handling Non-Respondents: Data can rarely be collected for all sampled units. People may refuse to answer or be away from home. This incompleteness can bias the results. Specific protocols must be planned to handle callbacks and non-response bias.
  8. Selection of Proper Sampling Design: Determine the sample size (\(n\)) and the mathematical procedure for selection (e.g., Simple Random, Stratified). The chosen design must guarantee good estimates while minimizing uncertainty and cost.
  9. Organization of Field Work (Pre-test & Training): It is highly advised to conduct a small-scale "Pre-test" or pilot survey to identify flaws in the questionnaire. Field workers must be intensely trained in locating units, taking measurements, and handling uncooperative respondents. Supervisory staff must be appointed for quality checks.
  10. Summary and Analysis of the Data: This involves several sub-stages:
    • Scrutiny and Editing: Initial quality checks to delete erroneous or impossible data.
    • Tabulation: Organizing data into machine-readable formats or databases.
    • Statistical Analysis: Applying appropriate mathematical formulas to derive estimates and margins of error.
    • Reporting and Conclusions: Writing a detailed report incorporating all stages of the survey and interpreting the statistical findings for the stakeholders.
  11. Information Gained for Future Surveys: A completed survey acts as a guide. Information regarding means, variances, and operational challenges should be recorded. This helps design more efficient surveys in the future and prevents the repetition of logistical mistakes.

1.6 Fundamental Principles of Sampling Surveys

The entire mathematical edifice of sampling is built upon three foundational principles. Understanding these is essential for any statistician.

1.6.1 Principle of Statistical Regularity

This principle traces its origins to the mathematical theory of probability, specifically the Law of Large Numbers. It states that a moderately large number of items chosen at random from a large group are almost sure, on average, to possess the characteristics of the large group.

It emphasizes the critical importance of random selection. If units are selected in a way that gives every unit an equal (or known) chance of selection, the sample will naturally mimic the population. It guarantees that the sample is representative without requiring the investigator to hand-pick units.

1.6.2 Principle of Validity

By validity of a sample design, we mean that the design must enable us to obtain valid tests and estimates about the population parameters. Crucially, a valid design allows us to calculate the objective estimate of the sampling error from the sample data itself. Only probability sampling techniques satisfy this principle of mathematical validity.

1.6.3 Principle of Optimization

This principle dictates that a sampling design must be practically efficient. The objective is to obtain optimum results considering the resources at our disposal. The efficiency of a design is usually measured by the reciprocal of its sampling variance, while the cost is measured in total expenses (money and man-hours). Optimization means:

  • (i) Achieving a specified, required level of efficiency at the minimum cost.
  • (ii) Obtaining the maximum possible efficiency (minimum variance) for a given, fixed budget.

1.7 Taxonomy & Types of Sampling Methods

The technique of selecting the sample is of fundamental importance. Sampling procedures are broadly classified under three main heads.

1.7.1 Subjective (Purposive or Judgment) Sampling

In this type, the sample is selected with a definite purpose in view, and the choice of sampling units depends entirely on the discretion, judgment, and expertise of the investigator. There is no role of chance or probability.

Drawbacks: This method suffers heavily from favoritism, nepotism, and the personal prejudices of the investigator. It seldom provides a truly representative sample. Furthermore, because probability laws are not used, there is no mathematical way to calculate the margin of error or confidence intervals. Therefore, it is rarely recommended for rigorous statistical inference, though it may be useful for preliminary pilot studies conducted by highly experienced experts.

1.7.2 Probability Sampling

This is the scientific method of selecting samples. It operates according to the laws of chance. In probability sampling, each unit in the population has a definite, pre-assigned mathematical probability of being selected. This eliminates personal bias completely.

Probability sampling comes in several variations:

  • Where each unit has an equal chance of being selected (e.g., Simple Random Sampling).
  • Where sampling units have different probabilities of selection (e.g., Varying Probability Sampling).
  • Where the probability of selection is proportional to the size of the unit (Probability Proportional to Size - PPS).

1.7.3 Mixed Sampling

If the samples are selected partly according to some laws of chance and partly according to a fixed, purposive rule, the technique is known as mixed sampling. Many complex, multi-stage survey designs utilized by governments incorporate elements of both to balance scientific rigor with administrative feasibility.

Under the broad umbrella of Probability and Mixed sampling, several specific designs exist, which will be covered in subsequent units:

  • i) Simple random sampling
  • ii) Stratified random sampling
  • iii) Systematic sampling
  • iv) Cluster sampling
  • v) Multi-stage sampling
  • vi) Quota sampling

1.8 Tools of Data Collection: Questionnaire vs. Schedule

Once the sample is identified, primary data must be collected. The two most prominent tools for this are the Questionnaire and the Schedule.

1.8.1 Questionnaire Method

A questionnaire consists of a printed or electronic list of questions relating to the inquiry, with blank spaces provided for answers. This is sent directly to the respondents (via mail, email, or web link), who are expected to read, fill out, and return it. It is usually accompanied by a polite covering letter requesting cooperation.

Merits:
  • Highly economical; saves money, labor, and time.
  • Can be widely used when the geographical area of investigation is very large.
  • Errors caused by the investigator's bias are eliminated since the respondent answers directly.
Demerits:
  • No direct connection between investigator and respondent, leading to doubts about accuracy.
  • Only suitable for literate populations.
  • High rates of non-response or extreme delays in returning the forms.
  • Misunderstood questions lead to false conclusions.

1.8.2 Schedule Method

The schedule is the most widely used method for collecting primary data in developing nations. A standard questionnaire is designed, but instead of sending it to the respondent, specially trained enumerators (investigators) visit the respondents physically. The enumerator asks the questions verbally, clarifies any doubts, and records the answers in the schedule themselves.

Merits:
  • Yields highly reliable and accurate results because enumerators are educated and trained.
  • Very useful in extensive, complex inquiries.
  • Can be utilized even if the respondent is entirely illiterate.
  • Significantly lower rates of non-response.
Demerits:
  • Extremely expensive due to enumerator salaries and travel costs.
  • Highly time-consuming.
  • The personal bias or behavior of the enumerator may influence the respondent and lead to false conclusions.
  • Quality relies entirely on the personal qualities of the enumerators.

1.9 Practice Exercises: Subjective Questions

Test your deep conceptual understanding. Formulate your answers before checking the detailed solutions.

Q1. Define 'Population' and 'Sample' in the context of sampling theory. Provide a real-world agricultural example to illustrate your definitions.
Solution:
Population: The entire set of all possible observations or units under study in a specified region and timeframe. It is denoted by 'N'.
Sample: A selected subset or fraction of the population from which data is actually collected. It is denoted by 'n'.
Example: If the objective is to estimate the average yield of wheat in the state of Haryana in 2026, the Population consists of every single wheat-cultivating farm in Haryana. Because it is impossible to visit every farm, researchers might select 500 farms using a random procedure. These 500 farms constitute the Sample.
Q2. Distinguish rigorously between a 'Parameter' and a 'Statistic'. Include the conventional notations used for both.
Solution:
A Parameter is a numerical constant that summarizes a characteristic of the entire population. It is usually unknown and is the target of estimation. Parameters are conventionally denoted by Greek letters. Examples include the population mean (\(\mu\)) and population variance (\(\sigma^2\)).

A Statistic is a numerical value calculated exclusively from the sample data. It is a mathematical function of the sample values and does not contain any unknown parameters. Since it varies depending on which sample is drawn, it is a random variable. Statistics are conventionally denoted by Roman letters. Examples include the sample mean (\(\bar{x}\) or \(\bar{y}\)) and sample variance (\(s^2\)).
Q3. Explain the concept of an 'Unbiased Estimator'. Write the mathematical condition that defines it.
Solution:
An estimator is a mathematical rule applied to sample data to estimate a population parameter. Because different samples yield different estimates, the estimator has a probability distribution.
An estimator \(t\) is considered an Unbiased Estimator for a parameter \(\theta\) if the expected value (the theoretical long-run average over all possible samples) of the estimator is exactly equal to the true population parameter.
Mathematical Condition: \(E(t) = \theta\) or \(E(t) - \theta = 0\).
If this condition is not met, the estimator has a bias, defined as \(B(t) = E(t) - \theta\).
Q4. What is a 'Sampling Frame'? Why is the accuracy of the sampling frame considered critical to the validity of a survey?
Solution:
A Sampling Frame is the physical or electronic list, map, or directory that identifies every single sampling unit within the population. It is the tool used to actually draw the sample.
Criticality: The frame must be accurate, exhaustive, and up-to-date. If the frame is inaccurate, the survey suffers from severe Frame Bias. For example, if a list of voters from 2015 is used for a 2026 election survey, newly eligible voters are completely omitted (under-coverage), and deceased individuals might still be listed (over-coverage). Consequently, the sample drawn will be fundamentally unrepresentative of the current population, rendering advanced statistical analysis useless.
Q5. Discuss the necessity of sampling in scenarios involving 'Destructive Testing'. Give three industrial examples.
Solution:
In certain experiments or quality control processes, determining the quality or characteristic of an item results in the destruction of that item. If a complete enumeration (census) were attempted, the entire population would be destroyed, defeating the purpose of production. Therefore, taking a small sample is the only scientifically viable method to assess quality while preserving the bulk of the population for use.
Examples:
1. Testing the bursting pressure of safety valves.
2. Testing the lifespan (hours to burnout) of electric lightbulbs.
3. Evaluating the explosive yield of a batch of ammunition.
Q6. Elaborate on the 'Accuracy Paradox': How can a sample survey theoretically yield more accurate results than a complete census?
Solution:
Total survey error consists of two parts: Sampling Error and Non-Sampling Error. A census has zero sampling error. However, conducting a census requires a massive administrative effort, utilizing thousands of temporary, under-trained enumerators. This leads to extremely high Non-Sampling Errors (measurement errors, data entry mistakes, incomplete coverage, respondent fatigue).
In contrast, a sample survey involves a much smaller scale of operations. Organizations can afford to hire elite, highly trained personnel, use superior measuring instruments, and apply intense supervision. This drastically reduces non-sampling errors. Often, the reduction in non-sampling error is so significant that it more than compensates for the introduction of sampling error, making the total error of the sample survey smaller (and thus more accurate) than the census.
Q7. State and mathematically justify the 'Principle of Statistical Regularity'. Which fundamental law of probability is it based upon?
Solution:
The Principle of Statistical Regularity states that a moderately large number of items chosen at random from a large population are almost certain, on average, to possess the characteristics of the large population. It assures us that random samples are representative.
This principle is directly based on the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) in probability theory. Mathematically, as the sample size \(n\) increases, the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) converges in probability to the population mean \(\mu\). The random variations inherent in individual units cancel each other out over a large, randomly selected aggregate.
Q8. Differentiate between 'Subjective (Judgment) Sampling' and 'Probability Sampling'. Why is probability sampling essential for statistical inference?
Solution:
Subjective Sampling: Units are selected based purely on the personal discretion, experience, or judgment of the investigator. There is no mathematical rule governing selection.
Probability Sampling: A scientific method where every unit in the population has a definite, pre-assigned, and known probability of being selected (governed by laws of chance).
Necessity for Inference: Statistical inference requires estimating the margin of error (Standard Error) and constructing confidence intervals. This requires knowing the sampling distribution of the estimator. Because subjective sampling lacks a mathematical foundation, calculating sampling error is impossible. Only probability sampling satisfies the Principle of Validity, allowing objective measurement of reliability.
Q9. Briefly outline the role of the 'Pre-test' or pilot survey in the organization of field work.
Solution:
A Pre-test (or pilot survey) is a small-scale replica of the main survey conducted before the actual large-scale field work begins. Its roles include:
1. Testing the questionnaire to identify ambiguous, offensive, or difficult-to-understand questions.
2. Estimating the time required per interview, which helps in budgeting and scheduling.
3. Identifying logistical or administrative problems the field staff might face.
4. Providing an initial estimate of the population variance, which is crucial for determining the final required sample size \(n\) for the main survey.
Q10. Compare the 'Questionnaire' and 'Schedule' methods of data collection, highlighting two critical merits and demerits of each.
Solution:
Questionnaire (Sent to respondent to fill):
Merits: 1. Highly economical (saves travel/enumerator costs). 2. Eliminates interviewer bias, giving respondents privacy to answer sensitive questions.
Demerits: 1. Extremely high non-response rate. 2. Useless if the target population is illiterate.

Schedule (Enumerator asks and fills):
Merits: 1. Yields highly accurate data as enumerators can explain complex questions. 2. Solves the illiteracy problem and ensures a high response rate.
Demerits: 1. Very expensive and time-consuming. 2. Susceptible to investigator bias (the enumerator's tone or demeanor might influence the answers).
Q11-20. Analytical questions covering finite/infinite classification, event spaces, Mean Square Error vs Variance, limitations of sampling, and cost optimization principles.
Analytical Summary:
- MSE vs Variance: \(MSE = Variance + Bias^2\). An estimator with a tiny bias but vastly smaller variance might be preferred over an unbiased estimator with massive variance, according to the Principle of Optimization.
- Limitations: Sampling fails if 100% legal compliance is required (e.g., tax audits, voter lists) or if the population is extremely small and heterogeneous. Furthermore, complex sampling designs require expert statisticians; improper application yields catastrophic, misleading results.

(a) \(Variance(t) = \theta\)    (b) \(E(t) = 0\)    (c) \(E(t) = \theta\)    (d) \(E(t) - \theta > 0\)

Q4. The positive square root of the variance of an estimator's sampling distribution is called its:
(a) Mean Square Error    (b) Bias    (c) Standard Error    (d) Probable Error
Correct Answer: (c) Standard Error

Correct Answer: (d) Probability Sampling

Q9. In the formula \(MSE(t) = Variance(t) + [B(t)]^2\), what does \(B(t)\) stand for?
(a) Base    (b) Beta    (c) Bias    (d) Boundary
Correct Answer: (c) Bias
Explanation: Mean Square Error accounts for both the variance of the estimator and the square of its bias.
Q10. A small-scale replica survey conducted before the main survey to test the questionnaire is called a:
(a) Post-test    (b) Pilot Survey (Pre-test)    (c) Census    (d) Subjective Sample

2. The parameter is a constant value for a given population, whereas a statistic varies from sample to sample. [True / False]
Answer: True
Reason: Parameters (\(\mu\)) are fixed. Statistics (\(\bar{x}\)) are random variables dependent on the specific units drawn.
3. Purposive sampling allows the statistician to calculate the standard error of the estimate. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: Purposive sampling lacks a probabilistic foundation, meaning the sampling distribution is unknown, making error calculation impossible.
4. The mailed questionnaire method is generally more expensive than the schedule (interview) method. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: The schedule method requires paying enumerator salaries and travel expenses, making it vastly more expensive than mailing forms.
5. An estimator is unbiased if its expected value over infinite repeated samples equals the true population parameter. [True / False]
Answer: True
Reason: By definition, \(E(t) = \theta\) implies unbiasedness.
6. A population containing the number of leaves on a large banyan tree is technically considered finite, though it is extremely large. [True / False]
Answer: True
Reason: While incredibly difficult to count, the number of leaves is theoretically countable and not infinite.
7. The Principle of Statistical Regularity contradicts the Law of Large Numbers. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: The principle is directly derived from and relies upon the Law of Large Numbers.
8. A sampling frame is completely unnecessary if the investigator intends to use Simple Random Sampling. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: To assign an equal probability to every unit, one must first be able to identify and list every unit. A frame is strictly necessary.
9. Standard Error is defined as the square of the sampling variance. [True / False]
Answer: False
Reason: Standard Error is the positive square root of the sampling variance, not the square.
10. Conducting a pilot survey (pre-test) helps in estimating the population variance, which is required to determine the final sample size. [True / False]
Answer: True
Reason: Without prior knowledge of the population variance (\(\sigma^2\)), one cannot use sample size formulas. A pilot survey provides this initial estimate.
