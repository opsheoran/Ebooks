1.1 Introduction to Sampling

Hello students! Welcome to the study of Sampling Techniques. Before we jump into formulas, let us understand what statistics is in simple English. Statistics is the science of data. Data means numbers that give us information about the world. We use statistical tools to study these numbers and make smart guesses (inferences) about the real world.

Think about how the government makes policies, or how companies decide what products to sell. They cannot ask every single person in the country. Instead, they do a Sample Survey. A sample survey means they only ask a small group of people, but they choose this small group very scientifically. Famous agencies like the National Sample Survey Organization (NSSO) in India, the World Health Organization (WHO), and the Food and Agricultural Organization (FAO) use these surveys every day to collect important data.

Simple Logic of Sampling

Imagine your mother is cooking rice. To check if the rice is fully cooked, she does not eat the whole pot of rice! She just takes out one spoon of rice, checks it, and then knows the condition of the whole pot. This is exactly what sampling is. The whole pot is the Population, and the one spoon is the Sample.

In Statistics, there are two ways to get information:

  1. Complete Enumeration or Census: This means counting or asking every single unit in the whole group. For example, India's population census happens every 10 years.
  2. Sample Surveys: This means collecting data from only a small fraction of the total group. This is much faster and cheaper.

1.2 Basic Concepts & Terminology

To understand sampling, you must know the basic words we use in statistics. Let us go through them one by one very clearly.

1. Population (or Universe)

The population is the complete set of all the things, people, or items you want to study. It is denoted by the capital letter N.

Example: If you want to find the average income of all farmers in Punjab, then all the farmers in Punjab make up your population. If there are 1 lakh farmers, then N = 1,00,000.

Populations can be of two types:

  • Finite Population: A population where you can count the number of units. Example: The number of students in a university, or the number of trees in an orchard.
  • Infinite Population: A population where you cannot count the number of units because it is endless. Example: The number of stars in the sky, or the number of times you can toss a coin.

2. Sample

A sample is a small part or subset taken from the population to study it. It is denoted by the small letter n.

Example: Out of the 1 lakh farmers in Punjab, you only choose 500 farmers to interview. Those 500 farmers are your sample (n = 500).

3. Parameter

A Parameter is a fixed, unknown numerical value that belongs to the Population. We use Greek letters to show parameters.

Examples: Population Mean is denoted by \(\mu\) (mu). Population Variance is denoted by \(\sigma^2\) (sigma square).

4. Statistic

A Statistic is a numerical value calculated only from the Sample. We use simple Roman letters for statistics. We use statistics to guess (estimate) the parameter.

Examples: Sample Mean is denoted by \(\bar{x}\) (x-bar). Sample Variance is denoted by \(s^2\).

Easy way to remember: Population goes with Parameter. Sample goes with Statistic.

5. Estimator and Unbiased Estimator

An Estimator is the mathematical formula or rule you use on the sample data to guess the population parameter. Because different samples will give different answers, the estimator can change.

An estimator (let's call it \(t\)) is called an Unbiased Estimator for a parameter (\(\theta\)) if its expected value (average of all possible samples) is exactly equal to the parameter. Mathematically: \(E(t) = \theta\).

If it does not match, we say there is a Bias. Bias is calculated as: \(Bias = E(t) - \theta\).

6. Standard Error

If you take many samples, the answers will jump around a bit. The positive square root of the variance of your estimator is called the Standard Error. It shows how much your sample guess might be wrong by mistake.

7. Sampling Unit and Sampling Frame

Sampling Unit: This is the smallest single piece you can pick for your sample. If you are checking the health of families, the "Family" is the sampling unit. If you are checking student marks, the "Individual Student" is the unit.

Sampling Frame: Before you can pick a sample, you need a full list or a map of all the sampling units in the population. This physical list is called the sampling frame. Example: The attendance register of a class is the sampling frame for the students.

8. Sample Space and Event

Sample Space: The total collection of all possible outcomes of an experiment. Example: When tossing a coin, the sample space \(S = \{Head, Tail\}\).

Event: A specific subset of the sample space. Example: Getting a "Head" is an event.

1.3 Why do we Need Sampling?

Why don't we just ask everyone? Why do we use samples? There are very strong practical and scientific reasons why we use sampling instead of complete enumeration (census).

1. Lack of Resources (Money and Manpower)

Asking every single person in a large country or state requires a huge amount of money, thousands of workers, and lots of equipment. In real life, we have a limited budget. Sampling gives us very good results using much less money and fewer workers.

2. Destructive Experimentation

This is a very important point! Sometimes, when you test something, you destroy it. For example:

  • To find the germination percentage of seeds, you have to plant them. Once planted, you cannot sell them.
  • To check the breaking strength of chalk or steel pipes, you have to break them.
  • To check the life of an electric bulb, you have to keep it on until it fuses and burns out.
  • To test the quality of milk or blood, you have to extract and use chemicals on it.

If we tested every single bulb a factory produced (Census), all the bulbs would be fused, and the factory would have nothing to sell! So, in destructive testing, sampling is the only possible way.

3. Infinite Populations

If the population is too large or infinite (like trees in a vast jungle, or fish in the sea), there is no way to count them all. Sampling is the only scientific method to study an infinite population.

4. Time Limit (Urgent Information)

If a disease is spreading, the government needs data immediately to send medicines. A census takes years to plan and complete. A sample survey can be done in a few days or weeks. When information is urgently required, sampling is the best choice.

5. Greater Accuracy

This sounds surprising, but a sample is often more accurate than a census! Why? Because in a census, the data is so huge that the workers get tired and make many mistakes in writing and counting. In a sample, because the group is small, we can hire highly trained experts, give them better tools, and supervise them closely. This reduces human errors drastically, making the sample very accurate.

1.4 Census vs. Sample Survey

Let us clearly compare Complete Enumeration (Census) and Sample Survey so you can see the difference easily.

Feature Census (Complete Count) Sample Survey
Definition Every single unit in the population is studied. Only a selected part of the population is studied.
Time Taken Takes a very long time (months or years). Saves considerable time; very quick.
Cost Highly expensive. Needs a massive budget. Highly economical. Saves money.
Destructive Testing Impossible (it would destroy everything). The only possible method.
Nature of Error No sampling error, but high human error (non-sampling error). Has sampling error, but human errors are kept very low.
Scope of Study Can only ask a few simple questions. Can ask detailed and intensive questions.

Limitation of Sampling

Sampling is not always perfect. If you need 100% accurate details about every single individual (like making a voter ID card list or a tax collection list), you cannot use a sample. In such cases, you must do a complete census. Also, if the sample is planned poorly by someone who doesn't know statistics, the results will be totally misleading.

1.5 The 11 Principal Steps in a Sample Survey

Planning a survey is a big job. You cannot just go out and start asking questions. A statistician must follow these 11 clear steps to make sure the survey is successful.

  1. Objectives of the Survey: The very first step is to be completely clear about what you want to achieve. If the objective is not clear, you will collect useless data.
  2. Defining the Population: You must clearly define who you are studying. If you say "farmers," it is not clear. You should say "Farmers growing wheat in Haryana in the year 2025."
  3. The Frame and Sampling Units: Divide the population into clear units (like families or villages). Then, get a physical list or a map of all these units. This list is called the Sampling Frame.
  4. Data to be Collected: Decide exactly what questions need to be answered. Do not ask unnecessary questions, as it wastes time and frustrates the people answering.
  5. The Questionnaire or Schedule: Prepare the form. Questions should be polite, clear, and easy to understand. Also, write instructions for the people who will fill the forms.
  6. Method of Collecting Information: Will you use the Interview method (going house to house) or the Mailed Questionnaire method (sending it by post/email)? Decide this here.
  7. Handling Non-Respondents: Sometimes people are not at home or refuse to answer. This changes the results. You must have a plan for what to do in such cases.
  8. Selection of Proper Sampling Design: Decide how big the sample should be (n) and the mathematical rule to pick them (like Simple Random Sampling).
  9. Organization of Field Work (Pre-test): Before doing the big survey, train your workers properly. It is best to do a small test survey (Pre-test) to see if there are any problems with the questionnaire or the field plan.
  10. Summary and Analysis of the Data: Once data comes in, check it for mistakes (editing), organize it into tables (tabulation), and use statistical formulas to get the final answers (analysis). Finally, write a clear report.
  11. Information Gained for Future Surveys: Every survey teaches us something. Keep notes of the problems faced so that the next survey can be planned even better.

1.6 Principles of Sampling Survey

Why does sampling actually work? It works because of three powerful mathematical principles.

1. Principle of Statistical Regularity

This principle comes from the mathematical theory of probability. It states that if you take a moderately large number of items chosen at random from a large group, they are almost sure (on average) to have the same characteristics as the large group.

Basically, random selection ensures that the sample becomes a true mini-version of the population. It removes human bias.

2. Principle of Validity

A sampling design is considered "valid" only if it allows us to calculate the margin of error (sampling error) from the sample data itself. Only probability sampling techniques follow this principle.

3. Principle of Optimization

This principle is about getting the best results for your effort. It means you should either:

  • Achieve a given level of efficiency (accuracy) at the minimum cost.
  • Obtain the maximum possible efficiency for a given fixed budget.

1.7 Types of Sampling

How do we select the units? The method of selection is very important. We broadly classify sampling into three types.

1. Subjective (or Purposive or Judgment) Sampling

In this type, the sample is selected simply because the investigator "feels" it is the right choice. The selection depends entirely on the personal judgment and discretion of the surveyor.

  • Drawback: It is highly affected by personal bias, favoritism, and prejudice. Also, because there is no probability involved, we cannot mathematically calculate the error. This method is usually avoided in scientific research, unless done by highly experienced experts for a small test.

2. Probability Sampling

This is the most scientific method. Here, samples are selected according to strict laws of chance. Every single unit in the population has a definite, known probability of being selected.

  • Every unit can have an equal chance of selection (Simple Random Sampling).
  • Units can have different chances of selection.
  • Probability can be proportional to the size of the unit.

3. Mixed Sampling

If the samples are selected partly by the laws of chance and partly by a fixed, purposive rule, it is called mixed sampling. This is used in very large, complex surveys.

1.8 Data Collection Tools: Questionnaire vs Schedule

When you have selected your sample, you need to collect the data. You can use either a Questionnaire or a Schedule.

1. Questionnaire

A questionnaire is a printed list of questions with blank spaces for answers. It is sent to the respondents (by post or email). The respondent reads it and fills it out themselves.

  • Merits: It is very cheap and economical. It saves time and labor. It is very useful when the survey area is very large (like the whole country). The investigator's personal bias is zero because they are not there.
  • Demerits: The investigator cannot clarify if the respondent does not understand a question. It is useless for illiterate people who cannot read or write. There is a huge delay in getting replies, and many people just throw it away (non-response).

2. Schedule

A schedule is also a list of questions, but here, the investigator goes personally to the respondent. The investigator asks the questions verbally and fills the answers on the paper themselves.

  • Merits: Very useful for extensive enquiries. Yields highly accurate and reliable results because the enumerator is educated and trained. Can be used perfectly well even if the respondent is illiterate. There is very little chance of non-response.
  • Demerits: It is highly expensive (you have to pay travel and salary to workers). It is very time-consuming. Sometimes, the personal bias of the investigator might change the respondent's answer. The quality of data depends heavily on the honesty of the enumerator.

Part A: Subjective Questions (20 Qs)

Q1. Define the terms 'Population' and 'Sample' in simple words with an example.
Solution: Population means the total collection of all the things or people we want to study. It is denoted by 'N'. A Sample is a small part or subset taken from this population to be studied. It is denoted by 'n'.
Example: If we want to check the marks of all students in a college (Population), we might just select 50 students (Sample) to get a good idea.
Q2. What is the difference between a Parameter and a Statistic?
Solution: A Parameter is a numerical value calculated from the whole Population. It is a fixed constant and denoted by Greek letters (like $\mu$ for mean). A Statistic is a numerical value calculated only from the Sample. It varies from sample to sample and is denoted by Roman letters (like $\bar{x}$ for mean).
Q3. Give two examples of Destructive Testing where sampling is absolutely necessary.
Solution: 1. Testing the life of electric bulbs: You have to keep them on until they fuse. If you test all (census), you have no bulbs left to sell.
2. Testing the breaking strength of chalk: You have to break the chalk to measure it. Thus, sampling is mandatory here.
Q4. What is a Sampling Frame? Why is it important?
Solution: A Sampling Frame is a complete list or a map that contains the names or identification of every single unit in the population. It is extremely important because without a proper list, we cannot use random sampling, and we might miss important people in the survey.
Q5. Explain the 'Principle of Statistical Regularity'.
Solution: This principle states that if we select a moderately large number of items completely at random from a very large group, the selected items will, on average, have the exact same characteristics as the large group. It relies on the Law of Large Numbers.
Q6. What makes an estimator an "Unbiased Estimator"? Write the formula.
Solution: An estimator (a formula used to guess a parameter) is called Unbiased if its expected value (average result over many samples) is exactly equal to the true population parameter. The formula is: $E(t) = \theta$.
Q7. What is Subjective or Judgment Sampling? Why is it bad?
Solution: Subjective sampling is when the investigator picks the sample based on his own personal choice or judgment, without any mathematical rule. It is bad because it introduces personal bias (favoritism) and there is no way to mathematically calculate how accurate the result is.
Q8. How is Probability Sampling different from Subjective Sampling?
Solution: In Probability Sampling, every unit in the population has a definite, pre-assigned mathematical chance of being selected. The selection is done by random methods, eliminating human bias completely, unlike Subjective sampling which relies on human feelings.
Q9. Mention two merits and two demerits of the Questionnaire method.
Solution: Merits: 1. Highly economical (saves money). 2. Can cover a very large geographic area easily.
Demerits: 1. Only useful for educated/literate people. 2. High chances of non-response (people ignoring it).
Q10. Why do we prefer a Schedule method in rural India?
Solution: In the Schedule method, the investigator goes physically to ask questions. This is highly preferred in rural areas because the investigator can explain the questions to illiterate respondents, clear their doubts, and ensure the form is completely filled.
Q11. What is meant by Finite and Infinite Population? Give an example of an infinite population.
Solution: A finite population has a countable number of items (like students in a class). An infinite population has an uncountable or limitless number of items. Example of infinite population: The number of stars in the sky or the number of times you can toss a coin.
Q12. What is Standard Error? What does it measure?
Solution: The positive square root of the variance of an estimator is called the Standard Error. It measures how much the sample estimates jump around or deviate from the true value due to sampling fluctuations.
Q13. How can a sample survey sometimes be more accurate than a complete census?
Solution: A census requires a massive amount of labor, which leads to huge human errors (non-sampling errors) like wrong data entry or poor supervision. A sample survey uses a small, highly trained team with strict supervision. The reduction in human errors makes the final result of the sample highly accurate.
Q14. What is a Pre-test in survey methodology? Why do we do it?
Solution: A pre-test is a small pilot survey done before the main survey. We do it to check if the questionnaire is easy to understand, to see how much time it takes, and to find out any practical field problems before spending a lot of money on the big survey.
Q15. State the 'Principle of Optimization' in sampling.
Solution: The Principle of Optimization says that a sampling design should be efficient. It means we should either get the maximum possible accuracy for a fixed budget, or achieve a required level of accuracy using the minimum possible money and time.
Q16. What is Mixed Sampling? When is it used?
Solution: Mixed sampling is a method where some units are selected by subjective judgment, and then further units are selected by probability laws. It is used in complex, multi-stage national surveys to balance scientific accuracy with administrative convenience.
Q17. Explain the term 'Non-respondent'. How does it affect a survey?
Solution: A non-respondent is a person in the sample who cannot be contacted or refuses to give an answer. It affects the survey by creating bias, because the people who answer might have very different views from the people who refuse to answer, making the data incomplete.
Q18. What do you mean by an 'Event' and 'Sample Space' in statistics?
Solution: Sample Space is the total collection of all possible outcomes of an experiment (like {Head, Tail} for a coin toss). An Event is simply a subset of this sample space (like getting a 'Head').
Q19. Give three important points to remember while designing a Questionnaire.
Solution: 1. Questions should be clear and unambiguous. 2. Questions should be polite and non-offending. 3. There should be clear instructions on how to fill out the blank spaces.
Q20. Summarize the major limitations of sampling.
Solution: Sampling cannot be used when information is legally required for every single unit (like a voter list). If the sample is selected poorly by untrained staff, the results will be completely misleading. It also requires advanced statistical knowledge to execute properly.

Part D: True or False (20 Qs)

1. A census survey is always faster than a sample survey.
False. A census takes much longer because every single unit must be studied.
2. Sampling is mandatory when testing the lifespan of an electric bulb.
True. This is destructive testing. If we do a census, all bulbs will be destroyed.
3. A Parameter is calculated from sample data.
False. A parameter is calculated from population data. A statistic is calculated from sample data.
4. Subjective sampling eliminates personal bias.
False. Subjective sampling is entirely based on personal bias and judgment. Probability sampling eliminates bias.
5. The Questionnaire method is very useful for illiterate respondents.
False. Since the respondent has to read and write the answers, it is useless for illiterate people. The Schedule method is used instead.
6. A sampling frame must not overlap or have duplicate entries.
True. Duplicate entries mean a unit has a double chance of being selected, which ruins the probability math.
7. An estimator is a fixed constant number.
False. An estimator is a formula or rule. It acts as a random variable because its value changes with each different sample.
8. The Principle of Statistical Regularity is based on the Law of Large Numbers.
True. It guarantees that a large random sample will represent the population properly.
9. Standard Error is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of a statistic.
True. It tells us how much our estimate fluctuates.
10. "All farmers in India" is an example of an infinite population.
False. It is a very large number, but it is finite (you can theoretically count them). Infinite means uncountable like stars.
11. A sample survey is generally much cheaper than a complete census.
True. You spend less money on labor, travel, and materials.
12. $E(t) = \theta$ means the estimator $t$ is biased.
False. It means the expected value equals the parameter, so the estimator is UNBIASED.
13. The Schedule method is highly expensive compared to the Questionnaire method.
True. You have to pay investigators to travel and conduct interviews face-to-face.
14. Probability sampling methods allow us to calculate the margin of error mathematically.
True. Because every unit has a known probability of selection, we can calculate the exact sampling error.
15. Mixed sampling uses only the laws of chance and zero human judgment.
False. Mixed sampling is a combination of both probability (chance) and subjective (judgment) methods.
16. The final step in a sample survey is defining the objectives.
False. Defining objectives is the FIRST step. The final step is reporting and saving lessons for future surveys.
17. A sample can sometimes be more accurate than a census.
True. A small sample allows for better supervision and expert staff, massively reducing human errors (non-sampling errors) compared to a huge census.
18. Bias is calculated as $B(t) = E(t) - \theta$.
True. It measures how far off the expected value of our formula is from the true target.
19. If a person selected for a survey is not at home, we should just pick the neighbor instead to save time.
False. This ruins the probability design. Specific rules for non-respondents (like call-backs) must be followed.
20. The principle of optimization is only concerned with minimizing the variance, regardless of the cost.
False. Optimization balances BOTH cost and accuracy. It seeks maximum accuracy for a fixed budget, or a set accuracy for minimum cost.
