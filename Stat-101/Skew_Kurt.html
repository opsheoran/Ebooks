Introduction to Skewness

Meaning of Skewness

Skewness means 'lack of symmetry'. We study skewness to get an idea about the shape of the curve that can be drawn with the given data. If in a distribution, Mean = Median = Mode, then that distribution is called a symmetrical distribution. If in a distribution, Mean ≠ Median ≠ Mode, then it is not a symmetrical distribution and is called a skewed distribution, and such a distribution can either be positively skewed or negatively skewed.

Understanding the shape of the distribution is very important in statistics because it provides us with valuable information about the pattern and behavior of the data. Skewness tells us on which side the data is concentrated and in which direction the outliers are.

Example of agricultural income in Haryana

A survey of the monthly income of farmers in a district of Haryana was conducted. If the income of the majority of farmers is low but the income of some large farmers is very high, then this distribution will be positively skewed. This means that Mean > Median > Mode will hold. For example, if out of 100 farmers, the income of 70 farmers is between ₹15,000-₹25,000, the income of 20 farmers is between ₹25,000-₹35,000, and the income of 10 farmers is more than ₹50,000, then this distribution will be right-skewed.

It is clear from the above diagrams that in a symmetrical distribution, the values of the mean, median, and mode coincide. The spread of frequencies is equal on both sides of the center point of the curve.

Positively Skewed Distribution

It is clear from the above diagrams that in a positively skewed distribution, the value of the mean is the maximum and the mode is the least, and the median is in between the two. In a positively skewed distribution, the frequencies are spread out over a greater range of values on the right-hand side as compared to the left-hand side.

Example: Household income in Haryana

A survey of the monthly income of households in a village in Haryana:

Income (₹) 10,000-20,000 20,000-30,000 30,000-40,000 40,000-60,000 60,000-100,000
Families 45 35 15 8 2

This distribution is positively skewed because the majority of families have a low income, but the income of some families is very high, which stretches the distribution to the right side.

Negatively Skewed Distribution

It is clear from the above diagrams that in a negatively skewed distribution, the value of the mode is the maximum and the mean is the least. The median is in between the two. In a negatively skewed distribution, the frequencies are spread out over a greater range of values on the left-hand side as compared to the right-hand side.

Example: Exam scores

If an exam is very easy and the majority of students get high marks, but a few students perform poorly:

Marks 0-20 20-40 40-60 60-80 80-100
Students 3 5 12 35 45

This distribution is negatively skewed because the majority of students have received high marks, but the low marks of some students create a tail on the left side of the distribution.

Measures of Skewness

The important measures of skewness are:

  • Karl-Pearson's coefficient of skewness
  • Bowley's coefficient of skewness

Karl-Pearson's Coefficient of Skewness

According to Karl-Pearson, the absolute measure of skewness = Mean - Mode. This measure is not suitable for a valid comparison of skewness in two or more distributions because the unit of measurement may be different for different series. To avoid this difficulty, the relative measure of skewness called Karl-Pearson's coefficient of skewness is used:

Formula for Karl-Pearson's Coefficient of Skewness

Karl-Pearson's Coefficient of Skewness = \(\frac{\text{Mean} - \text{Mode}}{\text{Standard Deviation}}\)

When the mode is ill-defined, the coefficient can be determined by the following formula:

Alternative Formula (when Mode is ill-defined)

Karl-Pearson's Coefficient of Skewness = \(\frac{3(\text{Mean} - \text{Median})}{\text{Standard Deviation}}\)

Example 1: Calculation of Karl-Pearson's Coefficient

Data of monthly income of farmers in a district of Haryana:

Income (in thousands of ₹) 10-15 15-20 20-25 25-30 30-35 35-40
Number of Farmers 25 35 40 30 15 5

Solution:

Mean = ₹22,500
Median = ₹21,800
Standard Deviation = ₹6,200

Karl-Pearson's Sk = \(\frac{3(22,500 - 21,800)}{6,200}\) = \(\frac{3 \times 700}{6,200}\) = \(\frac{2,100}{6,200}\) = 0.34

Since the coefficient is positive, the distribution is positively skewed.

Bowley's Coefficient of Skewness

In Karl-Pearson's method, the entire series is needed to measure skewness. Prof. Bowley suggested a formula based on the relative position of quartiles. In a symmetrical distribution, the quartiles are equidistant from the value of the median; that is,

Median - Q₁ = Q₃ - Median

But in a skewed distribution, the quartiles are not equidistant from the median. Therefore, Bowley suggested the following formula:

Formula for Bowley's Coefficient of Skewness

Bowley's Coefficient of Skewness (Sk) = \(\frac{Q_3 + Q_1 - 2\text{Median}}{Q_3 - Q_1}\)

Example 2: Calculation of Bowley's Coefficient

Distribution of marks of students of Haryana Agricultural University:

Marks 0-20 20-40 40-60 60-80 80-100
Students 5 15 30 35 15

Solution:

N = 100

$Q_1$ position = N/4 = 25th item = 43.33 marks
$Q_3$ position = 3N/4 = 75th item = 72.86 marks
Median position = N/2 = 50th item = 58 marks

Bowley's Sk = \(\frac{72.86 + 43.33 - 2(58)}{72.86 - 43.33}\) = \(\frac{116.19 - 116}{29.53}\) = \(\frac{0.19}{29.53}\) = 0.006

Since the coefficient is approximately zero, the distribution is approximately symmetrical.

Interpretation of Skewness

Interpretation of Skewness Values
  • Sk = 0: The distribution is perfectly symmetrical
  • Sk > 0: The distribution is positively skewed (right-tailed)
  • Sk < 0: The distribution is negatively skewed (left-tailed)
  • |Sk| < 0.5: Approximately symmetrical
  • 0.5 ≤ |Sk| < 1: Moderately skewed
  • |Sk| ≥ 1: Highly skewed

Kurtosis

Meaning of Kurtosis

The expression Kurtosis is used to describe the peakedness of a curve. The three measures - central tendency, dispersion, and skewness - describe the characteristics of frequency distributions. But these studies do not give us a clear picture of the characteristics of the distribution.

As far as the measurement of shape is concerned, we have two characteristics - skewness, which refers to the asymmetry of the series, and kurtosis, which measures the peakedness of the normal curve. All frequency curves expose different degrees of flatness or peakedness. This characteristic of a frequency curve is termed as kurtosis. The measure of kurtosis denotes the shape of the top of the frequency curve.

The measure of kurtosis tells us how much more peaked or more flat-topped the distribution is compared to the normal curve. The normal curve, which is symmetrical and bell-shaped, is designated as Mesokurtic. If the curve is relatively more narrow and peaked at the top, it is designated as Leptokurtic. If the frequency curve is more flat than the normal curve, it is designated as Platykurtic.

Distribution Kurtosis Comparison

Measure of Kurtosis

The measure of kurtosis of a frequency distribution is based on moments and is denoted by β₂:

Formula for Kurtosis

β₂ = \(\frac{\mu_4}{\mu_2^2}\)

Where μ₄ = fourth central moment, μ₂ = second central moment

Interpretation of Kurtosis
  • If β₂ = 3: The distribution is normal and the curve is mesokurtic
  • If β₂ > 3: The distribution is more peaked and the curve is leptokurtic
  • If β₂ < 3: The distribution is flat-topped and the curve is platykurtic
Example: Kurtosis of crop yield in Haryana

Analysis of per hectare yield of wheat in different districts of Haryana:

Yield (Quintals/hectare) Number of Districts
35-40 2
40-45 5
45-50 8
50-55 12
55-60 8
60-65 3

Solution:

On calculating the central moments:
μ₂ = 42.5
μ₄ = 110,250

β₂ = \(\frac{110,250}{(42.5)^2}\) = \(\frac{110,250}{1,806.25}\) = 61.04

Since β₂ = 61.04 > 3, the distribution is leptokurtic, i.e., it is more peaked compared to the normal distribution.

2. Comparison:
Karl-Pearson's Method: Based on the entire series, uses mean/mode/SD, affected by extreme values.
Bowley's Method: Based only on quartiles, less affected by extreme values, easier to calculate.
Bowley's method is more reliable because it is less affected by extreme values.

On calculating SD, it is 11.24
Karl-Pearson's Sk = (28.33 - 30)/11.24 = -0.149
The distribution is slightly negatively skewed.

a) Mesokurtic: β₂ = 3 (Normal distribution)
b) Leptokurtic: β₂ > 3 (More peaked)
