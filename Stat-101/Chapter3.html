3.1
INTRODUCTION

For practical use, it is not enough to reduce a dataset to a frequency table and chart, especially when comparing multiple datasets. A dataset can be summarised by a single value, usually near the centre, that represents the whole dataset. It shows where the data tends to gather. This tendency of data to group around a central point is called Central Tendency, and the value is called a measure of central tendency.

Since this value shows the general position of the dataset within the range of values, it is also called a measure of location or position. These measures are commonly known as Averages. In normal usage, the word 'average' usually means the arithmetic mean. So, when someone talks about the 'average' of a dataset without further detail, it is generally taken to mean the arithmetic mean. The term 'average' has been used since the time of Pythagoras (570–500 BC).

Two things should be noted. First, the central value must lie within the range of the dataset. Second, changing the order of the data should not affect this value.

From the late 19th century, it became common to separate a sample from the population it comes from. Latin letters are used for sample statistics, and Greek letters for population parameters. Population parameters are rarely calculated directly, because full population data is usually not available. Instead, sample data is used to calculate measures, which are treated as estimates of the population parameters.

3.2
Standards for a Proper Measure of Central Tendency

Different types of averages are used to find the representative or "typical" value of a dataset or distribution. So, it is important that an average should meet the following conditions:

  1. It should be clearly defined,
  2. It should be based on all the observations,
  3. It should be easy to understand and interpret,
  4. It should be quick and simple to calculate,
  5. It should allow further mathematical treatments,
  6. It should remain fairly stable in repeated sampling, and
  7. It should not be affected too much by extreme values.

An average that satisfies most or all of these points is considered a good average.

3.3
Types of Averages

The most common types of averages are:

  1. Arithmetic Mean – also called simply the mean; a mathematical average indicating the overall magnitude of values.
  2. Geometric Mean – a mathematical average useful for multiplicative data or growth rates.
  3. Harmonic Mean – a mathematical average suitable for rates and ratios.
  4. Median – shows the central position of the dataset.
  5. Mode – identifies the most frequently occurring value in the dataset.
3.4
The Arithmetic Mean

The arithmetic mean, or simply the mean, is the most commonly used average. It is defined as the value obtained by dividing the total of all observations by their count:

\[Mean = \frac{\text{Sum of all observations}}{\text{Number of observations}}\]

The mean may refer to either a population or a sample drawn from it. If the observations represent a population, the mean is called the population mean and is denoted by \(\mu\) (Greek letter mu). For a set of N observations \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_N\), it is given by:

\[\mu = \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \ldots + x_N}{N} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i}{N}\]

Here, \(\sum\) (Greek capital sigma) is used to indicate summation.

If the observations represent a sample, the mean is called the sample mean and is denoted by a bar over the variable. For n observations \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\), it is defined as:

\[\overline{x} = \frac{x_1 + x_2 + \ldots + x_n}{n} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}\]

Here, \(\overline{x}\) is the mean of a sample of size n.

The population mean is a fixed value, while the sample mean varies depending on the sample taken from the population.

To understand the meaning of the arithmetic mean, assume \(x_i\) is the marks scored by the i-th student in a class. Then \(\sum x_i\) is the total marks of all students, and \(\overline{x}\), the mean, shows the marks each student would have scored if all had equal marks. Geometrically, the mean is the point where the dataset would balance.

3.4.1
The Weighted Arithmetic Mean

In a dataset, numbers that show the importance of different observations are called weights. If observations are not equally important, we give them weights \(w_1, w_2, ..., w_n\) based on their significance. The weighted mean, denoted by \(\overline{x}_w\), for n values \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\) with weights \(w_1, w_2, ..., w_n\), is calculated as:

\[\overline{x}_w = \frac{w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \ldots + w_n x_n}{w_1 + w_2 + \ldots + w_n} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}\]

The weighted mean is commonly used for index numbers, birth rates, death rates, and similar statistics.

3.4.2
Properties of Arithmetic Mean

The arithmetic mean has four main properties, which are quite useful:

  1. For a given dataset, the algebraic sum of the deviations of the observations \(x_i\)'s from their arithmetic mean \(\overline{x}\) is always zero.

    Proof:

    \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \overline{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i - \sum_{i=1}^{n} \overline{x}\]

    Since \(\overline{x}\) is a constant value for the given dataset, \(\sum_{i=1}^{n} \overline{x}\) is simply \(n\) times \(\overline{x}\).

    \[= \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i - n\overline{x}\]

    We know that \(\overline{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}\). Substituting this into the equation:

    \[= \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i - n \left( \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n} \right)\] \[= \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i - \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\] \[= 0\]

  2. The sum of the squared deviations of the observations \(x_i\)'s from their mean \(\overline{x}\) is always a minimum.

    This property is often called the 'minimal property' of the mean.

    Proof:

    Let's consider a function \(f(a)\) which is the sum of squared deviations from an arbitrary value \(a\):

    \[f(a) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - a)^2\]

    To find the value of \(a\) that minimizes this function, we take the first derivative of \(f(a)\) with respect to \(a\) and set it to zero:

    \[\frac{d}{da} f(a) = \frac{d}{da} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - a)^2\] \[= \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{d}{da} (x_i - a)^2\] \[= \sum_{i=1}^{n} 2(x_i - a)(-1)\] \[= -2 \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - a)\]

    Set the derivative to zero:

    \[-2 \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - a) = 0\] \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - a) = 0\] \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i - \sum_{i=1}^{n} a = 0\] \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i - na = 0\] \[na = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\] \[a = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}\]

    This shows that \(a\) must be equal to the arithmetic mean \(\overline{x}\). To confirm it's a minimum, we check the second derivative:

    \[\frac{d^2}{da^2} f(a) = \frac{d}{da} \left( -2 \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - a) \right)\] \[= -2 \sum_{i=1}^{n} (-1)\] \[= -2(-n) = 2n\]

    Since \(n\) (the number of observations) is always positive, \(2n\) is also positive. A positive second derivative confirms that \(a = \overline{x}\) yields a minimum value for the sum of squared deviations.

  3. When you have a population divided into several subgroups, each with distinct means, the overall population mean \(\overline{X}\) can be determined by calculating the weighted mean of these subgroup means. The weights here are proportional to the size of each subgroup.

    Proof:

    Let's use the notation \(X\) to represent the total dataset/population, and \(X_1, X_2, ..., X_k\) for the \(k\) subgroups. Their respective means are \(\overline{x}_1, \overline{x}_2, ..., \overline{x}_k\), and the number of observations in each subgroup is \(n_1, n_2, ..., n_k\).

    The mean for each subgroup is:

    \[\overline{x}_j = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n_j} x_{ji}}{n_j}\]

    The overall mean \(\overline{X}\) for the entire population is then:

    \[\overline{X} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{k} \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} x_{ji}}{\sum_{j=1}^{k} n_j}\]

    We can re-arrange this a bit:

    \[\overline{X} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{k} n_j \cdot \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n_j} x_{ji}}{n_j}}{\sum_{j=1}^{k} n_j}\]

    Which simplifies to the weighted mean of the subgroup means:

    \[\overline{X} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{k} n_j \overline{x}_j}{\sum_{j=1}^{k} n_j}\]

  4. If observations \(y_i\) are linearly transformed from observations \(x_i\) such that \(y_i = ax_i + b\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants and \(a \ne 0\), then the arithmetic mean of \(y\) (\(\overline{y}\)) will also be related to the arithmetic mean of \(x\) (\(\overline{x}\)) by the same linear transformation.

    This is often called the 'invariance of the mean under linear transformation'.

    Proof:

    The mean of \(y_i\) is defined as:

    \[\overline{y} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i}{n}\]

    Substitute the given relationship \(y_i = ax_i + b\):

    \[\overline{y} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (ax_i + b)}{n}\]

    We can separate the sum:

    \[\overline{y} = \frac{1}{n} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{n} ax_i + \sum_{i=1}^{n} b \right)\]

    Since \(a\) is a constant, we can pull it out of the summation. Also, \(\sum_{i=1}^{n} b\) is simply \(n\) times \(b\):

    \[\overline{y} = \frac{1}{n} \left( a \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i + nb \right)\]

    Now, distribute the \(\frac{1}{n}\):

    \[\overline{y} = \frac{a \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n} + \frac{nb}{n}\] \[\overline{y} = a \left( \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n} \right) + b\]

    Since \(\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}\) is by definition the mean of \(x\), i.e., \(\overline{x}\):

    \[\overline{y} = a\overline{x} + b\]

    This proves the property.

3.4.3
Mean from Grouped Data: A Weighted‑Average Approach

When the number of observations is very large, data is often grouped into a frequency distribution. This grouping provides approximate values for descriptive statistics because the individual identity of observations is lost.

To calculate the approximate mean, observations within each class are assumed to be equal to the class mid-point. Thus, multiplying the class frequency by its midpoint approximately represents the sum of observations for that class.

Therefore, if a frequency distribution has \(k\) classes with class marks \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k\) and corresponding frequencies \(f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k\) (\(\sum f_i=n\)), the mean is calculated using the formula:

\[\overline{x} = \frac{f_1 x_1 + f_2 x_2 + \ldots + f_k x_k}{f_1 + f_2 + \ldots + f_k} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i x_i}{n}\]

Since \(f_i\) represents the count of observations in a class, the mean calculated from a frequency distribution can also be considered a weighted mean. Here, each class mid-point \(x_i\) is weighted by its frequency \(f_i\), and the total weighted products are divided by the sum of all frequencies (the total weights).

The calculated \(\overline{x}\) might show slight variations due to the approximation of all observations within a class by its midpoint. However, experience indicates that this error is typically minor and not a serious concern. The following example demonstrates this point.

3.4.4
Change of Origin and Scale

To simplify computational labor, particularly for large datasets or grouped data, the arithmetic mean can be calculated using a change of origin and/or scale. This method is often referred to as the "coding method" or "short method."

If \(x_i\) represents an observed value, and constants \(a\) (arbitrary origin or assumed mean) and \(h\) (scale, typically the class interval width for grouped data, where \(h \ne 0\)) are chosen, a coded value \(u_i\) can be defined as:

\[u_i = \frac{x_i - a}{h}\]

From this transformation, we can express \(x_i\) as \(x_i = a + hu_i\). Substituting this into the formula for the arithmetic mean \(\overline{x}\):

\[\overline{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\] \[\overline{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (a + hu_i)\]

By the properties of summation, where \(a\) and \(h\) are constants:

\[\overline{x} = \frac{1}{n} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{n} a + \sum_{i=1}^{n} hu_i \right)\] \[\overline{x} = \frac{na}{n} + \frac{h}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i\]

Recognizing that \(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i\) is the mean of the coded values, \(\overline{u}\):

\[\overline{x} = a + h\overline{u}\]

This formula allows for the calculation of the arithmetic mean from a transformed set of values. For grouped data, \(h\) is typically chosen as the class width, and \(a\) as the midpoint of a class, often one with a high frequency, to minimize the magnitude of \(u_i\) values and thus simplify calculations. This procedure significantly reduces the complexity of manual computation.

3.5
The Geometric Mean

The Geometric Mean (GM) is a type of average that is used for a set of positive numbers. It is particularly useful when dealing with data that is multiplicative in nature, such as growth rates, ratios, or percentages.

Definition for Ungrouped Data:

For a set of \(n\) positive observations \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\), the geometric mean (denoted as \(G\)) is defined as the \(n\)-th root of their product:

\[G = \sqrt[n]{x_1 x_2 \ldots x_n} = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} x_i\right)^{\frac{1}{n}} \quad (\text{where } x_i > 0)\]

Calculation using Logarithms:

For computational convenience, especially with a large number of observations, the geometric mean can be calculated using logarithms. Taking the logarithm of both sides of the definition:

\[\log G = \log \left[ \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} x_i\right)^{\frac{1}{n}} \right]\] \[\log G = \frac{1}{n} \log \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} x_i\right)\] \[\log G = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log x_i\]

This implies that the logarithm of the geometric mean is the arithmetic mean of the logarithms of the individual observations. To find \(G\), one takes the antilogarithm of this result:

\[G = \text{antilog} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log x_i \right]\]

Definition for Grouped Frequency Distributions:

For data arranged in a grouped frequency distribution with \(k\) classes, having class marks \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k\) and corresponding frequencies \(f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k\) (where \(n = \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i\) is the total number of observations), the geometric mean is given by:

\[G = \left[x_1^{f_1} x_2^{f_2} \ldots x_k^{f_k}\right]^{\frac{1}{n}} = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{k} x_i^{f_i}\right)^{\frac{1}{n}}\]

In logarithmic form, the formula becomes:

\[\log G = \frac{1}{n} \left[f_1 \log x_1 + f_2 \log x_2 + \ldots + f_k \log x_k\right] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i \log x_i\]

And subsequently:

\[G = \text{antilog} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i \log x_i \right]\]

Weighted Geometric Mean:

For a set of values \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k\) with respective weights \(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_k\), the logarithm of the weighted geometric mean (\(G_w\)) is given by:

\[\log G_w = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i \log x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i}\] \[G_w = \text{antilog} \left[ \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i \log x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i} \right]\]

Application:

The geometric mean is particularly appropriate for averaging ratios, rates of change, and quantities that exhibit multiplicative relationships over time or across different categories.

3.6
The Harmonic Mean

The Harmonic Mean (HM) is a type of average that is particularly useful for data sets consisting of rates, speeds, or ratios, especially when the numerator (e.g., distance in speed, amount of work in rates) is constant.

Definition for Ungrouped Data:

For a set of \(n\) non-zero values \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\), the harmonic mean (denoted as \(H\)) is defined as the reciprocal of the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals of those values:

\[H = \frac{n}{\frac{1}{x_1} + \frac{1}{x_2} + \ldots + \frac{1}{x_n}} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{x_i}} \quad (\text{where } x_i \ne 0)\]

Application and Example:

The harmonic mean is the appropriate average to use when rates are expressed as "x per y" and the quantity "x" is constant. Conversely, if "y" is constant, the arithmetic mean is typically more suitable. Consider an example: A car travels the first 30 km at 15 km/hr, the second 30 km at 20 km/hr, and the third 30 km at 25 km/hr. Since the distance (numerator) for each segment is constant (30 km), the harmonic mean is the correct average to find the overall average speed.

In this scenario, the harmonic mean is calculated as:

\[H = \frac{3}{\frac{1}{15} + \frac{1}{20} + \frac{1}{25}}\] \[H = \frac{3}{\left(\frac{4+3}{60}\right) + \frac{1}{25}} = \frac{3}{\frac{7}{60} + \frac{1}{25}} = \frac{3}{\frac{35+12}{300}} = \frac{3}{\frac{47}{300}}\] \[H = \frac{3 \times 300}{47} = \frac{900}{47} \approx 19.15 \text{ km/hr}\]

This result represents the average speed over the entire journey.

Definition for Grouped Frequency Distributions:

For data organized into a grouped frequency distribution with \(k\) classes, having class marks \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k\) and corresponding frequencies \(f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k\) (where \(n = \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i\) is the total number of observations), the harmonic mean is given by:

\[H = \frac{n}{\frac{f_1}{x_1} + \frac{f_2}{x_2} + \ldots + \frac{f_k}{x_k}} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{f_i}{x_i}}\]

Weighted Harmonic Mean:

For a set of values \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k\) with respective weights \(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_k\), the weighted harmonic mean (\(H_w\)) is defined as:

\[H_w = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{w_i}{x_i}}\]

3.7
The Median

The Median is a measure of central tendency that represents the middle value in an ordered dataset. It divides the data into two equal halves: 50% of the observations are at or below the median, and 50% are at or above it. Unlike the mean, the median is not affected by extremely large or small values (outliers), making it a robust measure for skewed distributions.

Calculating the Median for Ungrouped Data:

To find the median for a set of \(n\) observations \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\):

  1. Order the data: Arrange all observations from the smallest to the largest.
  2. Determine the position:
    • If \(n\) (the total number of observations) is odd, the median is the value at the \((\frac{n+1}{2})^{\text{th}}\) position.
    • If \(n\) is even, the median is the average of the two middle values, specifically the values at the \((\frac{n}{2})^{\text{th}}\) and \((\frac{n}{2}+1)^{\text{th}}\) positions.

For an ungrouped frequency distribution, the median can be identified by constructing a cumulative frequency distribution and locating the value corresponding to the \((\frac{n+1}{2})^{\text{th}}\) or the average of the \((\frac{n}{2})^{\text{th}}\) and \((\frac{n}{2}+1)^{\text{th}}\) observation, similar to ungrouped data, but using cumulative frequencies to find the position.

Calculating the Median for Grouped Frequency Distributions:

For data presented in a grouped frequency distribution, the median represents the value on the horizontal axis that corresponds to a cumulative frequency of \(n/2\). This value lies within a specific class interval, known as the median class or median group.

To obtain a precise median value from grouped data, an assumption is made that the observations are uniformly distributed within the median class. The median is then calculated using the following formula:

\[\text{Median} = L + \left(\frac{\frac{n}{2} - CF}{f}\right) \times h\]

Where:

  • \(L\) = Lower boundary of the median class.
  • \(n\) = Total number of observations (sum of all frequencies).
  • \(CF\) = Cumulative frequency of the class immediately preceding the median class.
  • \(f\) = Frequency of the median class.
  • \(h\) = Class width (or class size) of the median class.

This formula essentially interpolates the median within the median class, assuming a linear distribution of observations within that interval.

3.7.1
Quantiles

In statistics, when dealing with large datasets, it is often useful to divide the data into several equal parts to understand its distribution more comprehensively. These division points are collectively known as Quantiles or Fractiles.

Types of Quantiles:

  • Quartiles:

    These are three specific values that divide an ordered dataset into four equal parts. They are denoted as \(Q_1\), \(Q_2\), and \(Q_3\):

    • \(Q_1\) (First or Lower Quartile): The value below which 25% of the data falls.
    • \(Q_2\) (Second Quartile): The value below which 50% of the data falls. This is equivalent to the median.
    • \(Q_3\) (Third or Upper Quartile): The value below which 75% of the data falls.

  • Deciles:

    These are nine values that divide an ordered dataset into ten equal parts. They are denoted as \(D_1, D_2, \ldots, D_9\). For instance, \(D_1\) is the value below which 10% of the data falls, and \(D_5\) is equivalent to the median.

  • Percentiles:

    These are 99 values that divide an ordered dataset into one hundred equal parts. They are denoted as \(P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_{99}\). \(P_j\) is the value below which \(j\) percent of the data falls. For example, \(P_{25}\) is equivalent to \(Q_1\), \(P_{50}\) is the median (\(Q_2\) or \(D_5\)), and \(P_{75}\) is equivalent to \(Q_3\).

Calculating Quantiles for Ungrouped Data:

For a set of \(n\) observations arranged in ascending order, the \(j\)-th percentile, \(P_j\), can be calculated using the following rules:

  1. If \(jn/100\) is not an integer, the \(j\)-th percentile is the observation located at the position given by: \[\text{Position} = \lfloor \frac{jn}{100} \rfloor + 1\] where \(\lfloor x \rfloor\) denotes the greatest integer less than or equal to \(x\).

  2. If \(jn/100\) **is an integer**, the \(j\)-th percentile is the average of the observations at the following two positions: \[\text{Position 1} = \frac{jn}{100}\] \[\text{Position 2} = \frac{jn}{100} + 1\]

Calculating Quantiles for Grouped Data:

For data presented in a grouped frequency distribution, the calculation of quantiles (quartiles, deciles, percentiles) follows a method similar to that used for the median. The general formula for the \(j\)-th percentile \(P_j\) for grouped data is:

\[P_j = L + \left(\frac{\frac{jn}{100} - CF}{f}\right) \times h\]

Where:

  • \(L\) = Lower boundary of the class containing the \(j\)-th percentile.
  • \(n\) = Total number of observations (sum of all frequencies).
  • \(CF\) = Cumulative frequency of the class immediately preceding the percentile class.
  • \(f\) = Frequency of the percentile class.
  • \(h\) = Class width of the percentile class.

Historical Note:

The terms "median" and "percentile" are often attributed to Sir Francis Galton (1822–1911), although the concept of the median was earlier suggested by Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777–1855).

From the cumulative frequency table, we see that the 100th observation falls in the class 0-2.

Therefore \(l = 0\), \(h = 2\), \(C = 0\), \(f = 100\)

\[Q_1 = l + h \left( \frac{\frac{n}{4} - C}{f} \right) = 0 + 2 \left( \frac{100 - 0}{100} \right) = 0 + 2 \left( \frac{100}{100} \right) = 0 + 2(1) = 2 \text{ acres}\]

For \(Q_3\), we have \(3n/4 = (3 \times 400)/4 = 300\)

From the cumulative frequency table, the 300th observation falls in the class 5-10.

Therefore \(l = 5\), \(h = 5\), \(C = 250\), \(f = 80\)

\[Q_3 = l + h \left( \frac{\frac{3n}{4} - C}{f} \right) = 5 + 5 \left( \frac{300 - 250}{80} \right) = 5 + 5 \left( \frac{50}{80} \right) = 5 + 5(0.625) = 5 + 3.125 = 8.125 \text{ acres}\]
3.8
The Mode

The Mode is a measure of central tendency that represents the value or values that occur most frequently in a dataset or distribution. It signifies the most typical observation within the data.

Characteristics of the Mode:

  • Unimodal: A distribution with only one mode.
  • Bimodal: A distribution with two modes, indicating two distinct peaks in frequency.
  • Multimodal: A distribution with more than two modes.
  • Unlike the mean and median, the mode can be used with categorical data (non-numeric data).

Calculating the Mode for Ungrouped Data:

For ungrouped data or an ungrouped frequency distribution, the mode is directly identified as the value of the variable that has the highest frequency of occurrence.

Calculating the Mode for Grouped Frequency Distributions:

For data presented in a grouped frequency distribution with equal class intervals, a specific value for the mode is typically estimated. First, the **modal class** is identified as the class interval with the highest frequency. The mode is then calculated using the following formula, which interpolates within the modal class:

\[\text{Mode} = L + \left( \frac{d_1}{d_1 + d_2} \right) \times h\]

Where:

  • \(L\) = Lower boundary of the modal class.
  • \(h\) = Width of the class interval of the modal class.
  • \(d_1\) = The difference between the frequency of the modal class and the frequency of the class immediately preceding it.
  • \(d_2\) = The difference between the frequency of the modal class and the frequency of the class immediately succeeding it.

This formula provides an estimate of the mode, assuming a specific distribution pattern of frequencies around the modal class.

Example 3.13a: Mode of Daily Temperature in an Agricultural Farm in Haryana

Find the mode of the frequency distribution for daily temperature (in degrees Celsius) in an agricultural farm:

Temperature (°C) 20-25 25-30 30-35 35-40 40-45 45-50
Number of Days (f) 8 12 25 18 10 5

Solution:

The highest frequency is 25, which falls in the class 30-35. Therefore, the modal class is 30-35, and we have:

\(l = 30\), \(h = 5\), \(d_1 = 25 - 12 = 13\), \(d_2 = 25 - 18 = 7\)

\[\text{Mode} = l + h \left( \frac{d_1}{d_1 + d_2} \right) = 30 + 5 \left( \frac{13}{13 + 7} \right) = 30 + 5 \left( \frac{13}{20} \right) = 30 + 5(0.65) = 30 + 3.25 = 33.25 \text{ °C}\]

3.9
Emprical Relationship Between Mean, Median and Mode

For moderately asymmetrical distributions, the empirical relationship between mean, median and mode is as follows:

\[\text{Mode} \approx 3(\text{Median}) - 2(\text{Mean})\] or \[\text{Mean} - \text{Mode} \approx 3(\text{Mean} - \text{Median})\]

This relationship is approximately true for a normal curve and any symmetrical distribution.

  • Median = 50.8 quintals (approximate)

3.10
Relative Merits and Demarits of Measures of Central Tandencies

Each of the averages we have discussed has its own merits and demerits, and is useful in certain situations. We list below the merits and demerits of the different averages.

3.10.1 The Arithmetic Mean

Merits:

  1. It is easy to understand and simple to calculate.
  2. It is based on all observations.
  3. It is capable of further algebraic treatment.
  4. It is a stable average as it is least affected by fluctuations of sampling.
  5. The sum of deviations from the arithmetic mean is always zero.
  6. The sum of squares of deviations from the mean is minimum.

Demerits:

  1. It is unduly influenced by extreme values.
  2. It cannot be calculated for distributions with open-end classes.
  3. It may not be a value actually observed in the data.
  4. It cannot be determined by inspection or graphical method.

3.10.2 The Geometric Mean

Merits:

  1. It is based on all observations.
  2. It is not unduly influenced by extreme values.
  3. It gives proper results when averaging ratios, percentages, and rates of change.
  4. It is capable of algebraic treatment.

Demerits:

  1. It is relatively difficult to understand.
  2. It cannot be easily calculated.
  3. It cannot be calculated if the value of any observation is negative or zero.
  4. It is always less than the arithmetic mean except when all observations are equal.
  5. It gives too much importance to smaller items.

3.10.3 The Harmonic Mean

Merits:

  1. It is based on all observations.
  2. It can be used to average ratios or rates, especially when the numerator is kept constant.
  3. It gives minimum importance to larger items.

Demerits:

  1. It is difficult to understand and calculate.
  2. It cannot be calculated if the value of any observation is zero or negative.
  3. It gives too much importance to smaller items.
  4. It is always less than the arithmetic mean and geometric mean except when all observations are equal.

3.10.4 The Median

Merits:

  1. It is easy to understand and calculate.
  2. It can be located graphically.
  3. It can be calculated for distributions with open-end classes.
  4. It is not unduly influenced by extreme values.
  5. It can also be determined when some information about the data is unavailable.

Demerits:

  1. It is not based on all observations.
  2. It is not capable of further mathematical treatment.
  3. It is not a stable average as it is affected by fluctuations of sampling.

3.10.5 The Mode

Merits:

  1. It is easy to understand.
  2. It can be located graphically.
  3. It can be determined for qualitative data.
  4. It is not affected by extreme values.
  5. It is the most typical value.

Demerits:

  1. It is not based on all observations.
  2. It is not capable of further mathematical treatment.
  3. In some cases, it is ill-defined.
  4. It is the least stable among all averages.
3.11
When to Choose which Measure of Central Tendacies

Each measure of central tendency possesses distinct strengths and limitations; consequently, the choice of an appropriate average depends on the characteristics of the data and the analytical objective. The following guidelines assist in selecting a suitable average:

  • Symmetrical distributions: When the frequency distribution is roughly symmetric, the mean, median, and mode are nearly equal. In such cases, the arithmetic mean is preferred because of its desirable mathematical properties (e.g., linearity, algebraic manipulability).
  • Skewed distributions: For markedly skewed data, the median (robust to outliers) or the mode (indicates the most frequent value) provides a more representative summary than the mean.
  • Open‑ended class intervals: When the data contain open‑ended (unbounded) classes, the median is generally favored because it does not require knowledge of the extreme values.
  • Only totals are available: If the only information supplied is the aggregate total and the number of observations, the arithmetic mean is the sole feasible average.
  • Averaging ratios or rates of change: The geometric mean is theoretically optimal for multiplicative quantities such as growth rates, index numbers, or ratios. When the numerator is held constant (e.g., speeds, travel times), the harmonic mean is more appropriate.
  • Empirical relationship for skewed data: When the mean is unsuitable and the other averages are difficult to compute, the empirical approximation Mean–Mode≈3(Mean–Median) may be employed as a rough adjustment.
  • Pricing of heterogeneous items: For aggregating prices of disparate goods (e.g., furniture, appliances), the mode often reflects the most common price level and can be more informative than the mean.
  • Equal‑period rates of change: When rates are measured over equal time intervals (e.g., annual returns), the geometric mean provides the correct average growth factor.
  • Weighted observations: If observations carry different relative importance, the weighted arithmetic mean should be used.
  • Qualitative data: For categorical or ordinal data, the median offers a central position, while the mode identifies the most frequent category; both are preferable to the mean, which lacks interpretability for non‑numeric data.

In practice, no single average fully characterizes a data set. Computing multiple measures—such as the mean, median, and mode—provides a more comprehensive description of the distribution’s central tendency and dispersion.

3.12
PARTITION VALUES

Partition values are points that divide a data set or statistical distribution into equal portions. The most common partition values are the median, quartiles, deciles, and percentiles.

3.12.1 Median

The median is the observation that separates the ordered data into two halves; 50% of the observations are ≤median and 50% are ≥median.

3.12.2 Quartiles

Quartiles split the data into four equal parts.

  • First quartile\(Q_{1}\): the value below which 25% of the observations lie.
  • Second quartile\(Q_{2}\): identical to the median (50%).
  • Third quartile\(Q_{3}\): the value below which 75% of the observations lie.

3.12.3 Deciles

Deciles partition the data into ten equal segments.

  • First decile\(D_{1}\): 10% of observations are ≤\(D_{1}\).
  • Second decile\(D_{2}\): 20% of observations are ≤\(D_{2}\).
  • ⋮
  • Fifth decile\(D_{5}\): coincides with the median (50%).
  • ⋮
  • Tenth decile\(D_{10}\): the maximum observation.

3.12.4 Percentiles

Percentiles divide the data into one hundred equal parts.

  • First percentile\(P_{1}\): 1% of observations are ≤\(P_{1}\).
  • Second percentile\(P_{2}\): 2% of observations are ≤\(P_{2}\).
  • ⋮
  • Fiftieth percentile\(P_{50}\): identical to the median (50%).
  • ⋮
  • Hundredth percentile\(P_{100}\): the greatest observation.

All partition values are calculated in the same way as the median: one determines the ordered data, computes the cumulative frequencies, and locates the value at which the required cumulative proportion (\(\frac{k}{m}\), where \(k\) is the desired part and \(m\)=2,4,10, or100) is attained. When the required proportion falls between two observed values, linear interpolation within the containing class (for grouped data) provides an estimate of the partition value.

3.13
Applications of Partition Values

Partition values have numerous applications in statistical analysis, including the following:

  • Assessing distribution shape: By comparing the distances between successive quartiles (e.g., \(Q_{1}, Q_{2}, Q_{3}\)), one can infer whether the distribution is symmetric, skewed, or has heavy tails.
  • Estimating the proportion of observations within a range: If \(Q_{1}=20\) and \(Q_{3}=60\), then 50 % of the data lie between 20 and 60, because the inter‑quartile range always contains the middle half of the observations.
  • Defining measures of dispersion:
    • Inter‑quartile range (IQR): \(IQR = Q_{3} - Q_{1}\)
    • Semi‑inter‑quartile range: \(\frac{IQR}{2}\)
    • Percentile coefficient of dispersion: \(\frac{P_{90} - P_{10}}{P_{90} + P_{10}}\)
  • Quantifying skewness: Bowley’s coefficient of skewness uses quartiles: \[ \text{Skewness}_{\text{Bowley}} = \frac{(Q_{3}+Q_{1}-2Q_{2})}{Q_{3}-Q_{1}} \]
  • Interpolation and extrapolation in cumulative frequency distributions: Partition values serve as reference points for estimating intermediate frequencies or extending the distribution beyond observed data.
  • Testing normality: Comparing empirical quartiles or deciles with their theoretical counterparts under a normal distribution (e.g., via a Q‑Q plot) helps assess whether the data follow a normal pattern.
  • Interpreting test scores in educational statistics: Percentile ranks (e.g., a student at the 85th percentile) provide a relative standing of an individual’s performance within a reference group.

Example 3.15a: Results of a Crop Yield Survey

In a crop yield survey, the yield (in quintals per acre) ranged from 5 to 25. The median yield was 15, the first quartile was 10, and the third quartile was 20. Answer the following questions:

  2. What percentage of fields had a yield between 10 and 15 quintals?
  3. What percentage of fields had a yield between 15 and 20 quintals?
  4. What percentage of fields had a yield greater than 20 quintals?
  5. If the survey included 500 fields, how many fields had a yield greater than 20 quintals?

Solution:

  1. The first quartile (\(Q_1\)) is the yield below which 25% of the fields have yield. Therefore, 25% of the fields had a yield less than 10 quintals.
  2. The median (\(Q_2\)) is the yield below which 50% of the fields have yield. Therefore, 50% - 25% = 25% of the fields had a yield between 10 and 15 quintals.
  3. The third quartile (\(Q_3\)) is the yield below which 75% of the fields have yield. Therefore, 75% - 50% = 25% of the fields had a yield between 15 and 20 quintals.
  4. 100% - 75% = 25% of the fields had a yield greater than 20 quintals.
  5. 25% of 500 = 125 fields had a yield greater than 20 quintals.

3.14
SUMMARY
  • An average is a single numerical summary that represents a collection of data points. Five common types of averages are:
    • Arithmetic mean
    • Geometric mean
    • Harmonic mean
    • Median
    • Mode
  • The arithmetic mean (or simply the mean) of \(n\) observations \(\{x_{1},\dots ,x_{n}\}\) is obtained by dividing the sum of the observations by \(n\): \[ \text{Mean} = \frac{x_{1}+x_{2}+\dots +x_{n}}{n}. \]
  • The geometric mean of \(n\) positive values is the \(n\)-th root of their product: \[ \text{Geometric Mean} = \bigl(x_{1}\,x_{2}\,\dots \,x_{n}\bigr)^{1/n}. \]
  • The harmonic mean of \(n\) values is the reciprocal of the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals: \[ \text{Harmonic Mean} = \frac{n}{\displaystyle\frac{1}{x_{1}}+\frac{1}{x_{2}}+\dots +\frac{1}{x_{n}}}. \]
  • The median of ordered observations is the middle observation when \(n\) is odd; if \(n\) is even, it is the average of the two central observations.
  • The mode is the value that occurs most frequently in a data set; a distribution may be unimodal, bimodal, or multimodal.
  • For moderately asymmetrical distributions the empirical relationship among mean, median, and mode may be approximated by: \[ \text{Mode} \approx 3(\text{Median}) - 2(\text{Mean}). \]
  • Each average has specific merits and limitations, making it appropriate for particular data structures and analytical objectives.
  • Partition values divide a data set into equal parts; common examples are the median, quartiles, deciles, and percentiles.
  • Applications of partition values in statistical analysis include:
    • Assessing distribution shape by examining distances between quartiles.
    • Estimating the proportion of items within a specified range (e.g., the inter‑quartile range contains 50 % of observations).
    • Constructing measures of dispersion such as the inter‑quartile range, semi‑inter‑quartile range, and percentile coefficient of dispersion.
    • Evaluating skewness via Bowley’s coefficient of skewness that employs quartiles.
    • Performing interpolation and extrapolation in cumulative‑frequency distributions.
