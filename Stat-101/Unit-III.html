Introduction

Introduction

The measures of central tendency serve to locate the center of the distribution, but they do not reveal how the items are spread out on either side of the center. This characteristic of a frequency distribution is commonly referred to as dispersion. In any series, all the items are not equal; there is a difference or variation among the values. The degree of this variation is evaluated by various measures of dispersion. Small dispersion indicates high uniformity of the items, while large dispersion indicates less uniformity.

Central tendency के measures distribution के center का पता लगाने का काम करते हैं, लेकिन वे यह प्रकट नहीं करते हैं कि items center के दोनों ओर कैसे फैले हुए हैं। Frequency distribution की इस विशेषता को आमतौर पर dispersion कहा जाता है। किसी भी series में, सभी items समान नहीं होते हैं; values के बीच अंतर या variation होता है। इस variation की degree का मूल्यांकन विभिन्न measures of dispersion द्वारा किया जाता है। Small dispersion items की उच्च एकरूपता (high uniformity) को इंगित करता है, जबकि large dispersion कम एकरूपता को इंगित करता है।

Consider the following marks of two students:

निम्नलिखित दो students के marks पर विचार करें:

दोनों students के कुल 345 marks हैं और प्रत्येक का average 69 है। तथ्य यह है कि दूसरा student एक paper (25 marks) में fail हो गया है। जब केवल averages पर विचार किया जाता है, तो दोनों students समान प्रतीत होते हैं। हालाँकि, पहले student के marks में दूसरे की तुलना में कम variation है, जो एक desirable characteristic है।

Characteristics of a Good Measure of Dispersion

Characteristics of a Good Measure of Dispersion

An ideal dispersion statistic should possess the same properties required of an ideal measure of central tendency. Specifically, it should:

एक ideal dispersion statistic में वही properties होनी चाहिए जो एक ideal measure of central tendency के लिए आवश्यक हैं। विशेष रूप से, इसे यह होना चाहिए:

  1. Be well‑defined: The formula must be unambiguous and applicable to any data set that meets the required conditions.
  2. Be easy to calculate and interpret: Practitioners should be able to compute the value without excessive effort and understand what the number conveys about the data.
  3. Rely solely on the observed values: No external constants or parameters should be needed.
  4. Be amenable to further mathematical treatment: The measure should integrate smoothly into algebraic manipulations, hypothesis testing, and comparison across samples.
  5. Exhibit stability against sampling variation: Small changes in the sample should not cause large, erratic shifts in the value.
  1. Be well‑defined: Formula unambiguous होना चाहिए और किसी भी data set पर लागू होना चाहिए जो आवश्यक शर्तों को पूरा करता हो।
  2. Be easy to calculate and interpret: Practitioners को बिना अत्यधिक प्रयास के value की गणना करने में सक्षम होना चाहिए और यह समझना चाहिए कि संख्या data के बारे में क्या बताती है।
  3. Rely solely on the observed values: किसी बाहरी constants या parameters की आवश्यकता नहीं होनी चाहिए।
  4. Be amenable to further mathematical treatment: Measure को algebraic manipulations, hypothesis testing और samples में comparison में आसानी से integrate होना चाहिए।
  5. Exhibit stability against sampling variation: Sample में छोटे बदलावों के कारण value में बड़े, अनिश्चित (erratic) बदलाव नहीं होने चाहिए।

3.3 Absolute and Relative Measures

3.3 Absolute and Relative Measures

Understanding the two types of dispersion measures

There are two kinds of measures of dispersion, namely:

Dispersion के measures दो प्रकार के होते हैं, अर्थात्:

  1. Absolute measure of dispersion
  2. Relative measure of dispersion
  1. Absolute measure of dispersion
  2. Relative measure of dispersion

An absolute measure of dispersion indicates the amount of variation in a set of values in terms of the units of observation. For example, if rainfall on different days is measured in mm, any absolute measure of dispersion (like standard deviation) will give the variation in rainfall in mm.

एक absolute measure of dispersion observation की units के संदर्भ में values के एक set में variation की मात्रा को इंगित करता है। उदाहरण के लिए, यदि अलग-अलग दिनों में बारिश को mm में मापा जाता है, तो कोई भी absolute measure of dispersion (जैसे standard deviation) बारिश में variation को mm में देगा।

On the other hand, relative measures of dispersion are free from the units of measurement of the observations. They are pure numbers and are used to compare the variation in two or more sets which have different units of measurement.

दूसरी ओर, relative measures of dispersion observations की measurement की units से मुक्त होते हैं। वे शुद्ध संख्याएँ (pure numbers) हैं और इनका उपयोग दो या दो से अधिक sets में variation की तुलना करने के लिए किया जाता है जिनकी measurement की units अलग-अलग होती हैं।

The various absolute and relative measures of dispersion are listed below:

Dispersion के विभिन्न absolute और relative measures नीचे सूचीबद्ध हैं:

Absolute Measure Relative Measure
Range Coefficient of Range
Quartile Deviation Coefficient of Quartile Deviation
Mean Deviation Coefficient of Mean Deviation
Standard Deviation Coefficient of Variation

Range and Coefficient of Range

Range and Coefficient of Range

The simplest measure of dispersion

The simplest measure of dispersion

Range

Range

The range is the simplest possible measure of dispersion and is defined as the difference between the largest and smallest values of the variable.

Range dispersion का simplest possible measure है और इसे variable की largest और smallest values के difference के रूप में define किया जाता है।

Range = \(L - S\)

Where, \(L\) = Largest value and \(S\) = Smallest value.

In individual observations and discrete series, \(L\) and \(S\) are easily identified. In continuous series, one of the following two methods is used:

Individual observations और discrete series में, \(L\) और \(S\) को आसानी से पहचाना जा सकता है। Continuous series में, निम्नलिखित दो methods में से एक का उपयोग किया जाता है:

  • Method 1: \(L\) = Upper boundary of the highest class; \(S\) = Lower boundary of the lowest class.
  • Method 2: \(L\) = Mid-value of the highest class; \(S\) = Mid-value of the lowest class.
  • Method 1: \(L\) = Highest class की Upper boundary; \(S\) = Lowest class की Lower boundary.
  • Method 2: \(L\) = Highest class की Mid-value; \(S\) = Lowest class की Mid-value.

Coefficient of Range

Coefficient of Range

The coefficient of range is a relative measure of dispersion, used to compare the spread of two different datasets.

Coefficient of range dispersion का एक relative measure है, जिसका उपयोग दो अलग-अलग datasets के फैलाव (spread) की तुलना करने के लिए किया जाता है।

Coefficient of Range = \(\frac{L - S}{L + S}\)

Example

Find the value of range and its coefficient for the following data: 7, 9, 6, 8, 11, 10, 4.

Solution:

Largest value (\(L\)) = 11

Smallest value (\(S\)) = 4

\[ \text{Range} = L - S = 11 - 4 = 7 \] \[ \text{Coefficient of Range} = \frac{L - S}{L + S} = \frac{11 - 4}{11 + 4} = \frac{7}{15} \approx 0.4667 \]

Example (Continuous Series)

Size 60-63 63-66 66-69 69-72 72-75
Number 5 18 42 27 8

Solution:

Solution:

Using Method 1:

Method 1 का उपयोग करते हुए:

Largest value (\(L\)) = Upper boundary of the highest class = 75

Largest value (\(L\)) = Highest class की upper boundary = 75

Smallest value (\(S\)) = Lower boundary of the lowest class = 60

Smallest value (\(S\)) = Lowest class की lower boundary = 60

\[ \text{Range} = L - S = 75 - 60 = 15 \] \[ \text{Coefficient of Range} = \frac{L - S}{L + S} = \frac{75 - 60}{75 + 60} = \frac{15}{135} \approx 0.1111 \]

Quartile Deviation and Coefficient of Quartile Deviation

A measure based on inner quartiles

Quartile Deviation (Q.D.)

Quartile Deviation is half of the difference between the third and first quartiles. Hence, it is also known as the Semi-Interquartile Range.

Q.D. = \(\frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{2}\)

The range \((Q_3 - Q_1)\) is called the interquartile range, and \(\frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{2}\) is the semi-interquartile range.

Coefficient of Quartile Deviation

The coefficient of quartile deviation is a relative measure that can be used for comparing the dispersion of different distributions.

Coefficient of Quartile Deviation = \(\frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{Q_3 + Q_1}\)

Mean Deviation and Coefficient of Mean Deviation

Mean Deviation and Coefficient of Mean Deviation

A measure based on all observations

A measure based on all observations

Definition

Definition

Mean Deviation is the arithmetic mean of the absolute deviations of a series computed from any measure of central tendency (mean, median, or mode). The deviations are taken as positive (ignoring signs). We usually compute mean deviation about the mean or median.

Mean Deviation किसी भी measure of central tendency (mean, median, या mode) से compute की गई series के absolute deviations का arithmetic mean है। Deviations को positive (signs को ignore करते हुए) लिया जाता है। हम आमतौर पर mean या median के आस-पास mean deviation compute करते हैं।

Coefficient of Mean Deviation

Coefficient of Mean Deviation

The coefficient of mean deviation is a relative measure used for comparing the variation among different series. It is obtained by dividing the mean deviation by the average used for its calculation.

Coefficient of mean deviation एक relative measure है जिसका उपयोग different series के बीच variation की तुलना करने के लिए किया जाता है। इसे इसके calculation के लिए उपयोग किए गए average से mean deviation को divide करके प्राप्त किया जाता है।

Coefficient of Mean Deviation = \(\frac{\text{Mean Deviation (M.D.)}}{\text{Mean or Median or Mode}}\)

Computation of Mean Deviation - Individual Series

Computation of Mean Deviation - Individual Series

Steps:

Steps:

  1. Calculate the average (mean, median, or mode) of the series.
  2. Find the absolute deviations of each item from the chosen average, denoted by \(|D|\).
  3. Compute the sum of these deviations, \(\sum |D|\).
  4. Divide the sum by the number of items, \(n\).

M.D. = \(\frac{\sum |D|}{n}\)

Merits and Demerits of Mean Deviation

Merits:

  • It is simple to understand and easy to compute.
  • It is rigidly defined.
  • It is based on all items of the series.
  • It is not much affected by the fluctuations of sampling.
  • It is less affected by extreme items.
  • It is flexible, as it can be calculated from any average.
  • It is a better measure of comparison than range and quartile deviation.

Demerits:

  • It is not a very accurate measure of dispersion.
  • It is not suitable for further mathematical calculation.
  • It is rarely used; it is not as popular as standard deviation.
  • Ignoring algebraic signs is mathematically illogical and unsound.

The most important measure of dispersion

Standard Deviation

Introduced by Karl Pearson in 1893, the standard deviation is the most important measure of dispersion and is widely used in statistical analysis. It is also known as the Root-Mean Square Deviation because it is the square root of the mean of the squared deviations from the arithmetic mean. Its square is called Variance.

Definition: Standard deviation (\(\sigma\)) is defined as the positive square root of the arithmetic mean of the squared deviations of the given observations from their arithmetic mean.

Additional Notes on Properties of Standard Deviations

A. Change of Origin

When we add a constant to all observations, the standard deviation remains unchanged.

Property:

If \(y_i = x_i + b\) (where \(b\) is a constant), then \(\sigma_y = \sigma_x\)

Proof:

Let \(y_i = x_i + b\) for all \(i = 1, 2, \dots, n\)

From the property of arithmetic mean, we know: \(\bar{y} = \bar{x} + b\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_y^2 &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}((x_i + b) - (\bar{x} + b))^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2\\ &= \sigma_x^2 \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, \(\sigma_y = \sigma_x\)

B. Change of Scale

When we multiply all observations by a constant, the standard deviation gets multiplied by the absolute value of that constant.

Property:

If \(y_i = ax_i\) (where \(a\) is a constant), then \(\sigma_y = |a|\sigma_x\)

Proof:

Let \(y_i = ax_i\) for all \(i = 1, 2, \dots, n\)

From the property of arithmetic mean, we know: \(\bar{y} = a\bar{x}\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_y^2 &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(ax_i - a\bar{x})^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}a^2(x_i - \bar{x})^2\\ &= a^2\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2\\ &= a^2\sigma_x^2 \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, \(\sigma_y = |a|\sigma_x\)

C. Relation Between \(\sigma\) and \(s\)

By expanding \(s^2\):

\[ s^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i (x_i - A)^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i \left[(x_i - \bar{x}) + (\bar{x} - A)\right]^2 \] \[ = \sigma^2 + (\bar{x} - A)^2 = \sigma^2 + d^2 \quad \text{where } d = \bar{x} - A \quad \]

Key Implications:

  • \(s^2\) is minimized when \(d = 0\) (i.e., \(A = \bar{x}\)), at which point \(s^2 = \sigma^2\).
  • Thus, standard deviation is the minimum possible root mean square deviation.
  • This aligns with the property that \(\sum f_i (x_i - A)^2\) is minimized when \(A = \bar{x}\).

D. Linear Transformation (Both Change of Scale and Origin)

When we apply a linear transformation to all observations, the standard deviation gets multiplied by the absolute value of the scaling factor.

Property:

If \(y_i = ax_i + b\) (where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants, \(a \neq 0\)), then \(\sigma_y = |a|\sigma_x\)

Proof:

Let \(y_i = ax_i + b\) for all \(i = 1, 2, \dots, n\)

From the property of arithmetic mean, we know: \(\bar{y} = a\bar{x} + b\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_y^2 &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}((ax_i + b) - (a\bar{x} + b))^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(ax_i - a\bar{x})^2\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}a^2(x_i - \bar{x})^2\\ &= a^2\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2\\ &= a^2\sigma_x^2 \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, \(\sigma_y = |a|\sigma_x\)

Example 1:

Suppose the marks obtained by 9 student in test are: 45, 32, 37, 46, 39, 36, 41, 48, 36

The mean is 40 marks and standard deviation is approximately 5.5 marks.

If we add 10 marks to each student (change of origin), the new data becomes: 55, 42, 47, 56, 49, 46, 51, 58, 46

The new mean will be 50 marks, but the standard deviation remains 5.5 marks.

If we multiply each mark by 2 (change of scale), the new data becomes: 90, 64, 74, 92, 78, 72, 82, 96, 72

The new mean will be 80 marks, and the standard deviation becomes \(2 \times 5.5 = 11\) marks.

Practical Use of Change of Origin and Scale

As these properties provide the basis for the "coding method" used to simplify calculations. For example, when computing moments from grouped data, we can use the transformation:

\[ u_i = \frac{x_i - a}{h} \]

Where:

  • \(a\) is an arbitrary origin (usually chosen to be a class midpoint)
  • \(h\) is the class width

This transformation simplifies calculations by working with smaller numbers, and the results can be transformed back using the properties established above.

3.9 Moments and its Properties

A. Raw Moments

\[ \text{The rth raw moment is defined as:} \quad \mu'_r = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i^r \]

Change of Origin and Scale:

If \(y_i = ax_i + b\), then:

Property for 1st Raw Moment:

\(\mu'_1(y) = a\mu'_1(x) + b\)

This is simply the property of the mean under linear transformation.

Property for Higher Raw Moments:

\[ \text{For } r \geq 2: \quad \mu'_r(y) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(ax_i + b)^r\\ = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{k=0}^{r}{r \choose k}(ax_i)^k b^{r-k} \]

This uses the binomial theorem to expand \((ax_i + b)^r\).

B. Central Moments

\[ \text{The rth central moment is defined as:} \quad \mu_r = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^r \]

Change of Origin and Scale:

If \(y_i = ax_i + b\), then:

Property for 2nd Central Moment (Variance):

\(\mu_2(y) = a^2\mu_2(x)\)

This follows from our standard deviation result, since \(\mu_2 = \sigma^2\).

Property for Higher Central Moments:

For \(r \geq 2\): \(\mu_r(y) = a^r\mu_r(x)\)

Proof:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mu_r(y) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^r\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}((ax_i + b) - (a\bar{x} + b))^r\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(ax_i - a\bar{x})^r\\ &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}a^r(x_i - \bar{x})^r\\ &= a^r\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^r\\ &= a^r\mu_r(x) \end{aligned} \]

C. Standardized Moments

The standardized moments like skewness (\(\beta_1\)) and kurtosis (\(\beta_2\)) are invariant under linear transformations.

Property for Skewness:

\(\beta_1(y) = \beta_1(x)\) where \(\beta_1 = \frac{\mu_3}{\mu_2^{3/2}}\)

Property for Kurtosis:

\(\beta_2(y) = \beta_2(x)\) where \(\beta_2 = \frac{\mu_4}{\mu_2^2}\)

Proof for Skewness:

\[ \begin{aligned} \beta_1(y) &= \frac{\mu_3(y)}{\mu_2(y)^{3/2}}\\ &= \frac{a^3\mu_3(x)}{(a^2\mu_2(x))^{3/2}}\\ &= \frac{a^3\mu_3(x)}{a^3\mu_2(x)^{3/2}}\\ &= \frac{\mu_3(x)}{\mu_2(x)^{3/2}}\\ &= \beta_1(x) \end{aligned} \]

Proof for Kurtosis:

\[ \begin{aligned} \beta_2(y) &= \frac{\mu_4(y)}{\mu_2(y)^2}\\ &= \frac{a^4\mu_4(x)}{(a^2\mu_2(x))^2}\\ &= \frac{a^4\mu_4(x)}{a^4\mu_2(x)^2}\\ &= \frac{\mu_4(x)}{\mu_2(x)^2}\\ &= \beta_2(x) \end{aligned} \]

  • Standard deviation = 15 marks
  • Skewness coefficient = 0.3 (slightly positively skewed)
  • Kurtosis = 2.8 (slightly platykurtic)

  • Kurtosis remains 2.8

D. Sheppard's Correction for Grouping Errors

D. Sheppard's Correction for Grouping Errors

When moments are calculated for a grouped frequency distribution, we usually assume that all the frequencies within a class interval are concentrated at the mid-point of that interval. This assumption is an approximation and introduces a grouping error into the calculated moments. The English mathematician W.F. Sheppard provided correction factors to adjust these moments, known as Sheppard's corrections.

जब grouped frequency distribution के लिए moments की गणना की जाती है, तो हम आमतौर पर यह मान लेते हैं कि एक class interval के भीतर सभी frequencies उस interval के mid-point पर केंद्रित (concentrated) होती हैं। यह धारणा एक approximation है और calculated moments में एक grouping error का परिचय देती है। English mathematician W.F. Sheppard ने इन moments को adjust करने के लिए correction factors प्रदान किए, जिन्हें Sheppard's corrections के रूप में जाना जाता है।

If \(h\) represents the uniform width of the class intervals, the corrected central moments are given by the following formulas:

यदि \(h\) class intervals की uniform width का प्रतिनिधित्व करता है, तो corrected central moments निम्नलिखित formulas द्वारा दिए जाते हैं:

\(\mu_2 (\text{corrected}) = \mu_2 (\text{uncorrected}) - \frac{h^2}{12}\)

\(\mu_3 (\text{corrected}) = \mu_3 (\text{uncorrected})\)

\(\mu_4 (\text{corrected}) = \mu_4 (\text{uncorrected}) - \frac{1}{2}h^2 \mu_2 (\text{uncorrected}) + \frac{7}{240}h^4\)

Note: These corrections are generally applied only when the distribution is continuous, \(N\) is reasonably large, and the distribution is moderately skewed (tapers off gradually in both directions). They should not be used for highly skewed distributions or discrete series.

Note: ये corrections आमतौर पर केवल तभी लागू की जाती हैं जब distribution continuous हो, \(N\) यथोचित रूप से बड़ा हो, और distribution moderately skewed हो (दोनों दिशाओं में धीरे-धीरे कम हो जाता है)। इनका उपयोग highly skewed distributions या discrete series के लिए नहीं किया जाना चाहिए।

Example: Applying Sheppard's Correction

Example: Applying Sheppard's Correction

For a frequency distribution with a class width of \(h = 10\), the uncorrected variance (\(\mu_2\)) is calculated as 141.07. Apply Sheppard's correction to find the corrected variance.

\(h = 10\) की class width वाले frequency distribution के लिए, uncorrected variance (\(\mu_2\)) की गणना 141.07 के रूप में की जाती है। Corrected variance ज्ञात करने के लिए Sheppard's correction लागू करें।

Solution:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mu_2 (\text{corrected}) &= \mu_2 (\text{uncorrected}) - \frac{h^2}{12} \\ &= 141.07 - \frac{10^2}{12} \\ &= 141.07 - \frac{100}{12} \\ &= 141.07 - 8.333 \\ &\approx 132.737 \end{aligned} \]

E. Charlier's Checks for Accuracy

E. Charlier's Checks for Accuracy

Calculating moments, especially higher-order moments, involves extensive arithmetic which is prone to errors. To verify the accuracy of the sums of products (like \(\sum fd, \sum fd^2, \sum fd^3, \text{ and } \sum fd^4\)), C.V.L. Charlier developed a set of mathematical identities known as Charlier's Checks.

Moments, विशेष रूप से higher-order moments की गणना में व्यापक अंकगणित (arithmetic) शामिल है जिसमें त्रुटियों (errors) की संभावना होती है। Products के sums (\(\sum fd, \sum fd^2, \sum fd^3, \text{ और } \sum fd^4\)) की सटीकता को verify करने के लिए, C.V.L. Charlier ने mathematical identities का एक set विकसित किया जिसे Charlier's Checks के रूप में जाना जाता है।

If \(d_i\) represents the deviations, the checks are based on expanding \((d+1)^k\) and summing over frequencies:

यदि \(d_i\) deviations का प्रतिनिधित्व करता है, तो checks \((d+1)^k\) को expand करने और frequencies पर sum करने पर आधारित हैं:

1st Check: \(\sum f(d+1) = \sum fd + \sum f = \sum fd + N\)

2nd Check: \(\sum f(d+1)^2 = \sum fd^2 + 2\sum fd + N\)

3rd Check: \(\sum f(d+1)^3 = \sum fd^3 + 3\sum fd^2 + 3\sum fd + N\)

4th Check: \(\sum f(d+1)^4 = \sum fd^4 + 4\sum fd^3 + 6\sum fd^2 + 4\sum fd + N\)

By computing an additional column for \((d+1)\) up to the required power and its product with frequency, one can check if the identities hold true. If both sides match, the arithmetic sums are highly likely to be correct.

3.10 Different Formulas for Calculating Variance

Variance, denoted as \(\sigma_x^2\), quantifies the average squared deviation from the mean. The definitional formula is:

\[ \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^n f_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \quad \text{where } N = \sum f_i \quad \]

When \(\bar{x}\) is fractional or the values are large, direct computation is cumbersome. Alternative forms simplify the arithmetic.

Raw Moments Form

Expanding the definitional formula:

\[ \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i x_i^2 - \bar{x}^2 \quad \]

Equivalently:

\[ \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i x_i^2 - \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i x_i \right)^2 \quad \]

Shifted Deviations Form

For large \(x_i\) or \(f_i\), compute deviations from an arbitrary point \(A\) (often near the middle of the distribution), where \(d_i = x_i - A\):

\[ \bar{x} = A + \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i d_i = A + \bar{d} \] \[ \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i (d_i - \bar{d})^2 \]

Expanding:

\[ \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i d_i^2 - \bar{d}^2 \quad \]

This is equivalent to the variance of the \(d_i\) values (\(\sigma_d^2\)), since variance is invariant under translation (\(\sigma_x^2 = \sigma_d^2\)).

This form reduces computation by working with smaller \(d_i\) values.

Variance and Standard Deviation: Effect of Change of Origin and Scale

Variance and, consequently, standard deviation exhibit specific behaviors when data undergoes a change of origin (shifting the data) or a change of scale (scaling the data).

Properties of Variance Under Transformation

If a new variable \( d_i \) is defined as \( d_i = \frac{x_i - A}{h} \), where \( A \) is the assumed origin and \( h \) is the common scale factor (class width), then:

We know that \( x_i - A = h d_i \). Also, the mean of \( x \) can be expressed as \( \bar{x} = A + h \bar{d} \), where \( \bar{d} = \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i d_i \).

The deviation of \( x_i \) from its mean \( \bar{x} \) can be written as:

\[ x_i - \bar{x} = (x_i - A) - (\bar{x} - A) = h d_i - h \bar{d} = h (d_i - \bar{d}) \]

Now, substituting this into the variance formula for \( x \):

\[ \sigma_x^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^n f_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \] \[ = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^n f_i [h (d_i - \bar{d})]^2 \] \[ = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^n f_i h^2 (d_i - \bar{d})^2 \]

Factoring out \( h^2 \) (since it's a constant):

\[ \sigma_x^2 = h^2 \left[ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^n f_i (d_i - \bar{d})^2 \right] \]

The term in the square brackets is the variance of the \( d \) values, denoted as \( \sigma_d^2 \). Thus, we get:

\[ \sigma_x^2 = h^2 \sigma_d^2 \quad \]

This relationship demonstrates that:

  • Variance is independent of change of origin (\(A\)): The term \(A\) does not appear in the final formula for \( \sigma_x^2 \) directly, only through its effect on \(d_i\) and subsequently \(\bar{d}\). The variance of the transformed variable \(d\) is unaffected by the choice of origin \(A\).
  • Variance is NOT independent of change of scale (\(h\)): The variance of \( x \) is \( h^2 \) times the variance of \( d \). This means scaling the data by \( h \) multiplies the variance by \( h^2 \) and the standard deviation by \( |h| \).

Example: Relationship Between Standard Deviation and Mean Deviation

Problem: Prove that for any discrete distribution, the standard deviation is not less than the mean deviation from the mean.

Contextual Relevance: This fundamental statistical relationship is crucial when comparing different measures of dispersion, such as in analyzing the variability of crop yields across various regions in India, or the consistency of milk production in dairy farms in Haryana. Understanding that standard deviation is always at least as large as mean deviation helps in interpreting the spread of agricultural data, especially when considering the impact of outliers.

Solution:
Let \(x_i\) be the values of a discrete distribution with corresponding frequencies \(f_i\), for \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, n\). Let \(N = \sum f_i\) be the total frequency. We need to prove that:

\[ \text{S.D.} \ge \text{Mean Deviation from Mean (M.D.)} \]

Squaring both sides, we aim to prove:

\[ (\text{S.D.})^2 \ge (\text{M.D. from mean})^2 \]

In terms of formulas:

\[ \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \ge \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i |x_i - \bar{x}| \right)^2 \]

Let \(z_i = |x_i - \bar{x}|\). Then we need to prove:

\[ \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i z_i^2 \ge \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i z_i \right)^2 \]

Rearranging the inequality, this is equivalent to proving:

\[ \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i z_i^2 - \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i z_i \right)^2 \ge 0 \]

This expression is precisely the formula for the variance of the variable \(z\), i.e., \( \sigma_z^2 \).

\[ \frac{1}{N} \sum f_i (z_i - \bar{z})^2 \ge 0 \]

Since the variance (or sum of squared deviations) can never be negative, this inequality is always true. Hence, the result is proven: standard deviation is always greater than or equal to the mean deviation from the mean.

Example: Mean Deviation and Standard Deviation for an Arithmetic Progression

Problem: Find the mean deviation from the mean and the standard deviation for a series in Arithmetic Progression (A.P.): \(a, a+d, a+2d, \ldots, a+2nd\). Verify that the standard deviation is greater than the mean deviation.

Contextual Relevance: Consider the yields of a new variety of fodder crop from \(2n+1\) experimental plots in Haryana, where the yields increase consistently with an additional input (e.g., specific fertilizer dose) in each successive plot. Here, \(a\) represents the base yield (e.g., from the control plot), and \(d\) represents the consistent increase in yield per unit of additional input. Analyzing such data helps agricultural scientists understand the variability and central tendency of yields under specific experimental conditions.

Solution:
The given series is an A.P. with \( (2n+1) \) terms. The terms are \(a, a+d, a+2d, \ldots, a+2nd\). Each term has a frequency of 1. The mean of an A.P. series is the mean of its first and last term:

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{1}{2} (a + (a+2nd)) = \frac{1}{2} (2a + 2nd) = a + nd \]

Now, we calculate \(|x - \bar{x}|\) and \((x - \bar{x})^2\) for each term:

\(x\) \(|x - \bar{x}|\) \((x - \bar{x})^2\)
\(a\) \(|a - (a+nd)| = nd\) \((nd)^2 = n^2d^2\)
\(a+d\) \(|(a+d) - (a+nd)| = |(1-n)d|\) (or \((n-1)d\)) \((1-n)^2d^2 = (n-1)^2d^2\)
\(a+2d\) \(|(a+2d) - (a+nd)| = |(2-n)d|\) (or \((n-2)d\)) \((2-n)^2d^2 = (n-2)^2d^2\)
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
\(a+(n-2)d\) \(|(a+(n-2)d) - (a+nd)| = |-2d| = 2d\) \((-2d)^2 = 4d^2\)
\(a+(n-1)d\) \(|(a+(n-1)d) - (a+nd)| = |-d| = d\) \((-d)^2 = d^2\)
\(a+nd\) \(|(a+nd) - (a+nd)| = 0\) \(0^2 = 0\)
\(a+(n+1)d\) \(|(a+(n+1)d) - (a+nd)| = |d| = d\) \(d^2\)
\(a+(n+2)d\) \(|(a+(n+2)d) - (a+nd)| = |2d| = 2d\) \((2d)^2 = 4d^2\)
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
\(a+2nd\) \(|(a+2nd) - (a+nd)| = |nd| = nd\) \((nd)^2 = n^2d^2\)

The total number of terms is \(N = 2n+1\).

Calculation of Mean Deviation (M.D.) from the Mean:

\[ \sum |x - \bar{x}| = 2 \sum_{k=1}^n kd = 2d \sum_{k=1}^n k = 2d \frac{n(n+1)}{2} = dn(n+1) \]

Mean Deviation \(= \frac{\sum |x - \bar{x}|}{N} = \frac{dn(n+1)}{2n+1}\)

Calculation of Standard Deviation (S.D.):

\[ \sum (x - \bar{x})^2 = 2 \sum_{k=1}^n (kd)^2 = 2d^2 \sum_{k=1}^n k^2 = 2d^2 \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} = d^2 \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3} \]

\[ \text{Variance} (\sigma^2) = \frac{\sum (x - \bar{x})^2}{N} = \frac{d^2 \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3}}{2n+1} = \frac{d^2 n(n+1)}{3}\]

\[ \therefore \text{Standard Deviation} (\sigma) = \sqrt{\frac{d^2 n(n+1)}{3}} = |d| \sqrt{\frac{n(n+1)}{3}}\]

Verification: S.D. \( \ge \) M.D.

We need to check if \( |d| \sqrt{\frac{n(n+1)}{3}} \ge \frac{dn(n+1)}{2n+1} \).

Assuming \(d > 0\), we compare \( \sqrt{\frac{n(n+1)}{3}} \) with \( \frac{n(n+1)}{2n+1} \).

Squaring both sides (which is valid for positive numbers):

Compare \( \frac{n(n+1)}{3} \) with \( \left( \frac{n(n+1)}{2n+1} \right)^2 \). Compare \( \frac{1}{3} \) with \( \frac{n(n+1)}{(2n+1)^2} \).

Consider the ratio: \( \frac{n(n+1)}{(2n+1)^2} \times 3 = \frac{3n^2 + 3n}{4n^2 + 4n + 1} \). For \(n=1\), the ratio is \( \frac{3(1)^2 + 3(1)}{4(1)^2 + 4(1) + 1} = \frac{6}{9} = \frac{2}{3} \). Since \( \frac{2}{3} < 1 \), it means \( \frac{n(n+1)}{(2n+1)^2} < \frac{1}{3} \) for small \(n\). Let's re-evaluate the direct comparison: We want to show \( \frac{1}{3} \ge \frac{n(n+1)}{(2n+1)^2} \) (this inequality seems to be inverted from the original proof's expectation if S.D. > M.D. is true).

Let's use the established result \( \sigma_z^2 \ge 0 \) from Example 3.2. For a discrete series with unit frequencies, S.D. \( = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N}} \) M.D. \( = \frac{\sum |x_i - \bar{x}|}{N} \) Let \( |x_i - \bar{x}| = y_i \). We need to prove \( \sqrt{\frac{\sum y_i^2}{N}} \ge \frac{\sum y_i}{N} \). This is equivalent to proving \( \frac{\sum y_i^2}{N} \ge \left( \frac{\sum y_i}{N} \right)^2 \), which is \( \text{Variance}(y) \ge 0 \), which is always true.

Therefore, for this specific A.P. series, the calculated standard deviation \( |d| \sqrt{\frac{n(n+1)}{3}} \) will always be greater than or equal to the mean deviation \( \frac{dn(n+1)}{2n+1} \), verifying the general statistical principle.

Theorem: Variance of the Combined Series

Statement of the Theorem:
If there are two series or groups of data, with sizes \(n_1\) and \(n_2\), means \(\bar{x}_1\) and \(\bar{x}_2\), and standard deviations \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\) respectively, then the variance (\(\sigma^2\)) of the combined series of total size \(N = n_1 + n_2\) is given by:

\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n_1 + n_2} [n_1 (\sigma_1^2 + d_1^2) + n_2 (\sigma_2^2 + d_2^2)] \quad \]

where:

  • \(d_1 = \bar{x}_1 - \bar{X}\) (deviation of the first group's mean from the combined mean)
  • \(d_2 = \bar{x}_2 - \bar{X}\) (deviation of the second group's mean from the combined mean)
  • \(\bar{X} = \frac{n_1 \bar{x}_1 + n_2 \bar{x}_2}{n_1 + n_2}\) (the mean of the combined series)

Proof Overview:
The proof begins by defining the individual means and variances for each series. The mean of the combined series (\(\bar{X}\)) is then established as the weighted average of the individual means. The total variance of the combined series (\(\sigma^2\)) is derived from the sum of squared deviations of all observations from the combined mean. By expanding \( (x_i - \bar{X})^2 \) for each group using \( (x_i - \bar{x}_j + \bar{x}_j - \bar{X})^2 \), and knowing that the sum of deviations from a group's own mean is zero (\(\sum f_i (x_i - \bar{x}_j) = 0\)), the expression for \(\sigma^2\) can be simplified to the stated formula .

Simplified Formula and Generalization as given above :
The formula can be further simplified to:

\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n_1+n_2} \left[ n_1 \sigma_1^2 + n_2 \sigma_2^2 + \frac{n_1 n_2 (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2)^2}{n_1 + n_2} \right] \quad \]

This formula is often more convenient as it directly uses the difference between the group means. For \(k\) component series, the variance of the combined series is generalized as:

\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^k n_i (\sigma_i^2 + d_i^2) \right] \quad \]

where \(N = \sum n_i\) and \(d_i = \bar{x}_i - \bar{X}\).

Example: Combining Data from Two Agricultural Regions

Problem: The first sample of 100 farms in a specific region of Haryana shows an average wheat yield of 15 quintals/acre with a standard deviation of 3 quintals/acre. If a whole group of 250 farms, including these 100, has a combined average yield of 15.6 quintals/acre and a combined standard deviation of \( \sqrt{13.44} \) quintals/acre, find the standard deviation of the second group of farms (the remaining 150 farms).

Solution:
Let's denote the first group (100 farms) as Group 1 and the second group (the remaining 150 farms) as Group 2. The combined group is the total of 250 farms.

Given for Group 1:

Size, \(n_1 = 100\)

Mean yield, \(\bar{x}_1 = 15\) quintals/acre

Standard deviation, \(\sigma_1 = 3\) quintals/acre, so variance \(\sigma_1^2 = 3^2 = 9\)

Given for the Combined Group:

Total size, \(N = 250\)

Combined mean yield, \(\bar{X} = 15.6\) quintals/acre

Combined standard deviation, \(\sigma = \sqrt{13.44}\) quintals/acre, so combined variance \(\sigma^2 = 13.44\)

We need to find the standard deviation of Group 2, \(\sigma_2\).

First, find the size of Group 2:
\(n_2 = N - n_1 = 250 - 100 = 150\) farms

Next, find the mean of Group 2, \(\bar{x}_2\), using the formula for the combined mean:

\[ \bar{X} = \frac{n_1 \bar{x}_1 + n_2 \bar{x}_2}{n_1 + n_2} \] \[ 15.6 = \frac{(100)(15) + (150)\bar{x}_2}{250} \] \[ 15.6 \times 250 = 1500 + 150 \bar{x}_2 \] \[ 3900 = 1500 + 150 \bar{x}_2 \] \[ 2400 = 150 \bar{x}_2 \] \[ \bar{x}_2 = \frac{2400}{150} = 16 \text{ quintals/acre} \]

So, the standard deviation of the second group is:

\[ \sigma_2 = \sqrt{16} = 4 \text{ quintals/acre} \]

Example: Correcting Mean and Standard Deviation of Crop Yield Data

Problem: For a data set of 200 rice fields in a district of India, the mean yield was calculated as 40 quintals/hectare and the standard deviation as 15 quintals/hectare. Later, it was discovered that two yield figures, 43 and 53 quintals/hectare, were misread as 34 and 35 quintals/hectare respectively. Find the corrected mean and standard deviation corresponding to the corrected figures.

Let \(x\) be the variable representing rice yield.

Number of fields, \(n = 200\)

Misread values:

Incorrect values (to be removed): 34, 35

Correct values (to be added): 43, 53

From the definition of mean, \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n}\), so \(\sum x = n \bar{x}\).

\[ \sum x_{inc} = n \times \bar{x}_{inc} = 200 \times 40 = 8000 \text{ quintals} \]

2. Calculate the Incorrect Sum of Squares of Observations (\(\sum x_{inc}^2\)):
From the variance formula, \(\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum x^2}{n} - \bar{x}^2\), so \(\sum x^2 = n (\sigma^2 + \bar{x}^2)\).

\[ \sigma_{inc}^2 = 15^2 = 225 \] \[ \sum x_{inc}^2 = n (\sigma_{inc}^2 + \bar{x}_{inc}^2) = 200 (225 + 40^2) = 200 (225 + 1600) = 200 (1825) = 365000 \]

3. Calculate the Corrected Sum of Observations (\(\sum x_{corr}\)):
To correct the sum, subtract the incorrect values and add the correct values:

\[ \sum x_{corr} = \sum x_{inc} - (\text{sum of incorrect values}) + (\text{sum of correct values}) \] \[ \sum x_{corr} = 8000 - (34 + 35) + (43 + 53) \] \[ \sum x_{corr} = 8000 - 69 + 96 \] \[ \sum x_{corr} = 7931 \text{ quintals} \]

\[ \sum x_{corr}^2 = \sum x_{inc}^2 - (34^2 + 35^2) + (43^2 + 53^2) \] \[ \sum x_{corr}^2 = 365000 - (1156 + 1225) + (1849 + 2809) \] \[ \sum x_{corr}^2 = 365000 - 2381 + 4658 \] \[ \sum x_{corr}^2 = 367277 \]

5. Calculate the Corrected Mean (\(\bar{x}_{corr}\)):
The number of observations \(n\) remains the same (200).

\[ \bar{x}_{corr} = \frac{\sum x_{corr}}{n} = \frac{7931}{200} = 39.655 \text{ quintals/hectare} \]

6. Calculate the Corrected Variance (\(\sigma_{corr}^2\)) and Standard Deviation (\(\sigma_{corr}\)):

\[ \sigma_{corr}^2 = \frac{\sum x_{corr}^2}{n} - \bar{x}_{corr}^2 \] \[ \sigma_{corr}^2 = 1836.385 - 1572.515025 \] \[ \sigma_{corr}^2 = 263.869975 \] \[ \sigma_{corr} = \sqrt{263.869975} \approx 16.244 \text{ quintals/hectare} \]

Coefficient of Dispersion and Coefficient of Variation

Whenever we want to compare the variability of two series which differ widely in their averages or which are measured in different units, we do not merely calculate the measures of dispersion. Instead, we calculate the coefficient of dispersion, which are pure numbers independent of the units of measurement. The coefficients of dispersion (C.D.) based on different measures are as follows:

  1. Based upon Range: \( C.D. = \frac{A - B}{A + B} \), where \(A\) and \(B\) are the greatest and the smallest items in the series.
  2. Based upon Quartile Deviation: \( C.D. = \frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{Q_3 + Q_1} \)
  3. Based upon Mean Deviation: \( C.D. = \frac{\text{Average from Mean Deviation}}{\text{Mean}} \)
  4. Based upon Standard Deviation: \( C.D. = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}} \)

Coefficient of Variation. 100 times the coefficient of dispersion based upon standard deviation is called co-efficient of variation (C.V.),

\[ C.V. = 100 \times \frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}} \quad \]

According to Professor Karl Pearson who suggested this measure, C.V. is the percentage variation in the mean, standard deviation being considered as the total variation in the mean.

For comparing the variability of two series, we calculate the coefficient of variation for each series. The series having greater C.V. is said to be more variable than the other and the series having lesser C.V. is said to be more consistent.

This we see that the firm B has larger wage bill.

(i) Variance of distribution of wages in firm A (say) \(\sigma_1^2 = 81\)
C.V. of distribution of wages in firm A = 100 × \(\frac{\sigma_1}{\bar{x}_1} = 100 \times \frac{9}{186} = 4.84\)

C.V. of distribution of wages in firm B = 100 × \(\frac{\sigma_2}{\bar{x}_2} = 100 \times \frac{10}{175} = 5.71\)

Since C.V. for firm B is greater than C.V. for firm A, firm B has greater variability in individual wages.

(ii) The average monthly wage (say) \(\bar{x}\), of firm the workers in the two cooperatives taken together:
Total no. of farmers \(n = n_1 + n_2 = 500 + 600 = 1,100\)
Total wages paid in the given month = \text{₹} 93,000 + \text{₹} 105,000 = ₹ 198,000
\(\bar{x} = \frac{198,000}{1,100} = \text{₹} 180\)

(b) The combined variance \(\sigma^2\) of the formula:

\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{n_1 (\sigma_1^2 + d_1^2) + n_2 (\sigma_2^2 + d_2^2)}{n_1 + n_2} \]

where \(d_1 = \bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}\), \(d_2 = \bar{x}_2 - \bar{x}\).

Here \(d_1 = 186 - 180 = 6\), \(d_2 = 175 - 180 = -5\)

\(\sigma_1^2 = 81\), \(\sigma_2^2 = 100\)

\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{500 (81 + 36) + 600 (100 + 25)}{1,100} = \frac{500 \times 117 + 600 \times 125}{1,100} = \frac{58,500 + 75,000}{1,100} = \frac{133,500}{1,100} = 121.36 \]

Hence, the combined standard deviation \(\sigma = \sqrt{121.36} \approx 11.02\) ₹.
