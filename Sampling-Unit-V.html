5.1 Concept & Pattern (The "Every \(k^{th}\)" Rule)

Hello students! In the previous units, we learned about Simple Random Sampling (picking blindly) and Stratified Sampling (grouping and then picking). Both methods require you to pick every single unit in the sample using random numbers. If your sample size is 1,000, you have to use the random number table 1,000 times! This is extremely tiring and slow in the real world.

To make field work drastically faster and easier, statisticians invented a brilliant shortcut: Systematic Random Sampling. In this method, you only use the random number table once to pick the very first unit. After that, you just march forward in a fixed, mechanical pattern, picking every \(k^{th}\) unit you see.

Linear Systematic Sampling

Suppose a population of size \(N\) units is numbered serially from 1 to \(N\). We want to draw a sample of size \(n\). We assume \(N = n \times k\), meaning \(N\) is a perfect multiple of \(n\).

We calculate the Sampling Interval, \(k = N/n\). We then select a random start number, let's call it \(r\), anywhere between 1 and \(k\). The units selected for our final sample will be:

\[ r,\; r+k,\; r+2k,\; r+3k,\; \dots,\; r+(n-1)k \]

Because only the first unit \(r\) is chosen randomly, this method is called a Systematic Random Sample.

Real-Life Examples of Systematic Sampling

  • Factory Quality Control: An inspector stands at a conveyor belt of Pepsi bottles. She wants to check 100 bottles out of 10,000. The interval \(k = 10000/100 = 100\). She picks a random start, say 42. She then checks the 42nd bottle, the 142nd, the 242nd, and so on.
  • Office Audits: A manager wants to check 50 files out of a cabinet of 500. Interval \(k = 10\). Random start is 7. He checks file 7, 17, 27, 37...
  • Forest Surveys: A scientist walking along a straight forest path measures the height of a tree every 50 meters exactly.

Implicit Stratification

Why is Systematic Sampling so good? Imagine your population list is arranged in a line. The sampling interval \(k\) naturally chops this line into exactly \(n\) equal blocks (or implicit strata) of size \(k\). By picking a random start \(r\) and jumping by \(k\), you are guaranteed to pick exactly one unit from every single block. You spread your sample perfectly and evenly across the entire population, preventing all your samples from accidentally bunching up in one corner (which can happen in pure SRS!).

5.2 Mathematical Estimation & Theorems

Now let's prove that this "shortcut" method is actually scientifically valid. First, we must organize our notation. Because the population is naturally chopped into \(k\) possible samples (depending on which random start \(r\) you pick), we can arrange the population data into a grid.

Notation Matrix

Let \(y_{ij}\) be the value of the \(j^{th}\) unit in the \(i^{th}\) possible systematic sample.

\(i = 1, 2, \dots, k\) (These are the possible random starts).

\(j = 1, 2, \dots, n\) (These are the elements within a specific sample).

  • Sample 1 (\(r=1\)): \(y_{11}, y_{12}, \dots, y_{1n}\) \(\rightarrow\) Mean = \(\bar{y}_{1.}\)
  • Sample 2 (\(r=2\)): \(y_{21}, y_{22}, \dots, y_{2n}\) \(\rightarrow\) Mean = \(\bar{y}_{2.}\)
  • ...
  • Sample k (\(r=k\)): \(y_{k1}, y_{k2}, \dots, y_{kn}\) \(\rightarrow\) Mean = \(\bar{y}_{k.}\)

The true Population Mean is the average of all these \(N = nk\) units: \(\bar{Y} = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^k \bar{y}_{i.}\)

Theorem 5.1: Unbiasedness of Systematic Mean

If \(N = nk\), the systematic sample mean (\(\bar{y}_{sys}\)) is an exactly unbiased estimator of the population mean (\(\bar{Y}\)). Mathematically: \(E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \bar{Y}\).

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 5.1

In linear systematic sampling, the entire sample is completely determined the moment we pick the random start \(r\). The random start \(r\) can be any number from 1 to \(k\), with equal probability. Therefore, the probability of selecting the \(i^{th}\) sample is exactly \(1/k\).

The expected value of our systematic sample mean is the sum of all possible sample means, multiplied by their probability of being chosen:

\[ E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \sum_{i=1}^k \bar{y}_{i.} \times P(\text{selecting sample } i) \]

\[ E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \sum_{i=1}^k \bar{y}_{i.} \times \left( \frac{1}{k} \right) \]

\[ E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^k \bar{y}_{i.} \]

By definition (look at our Notation Matrix above), the average of all the \(k\) possible sample means is exactly the grand Population Mean \(\bar{Y}\).

\[ E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \bar{Y} \]

This simple proof guarantees that our "lazy" systematic shortcut is perfectly, mathematically unbiased!

Theorem 5.2: Variance of Systematic Mean

The variance of the systematic sample mean is the variance of the \(k\) possible sample means around the true population mean:

\[ V(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^k (\bar{y}_{i.} - \bar{Y})^2 \]

Notice that this variance depends entirely on how much the means of the \(k\) specific sample columns differ from the grand mean. If all \(k\) columns have means very close to \(\bar{Y}\), the variance is tiny.

Solved Example 1: Systematic Sampling Calculation

Problem: A class has \(N=15\) students with the following marks: 45, 60, 55, 70, 80, 50, 65, 90, 85, 40, 75, 60, 95, 55, 80. We need a systematic sample of size \(n=5\).
(i) Determine the sampling interval \(k\).
(ii) If the random start is \(r=2\), list the sample units and calculate the sample mean \(\bar{y}_{sys}\).
(iii) Estimate the total marks of the class from this sample.

Solution:
(i) Interval \(k\):
\(k = N / n = 15 / 5 =\) 3.

(ii) Sample Selection & Mean:
Random start \(r=2\). The sequence formula is \(r, r+k, r+2k, r+3k, r+4k\).
Positions: 2, \(2+3=5\), \(5+3=8\), \(8+3=11\), \(11+3=14\).
We select the 2nd, 5th, 8th, 11th, and 14th students.
Their marks are: \(y_1 = 60\), \(y_2 = 80\), \(y_3 = 90\), \(y_4 = 75\), \(y_5 = 55\).
Sample Mean \(\bar{y}_{sys} = (60+80+90+75+55) / 5 = 360 / 5 =\) 72 marks.

(iii) Estimating Population Total:
Estimated Total \(\hat{Y} = N \times \bar{y}_{sys} = 15 \times 72 =\) 1080 marks.
(For fun, the true total is exactly 1005. Our sample estimate of 1080 is quite close for such a small sample!)

5.3 Circular Systematic Sampling

Linear Systematic Sampling is brilliant, but it has a massive, fatal flaw. It only works perfectly if the population size \(N\) is an exact, clean multiple of the sample size \(n\) (i.e., \(N = nk\)).

What if it doesn't divide cleanly? Suppose \(N = 11\) and you want \(n = 4\). \(11/4 = 2.75\). What is your interval \(k\)? If you round down to \(k=2\), and pick a random start \(r=2\), your sample sequence is 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12... But wait, there is no unit 12! The sample size changes depending on your random start. This violates the rule that sample size must be fixed.

To solve this integer problem, statisticians invented Circular Systematic Sampling.

The Circular Method

  1. Calculate \(k\) as the nearest integer to \(N/n\).
  2. Imagine the population is arranged in a circle, so unit number 1 immediately follows unit number \(N\).
  3. Pick a random start \(r\) from anywhere between 1 to N (not just 1 to \(k\)).
  4. Select every \(k^{th}\) item around the circle until you have exactly \(n\) items.
Mathematical Rule for the \(j^{th}\) selected unit:
If \(r + (j-1)k \le N \implies\) select unit \(r + (j-1)k\)
If \(r + (j-1)k > N \implies\) select unit \(r + (j-1)k - N\)

This guarantees that every single possible sample will have exactly \(n\) units, and every unit in the population has a perfectly equal probability (\(n/N\)) of being selected, even if \(N \neq nk\).

Solved Example 2: Circular Systematic Execution

Problem: A population consists of \(N=11\) households. A researcher needs a sample of \(n=4\). Using Circular Systematic Sampling, determine the interval \(k\) and list the exact units selected if the random start drawn is \(r=8\).

Solution:
Step 1: Find interval \(k\).
\(N/n = 11/4 = 2.75\). The nearest integer is 3. So, \(k=3\).

Step 2: Trace the circle starting at \(r=8\).
- 1st unit: start at 8.
- 2nd unit: jump 3 spaces. \(8 + 3 = 11\). Select 11.
- 3rd unit: jump 3 spaces. \(11 + 3 = 14\). Because 14 is greater than \(N\) (11), we wrap around the circle. \(14 - 11 = 3\). Select 3.
- 4th unit: jump 3 spaces from 3. \(3 + 3 = 6\). Select 6.

We now have our required \(n=4\) units.
Final Sample: Households 8, 11, 3, and 6.
Notice how we seamlessly "wrapped around" the end of the list back to the beginning to complete our sample without breaking any mathematical rules!

5.4 Efficiency & Comparison (Systematic vs. SRS)

Is Systematic Sampling better than Simple Random Sampling? It depends entirely on the hidden pattern (or lack of pattern) in the population data.

The Fatal Danger of Periodic Trends

Systematic sampling fails miserably if the population has a hidden cycle or "period" that matches the sampling interval \(k\).
Example: Suppose you want to estimate the average daily temperature. You take a measurement every 24 hours (interval \(k = 24\)). Your random start is 2:00 PM. Your sample will be: Day 1 at 2 PM, Day 2 at 2 PM, Day 3 at 2 PM... Your average will be massively biased (too high) because you are only measuring at the hottest time of day and completely ignoring the cold nights!

To mathematically prove when Systematic Sampling is better than SRS, we use the Intra-class Correlation Coefficient (\(\rho\) or rho). It measures how similar the units inside a specific systematic sample are to each other.

Theorem 5.3: Efficiency Condition

Systematic sampling is more precise (has lower variance) than Simple Random Sampling if and only if the intra-class correlation coefficient is negative (\(\rho < 0\)).

\[ V(\bar{y}_{sys}) \approx \frac{S^2}{n} [1 + (n-1)\rho] \]

Logic: If \(\rho\) is negative, the term \((n-1)\rho\) becomes negative, meaning we subtract it from 1. This makes the bracketed term less than 1, driving \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\) lower than the standard SRS variance \(\frac{S^2}{n}\). A negative \(\rho\) means units inside a sample are highly heterogeneous (different from each other), which is exactly what we want in a good sample!

Merits and Demerits Summary

Merits of Systematic Sampling Demerits of Systematic Sampling
Extremely simple, fast, and easy to execute in the field. Highly dangerous if hidden periodic trends exist in the data.
Less time-consuming and highly economical. Requires a complete, physically ordered sampling frame.
Usually more precise than SRS because it spreads the sample evenly across the whole population. It is mathematically impossible to calculate an unbiased estimate of the sampling variance from a single systematic sample.

Solved Example 3: Intra-Class Correlation Impact

Problem: In a large survey, the sample size is \(n=50\). The population variance is \(S^2 = 200\). The intra-class correlation coefficient between units in the systematic samples is found to be \(\rho = -0.015\). Calculate the variance under SRS and under Systematic Sampling. Which is better?

Solution:
1. Variance under SRS:
\(V_{srs} \approx \frac{S^2}{n} = \frac{200}{50} =\) 4.0

2. Variance under Systematic Sampling:
Formula: \(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) \approx \frac{S^2}{n} [1 + (n-1)\rho]\)
\(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) \approx 4.0 \times [1 + (50-1)(-0.015)]\)
\(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) \approx 4.0 \times [1 + (49)(-0.015)]\)
\(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) \approx 4.0 \times [1 - 0.735]\)
\(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) \approx 4.0 \times 0.265 =\) 1.06

Conclusion: \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\) (1.06) is massively lower than \(V_{srs}\) (4.0). Because \(\rho\) was negative, systematic sampling is significantly more efficient and accurate in this scenario!

5.5 Practice Section: Subjective Questions (20)

Test your deep conceptual understanding and mathematical derivation skills. Formulate your answers before checking the detailed solutions.

Q1. Define Linear Systematic Sampling. Why is it often called an "Every \(k^{th}\)" rule?
Solution: Linear Systematic Sampling is a probability sampling method where the first unit is selected randomly from the first \(k\) units (where \(k = N/n\) is the sampling interval), and every subsequent unit is selected by adding \(k\) to the previous unit's serial number. It is called the "Every \(k^{th}\)" rule because after the random start \(r\), the researcher simply mechanically picks every \(k^{th}\) item in the population list or physical sequence (e.g., picking every 10th file).
Q2. Explain the concept of 'Implicit Stratification' in the context of Systematic Sampling.
Solution: Systematic sampling acts like stratified sampling without explicitly creating strata. If the population is ordered in a line, the interval \(k\) naturally chops the population into \(n\) equal-sized blocks of size \(k\). By picking a random start \(r\) and jumping by \(k\), the method guarantees that exactly one unit is drawn from every single block. This evenly spreads the sample across the whole population, mimicking the representativeness of a highly stratified design.
Q3. Provide a real-world example where Systematic Sampling would be highly efficient, and one where it would fail disastrously.
Solution: Highly Efficient: Inspecting quality control on a factory conveyor belt. Checking every 500th bottle is incredibly easy to execute and provides a perfectly spread sample of the day's production.
Disastrous Failure: Estimating daily sales at a supermarket using a sampling interval of \(k=7\) days. If the random start is a Sunday, the sample will consist entirely of Sunday sales (which are usually huge), completely missing the low sales of weekdays, resulting in massive over-estimation.
Q4. State Theorem 5.1 and briefly explain the logic of its proof regarding the expected value of \(\bar{y}_{sys}\).
Solution: Theorem 5.1 states that if \(N=nk\), the systematic sample mean \(\bar{y}_{sys}\) is an exactly unbiased estimator of the population mean \(\bar{Y}\), meaning \(E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \bar{Y}\). The logic is that there are only \(k\) possible systematic samples. Because the random start \(r\) is picked from 1 to \(k\) with equal probability, the probability of selecting any specific sample is exactly \(1/k\). The expected value is the sum of the \(k\) sample means multiplied by \(1/k\), which is simply the unweighted average of all units in the population, i.e., the true population mean.
Q5. Why does Linear Systematic Sampling fail if the population size \(N\) is not a clean multiple of the sample size \(n\) (\(N \neq nk\))?
Solution: If \(N\) is not a multiple of \(n\), the interval \(k = N/n\) is a fraction. If we round it to an integer, say we round down, we might reach the required sample size \(n\) before hitting the end of the population list. Conversely, if we round up, the jumps might push the final unit selection past the end of the population size \(N\) before we secure \(n\) items. Consequently, the actual sample size will vary depending on which random start \(r\) is chosen, violating the statistical requirement of a fixed sample size.
Q6. How does Circular Systematic Sampling mathematically solve the problem described in Q5?
Solution: Circular Systematic Sampling treats the population list as a closed circle where unit 1 immediately follows unit \(N\). It selects \(k\) as the nearest integer to \(N/n\), but instead of picking the random start from 1 to \(N\). By stepping by \(k\) and wrapping around the circle (using \(r + (j-1)k - N\) if the sum exceeds \(N\)), it guarantees that exactly \(n\) units will be selected for every single possible random start, ensuring a constant sample size and equal inclusion probability for all units.
Q7. Explain the role of the Intra-class Correlation Coefficient (\(\rho\)) in determining the efficiency of Systematic Sampling.
Solution: The intra-class correlation coefficient (\(\rho\)) measures the degree of similarity between units that belong to the same systematic sample. If \(\rho\) is positive, it means the units within the sample are too similar to each other (homogeneous), making the sample unrepresentative of the whole population, and inflating the variance \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\) above SRS. If \(\rho\) is negative, the units within the sample are highly dissimilar (heterogeneous), which is ideal, making \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\) significantly lower (more efficient) than SRS.
Q8. Under what exact condition is Systematic Sampling mathematically more precise than Simple Random Sampling?
Solution: According to Theorem 5.3, Systematic Sampling is more precise (has a lower variance) than Simple Random Sampling if and only if the intra-class correlation coefficient among the units in the same systematic sample is strictly negative (\(\rho < 0\)).
Q9. What is the most severe practical demerit of Systematic Sampling regarding Variance estimation?
Solution: The most severe demerit is that it is impossible to calculate an unbiased estimate of the sampling variance (\(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\)) from a single systematic sample. Because a single systematic sample is effectively just one cluster of units drawn with probability \(1/k\), we have \(n=1\) cluster. You cannot calculate a valid mathematical variance with a sample size of 1. Statisticians must rely on approximations or assumptions about the population order to guess the variance.
Q10. A population is ordered such that there is a linear trend (values steadily increase from 1 to \(N\)). Will Systematic Sampling perform better or worse than SRS here?
Solution: It will perform significantly better than SRS. In a linear trend, an SRS might randomly pick too many items from the "low" end or the "high" end. Systematic sampling, however, takes exactly one item from every block along the trend line. This forces the sample to perfectly balance the low, middle, and high values, resulting in an incredibly representative sample mean and a variance much lower than SRS.
Q11. Explain how a "pseudo-random" ordering of the sampling frame affects the efficiency of Systematic Sampling.
Solution: If the population frame is ordered alphabetically (e.g., a list of names), this ordering has nothing to do with the variable being studied (like income). This creates a "pseudo-random" arrangement. In this case, systematic sampling behaves exactly like Simple Random Sampling. The variance of the systematic sample will be approximately equal to the variance of an SRS, meaning \(\rho \approx 0\).
Q12. Derive the formula for the variance of the systematic sample mean \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\) in terms of the individual sample means \(\bar{y}_{i.}\).
Solution: By definition, Variance is the expected squared deviation from the mean: \(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) = E[(\bar{y}_{sys} - \bar{Y})^2]\). Since there are exactly \(k\) possible systematic samples, each selected with probability \(1/k\), the expectation is simply the average over these \(k\) samples. Therefore, substituting the possible values \(\bar{y}_{i.}\) gives: \(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^k (\bar{y}_{i.} - \bar{Y})^2\).
Q13. In Circular Systematic Sampling, prove that every unit has an equal probability of selection (\(n/N\)).
Solution: In the circular method, the random start \(r\) is chosen uniformly from \(1\) to \(N\). A specific unit \(x\) is included in the sample if and only if it falls on one of the \(n\) selected spots: \(r, r+k, r+2k, \dots, r+(n-1)k\) (modulo \(N\)). For any specific unit \(x\), there are exactly \(n\) possible values of the random start \(r\) that will cause \(x\) to be hit by the jumps of size \(k\). Since there are \(n\) favorable starts out of \(N\) total possible starts, the probability is exactly \(n/N\).
Q14. Why is Systematic Sampling considered a special case of Cluster Sampling?
Solution: A cluster sample divides a population into groups (clusters) and randomly selects whole clusters. In linear systematic sampling, the interval \(k\) naturally defines \(k\) completely rigid, fixed sequences (clusters) of size \(n\). When you pick the random start \(r\), you are not picking \(n\) independent units; you are randomly picking exactly 1 of these \(k\) predefined clusters. Therefore, systematic sampling is mathematically equivalent to cluster sampling where the sample size is 1 cluster.
Q15. Compare Systematic Sampling with Stratified Random Sampling.
Solution: Stratified sampling divides the population into explicit, homogeneous groups, and then randomly draws units from *every* group. Systematic sampling divides the population into implicit blocks (size \(k\)), and draws exactly one unit from each block based on a single random start. While both spread the sample evenly, stratified sampling uses independent random draws in each block, whereas systematic sampling's draws are perfectly locked together by the interval \(k\).
Q16. What is the formula for the sample total estimate \(\hat{Y}\) in systematic sampling, and how is its variance related to \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\)?
Solution: The estimated population total is simply the population size multiplied by the unbiased sample mean: \(\hat{Y} = N \cdot \bar{y}_{sys}\). According to variance rules, \(V(aX) = a^2 V(X)\). Therefore, the variance of the estimated total is \(V(\hat{Y}) = N^2 \cdot V(\bar{y}_{sys})\).
Q17. Explain the scenario where the intra-class correlation coefficient \(\rho\) achieves its maximum possible value (\(\rho = 1\)). What does this imply?
Solution: \(\rho = 1\) occurs when all units *within* every single systematic sample are exactly identical to each other, but the sample means differ wildly from one another. This is the absolute worst-case scenario for systematic sampling. If all units in a sample are identical, drawing \(n\) units gives you no more information than drawing just 1 unit. The variance explodes, and systematic sampling becomes vastly inferior to SRS.
Q18. Explain the mathematical condition \(\rho < -\frac{1}{N-1}\). Why is this important?
Solution: We know Systematic is better than SRS when \(\rho < 0\). However, if we compare Systematic against a fully *Stratified* design, Systematic sampling is only more precise if \(\rho\) is exceptionally negative, specifically \(\rho < -\frac{1}{N-1}\). This requires extreme internal heterogeneity within the systematic sample, meaning the sequence perfectly captures the full variance of the population in every single draw.
Q19. Describe how one might practically estimate the sampling variance in a systematic survey despite the theoretical impossibility (\(n=1\) cluster).
Solution: Since a single systematic sample provides no unbiased estimate of variance, researchers often use approximations. If they assume the population is randomly ordered, they simply use the SRS variance formula \((1-f)s^2/n\). Another common method is treating adjacent pairs of selected units as if they were drawn from a single stratum (the "Method of Successive Differences") to estimate the variance.
Q20. Walk through a Circular Systematic Sampling example where \(N=7\), \(n=3\). What is \(k\), and what are the selected units if \(r=6\)?
Solution: First, calculate the interval: \(k = N/n = 7/3 = 2.33\). The nearest integer is \(k=2\).
We need \(n=3\) units starting at \(r=6\).
- Unit 1: \(6\)
- Unit 2: \(6 + 2 = 8\). Since \(8 > 7\), we wrap around: \(8 - 7 = 1\). Select unit \(1\).
- Unit 3: \(1 + 2 = 3\). Select unit \(3\).
The final circular sample consists of units 6, 1, and 3.

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Qs)

Q1. In linear systematic sampling, the sampling interval \(k\) is calculated as:
Correct Answer: (C) \(N/n\)
Reason: It chops the total population \(N\) into \(n\) equal blocks.
Q2. In linear systematic sampling, how many units are chosen purely by random chance?
Correct Answer: (B) Only the first unit (\(r\))
Reason: Once \(r\) is randomly drawn, every subsequent unit is mechanically picked by adding \(k\).
Q3. If the random start is \(r=4\) and interval \(k=10\), the third unit selected in the sample will be:
Correct Answer: (B) 24
Reason: Sequence is \(r, r+k, r+2k\). 4, 14, 24.
Q4. Systematic sampling acts as a form of:
Correct Answer: (B) Implicit Stratification
Reason: It automatically divides the population into blocks without the researcher having to manually stratify them.
Q5. If \(N = nk\), the systematic sample mean \(\bar{y}_{sys}\) is an estimator for the population mean \(\bar{Y}\) that is:
Correct Answer: (B) Exactly Unbiased
Reason: Theorem 5.1 proves \(E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \bar{Y}\).
Q6. Circular Systematic Sampling is used primarily when:
Correct Answer: (C) \(N\) is not an exact multiple of \(n\)
Reason: It solves the non-integer interval problem by treating the population as a closed loop.
Q7. In Circular Systematic Sampling, the random start \(r\) is chosen between:
Correct Answer: (C) 1 and \(N\)
Reason: Unlike linear sampling, circular sampling allows ANY unit in the population to be the starting point.
Q8. Systematic sampling will give disastrously bad (biased) results if the population frame has a:
Correct Answer: (C) Periodic (cyclical) trend
Reason: If the interval matches the cycle (e.g., measuring every 7th day), you only measure one part of the cycle, missing the rest.
Q9. Systematic sampling is more precise than SRS if the intra-class correlation coefficient (\(\rho\)) is:
Correct Answer: (C) \(\rho < 0\)
Reason: Negative correlation means units inside the sample are very different from each other, maximizing representativeness.
Q10. A major demerit of Systematic Sampling is that it is impossible to calculate an unbiased estimate of:
Correct Answer: (C) The sampling variance
Reason: Because a systematic sample is effectively a single draw of a cluster (\(n=1\)), variance cannot be mathematically estimated without assumptions.
Q11. If the sampling frame is ordered alphabetically by name, the resulting systematic sample will behave similarly to:
Correct Answer: (B) Simple Random Sampling (SRS)
Reason: Alphabetical order is usually uncorrelated with the study variable, creating a pseudo-random distribution.
Q12. What is the total number of possible samples of size \(n\) that can be drawn using linear systematic sampling when \(N=nk\)?
Correct Answer: (C) \(k\)
Reason: The entire sample sequence is determined entirely by the random start \(r\), which has exactly \(k\) possible values.
Q13. In Circular Systematic Sampling with \(N=14\) and \(n=4\), what is the sampling interval \(k\)?
Correct Answer: (C) 4
Reason: \(N/n = 14/4 = 3.5\). In circular sampling, \(k\) is rounded to the nearest integer. Half rounds up, so \(k=4\).
Q14. In a linear trend population, Systematic Sampling places the sample mean exactly at the population mean if \(n\) is:
Correct Answer: (B) Odd
Reason: Mathematical property of linear trend variances. For odd \(n\), the centered systematic sample perfectly hits the mean.
Q15. The formula for the variance of the systematic mean is \(V(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \frac{1}{k} \sum (\bar{y}_{i.} - \bar{Y})^2\). This calculates the variance:
Correct Answer: (B) Between the possible sample means
Reason: It measures how much the \(k\) different sample means deviate from the true population mean.
Q16. Systematic sampling is mathematically equivalent to which other sampling method if the sample size is exactly 1?
Correct Answer: (C) Cluster
Reason: The systematic selection locks \(n\) units together into a single rigid group, just like a cluster. We randomly pick 1 of the \(k\) clusters.
Q17. Which of the following is NOT an advantage of systematic sampling over SRS?
Correct Answer: (C) Always provides an unbiased variance estimate
Reason: As discussed, variance estimation is mathematically impossible from a single systematic sample.
Q18. In circular systematic sampling, if \(r + (j-1)k > N\), the selected unit is:
Correct Answer: (B) \(r + (j-1)k - N\)
Reason: This is the mathematical "wrap-around" rule to continue counting from the start of the circle.
Q19. For systematic sampling to be more efficient than *Stratified* sampling, \(\rho\) must be less than:
Correct Answer: (B) \(-1/(N-1)\)
Reason: A stricter negative bound is required to beat stratification compared to beating SRS (which only requires \(\rho < 0\)).
Q20. If all units within every possible systematic sample are perfectly identical, then \(\rho = 1\). In this case, \(V(\bar{y}_{sys})\) is:
Correct Answer: (C) Maximized
Reason: If units inside the sample are identical, the sample provides no diversity. The variance explodes to its maximum possible value.

Part D: True or False (20 Qs)

1. In systematic sampling, every single unit in the final sample is chosen randomly.
False. Only the FIRST unit (\(r\)) is chosen randomly. The rest are determined mechanically by adding \(k\).
2. Linear systematic sampling guarantees that every possible combination of \(n\) units has an equal chance of selection.
False. Only \(k\) specific sequences are possible. Any sequence not matching the "every \(k^{th}\)" rule has zero probability of selection, unlike in SRS.
3. If \(N = nk\), the systematic sample mean is an exactly unbiased estimator of the population mean.
True. Theorem 5.1 mathematically proves that \(E(\bar{y}_{sys}) = \bar{Y}\).
4. Systematic sampling is generally much faster and cheaper to execute in the field than pure SRS.
True. It eliminates the need to constantly look up random numbers and track specific wildly scattered units.
5. Circular systematic sampling is used when the data contains strong periodic cycles.
False. Circular sampling is used strictly to solve the mathematical problem of \(N\) not being a multiple of \(n\). It does not fix periodic data problems.
6. In circular systematic sampling, the random start can be any number from 1 up to \(N\).
True. Unlike linear sampling (which restricts the start to \(1 \dots k\)), circular allows the entire loop to be a starting point.
7. If the data has a hidden cycle matching the interval \(k\), systematic sampling will be highly precise.
False. It will be highly BIASED and disastrous, because it will only measure one extreme of the cycle over and over again.
8. If the intra-class correlation \(\rho\) is a large positive number, Systematic Sampling is worse than Simple Random Sampling.
True. Positive \(\rho\) means the sample units are too similar to each other, failing to capture the population's true diversity, inflating the variance.
9. It is mathematically impossible to calculate an unbiased estimate of the sampling variance from a single systematic sample.
True. Because a systematic sample represents just one clustered draw (\(n=1\) cluster), you need at least two independent draws to objectively calculate variance.
10. Systematic sampling essentially forces the population to be implicitly stratified into \(n\) strata of size \(k\).
True. This is the exact mechanism that makes systematic sampling spread out evenly and perform better than SRS on average.
11. In Circular sampling, if the interval \(k=3\), random start \(r=10\), and \(N=11\), the second unit selected will be 2.
True. First unit is 10. Second is \(10+3 = 13\). Since \(13 > 11\), we do \(13 - 11 = 2\).
12. Linear systematic sampling can sometimes draw a sample where the number of units collected is less than \(n\).
True. This happens when \(N \neq nk\) and you round the interval \(k\) incorrectly, reaching the end of the population list too early.
13. Sorting a population list alphabetically by respondent name usually creates a linear trend in the data.
False. Alphabetical names rarely correlate with study variables (like income or height), creating a pseudo-random order, not a linear trend.
14. Systematic sampling provides better protection against drawing a highly concentrated, unrepresentative sample than pure SRS.
True. By jumping at fixed intervals, it guarantees the sample is physically spread across the entire sampling frame.
15. To estimate population variance from a systematic sample, researchers often pretend the data was collected via SRS.
True. Because an exact unbiased estimate is impossible, treating it as pseudo-SRS is a common practical approximation.
16. The expected value of the systematic sample mean is derived by summing the \(k\) possible sample means and dividing by \(n\).
False. You divide by \(k\) (the number of possible samples), not by \(n\) (the sample size).
17. Systematic sampling is exactly equivalent to Cluster Sampling where the population is divided into \(k\) clusters of size \(n\).
True. The interval locks the units into \(k\) rigid, predefined sequence groups (clusters), and we randomly select just 1 group.
18. The intra-class correlation coefficient \(\rho\) reaches its maximum value of \(+1\) when all sample means \(\bar{y}_{i.}\) are exactly equal to the population mean \(\bar{Y}\).
False. \(\rho = 1\) when all units *inside* the same sample are identical (zero within-sample variance), which forces the sample means to differ wildly from the population mean (maximum variance).
19. If \(\rho = -1/(n-1)\), the variance of the systematic sample mean drops to exactly zero.
True. In the formula \(V \propto [1 + (n-1)\rho]\), plugging in \(-1/(n-1)\) results in \([1 - 1] = 0\). This is the mathematical threshold for perfect precision.
20. One major requirement for Systematic Sampling is that the sampling frame must exist in some physical or ordered list format.
True. Because you must "jump" by \(k\) units, you cannot conduct systematic sampling without a pre-ordered list, a line of houses, or a conveyor belt of items.
