5.1 The Concept of Systematic Sampling

Systematic sampling is a highly organized and easy way to pick a sample. Instead of picking every unit randomly, you only pick the first unit randomly and then select the rest at a fixed, regular interval.

Imagine a librarian wants to check 100 books from a shelf of 1,000. They pick a random starting number between 1 and 10 (let's say 3). Then, they pick the 3rd, 13th, 23rd, 33rd book, and so on. This is Systematic Sampling.

Linear Systematic Sampling

If the population size (N) is a perfect multiple of the sample size (n), we calculate the interval \(k = N/n\). We pick a random start \(r\) where \(1 \le r \le k\). The sample units will be: \(r, r+k, r+2k, \ldots, r+(n-1)k\).

Theorem 5.1

If \(N = nk\), the systematic sample mean (\(\bar{y}_{sys}\)) is an unbiased estimator of the population mean (\(\bar{Y}\)).

5.2 Circular Systematic Sampling

What if \(N\) is not exactly divisible by \(n\)? For example, if you have 11 students and want to pick 3. \(11/3 = 3.66\). In such cases, Circular Systematic Sampling is the best solution.

How it works:

  1. Think of the population as a circle (the last unit is followed by the first).
  2. Pick a random starting number \(r\) from 1 to N (any unit can be the start).
  3. Calculate the interval \(k\) (usually the integer closest to \(N/n\)).
  4. Pick your units by jumping by \(k\) each time around the circle until you have \(n\) units.

This method ensures that every single unit in the population has an equal chance of being selected, which makes it more fair than simple linear sampling when \(N \neq nk\).

5.3 Efficiency and Comparison

How does Systematic Sampling compare to Simple Random Sampling (SRS) and Stratified Sampling?

1. vs. Simple Random Sampling

Systematic sampling is usually more precise than SRS if the population is ordered in a way that creates diversity within the sample. However, it can be very poor if there is a "periodic trend" in the data (e.g., if you sample every 7th day, you might always pick a Sunday!).

2. vs. Stratified Sampling

Systematic sampling is actually a type of Implicit Stratification. It's like dividing the population into 'n' blocks and picking one unit from each block at the same position. It is often as efficient as stratified sampling but much easier to carry out.

5.4 Merits and Demerits

Merits (Advantages) Demerits (Disadvantages)
Very simple and fast to implement. Can be highly biased if there are periodic patterns.
Low cost and less field work required. Hard to estimate the "Sampling Error" from a single sample.
Sample is spread evenly across the population. Not truly "random" for all units after the first one.

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions

Q1. If \(N=100\) and \(n=10\), the sampling interval \(k\) is:
(a) 1    (b) 10    (c) 100    (d) 0.1
Correct Answer: (b) 10
Explanation: \(k = N/n = 100/10 = 10\).
Q2. Systematic sampling is most likely to fail if the population has:
(a) Random order    (b) Periodic variation    (c) Homogeneity    (d) Small size
Correct Answer: (b) Periodic variation

Part C: Fill in the Blanks

1. In systematic sampling, only the ___________ unit is selected at random.
Answer: First
2. Circular systematic sampling is used when \(N\) is not a ___________ of \(n\).
Answer: Multiple (or divisible)

Part D: True or False

1. Systematic sampling is generally easier to perform in the field than stratified sampling. [True / False]
Answer: True
2. In systematic sampling, every possible combination of 'n' units has an equal chance of being selected. [True / False]
Answer: False
Explanation: Only 'k' possible samples are ever possible in linear systematic sampling, unlike SRS.
