About This Course

STAT-M-102: Probability Theory is a core course offered in Semester II carrying 2 credit hours (theory). It lays the rigorous mathematical foundations of probability — an indispensable language of uncertainty — that underpins all subsequent courses in mathematical statistics, stochastic processes, and data science.

The course opens with the classical and axiomatic conceptions of probability (Unit I), advances through conditional probability and Bayes' theorem (Unit II), introduces the all-important notion of random variables and their distribution functions (Unit III), and culminates in the powerful machinery of mathematical expectation, generating functions, and characteristic functions (Unit IV).

Each unit contains detailed theory, worked examples, practice problems, and multiple-choice questions to reinforce understanding and prepare students for examinations.

Preface

Probability theory stands at the very heart of modern statistics, science, and rational thought. From the toss of a coin to the modelling of complex natural phenomena, the language of probability is inescapable for anyone who wishes to reason carefully about uncertainty. This web-based textbook on Probability Theory has been prepared to serve the students of STAT-M-102 at CCS Haryana Agricultural University, Hisar, in accordance with the prescribed syllabus for Semester II.

The preparation of this material has been guided by a single overriding principle: clarity without sacrifice of rigour. Every definition is stated precisely, every theorem is accompanied by a proof or an indication of the method of proof, and every abstract concept is illustrated with concrete, accessible examples drawn from everyday experience and agricultural contexts familiar to the students of this university.

Probability is the very guide of life.

— Marcus Tullius Cicero, De Natura Deorum

Scope and Organisation

The course is divided into four self-contained yet interconnected units. Unit I introduces the fundamental language of probability — sample spaces, events, and their operations — and systematically examines four conceptual approaches to defining probability: the classical, the relative-frequency, the statistical, and the modern axiomatic approach due to Kolmogorov. Students are encouraged to appreciate both the historical development and the logical necessity of each approach.

Unit II deepens the study with conditional probability, the multiplication and addition laws, Boole's inequality, and the celebrated theorem of Thomas Bayes — whose applications range from medical diagnostics to spam filters and machine learning classifiers.

Unit III introduces the random variable, arguably the single most important concept in the entire theory. Both discrete and continuous types are treated, with careful attention to probability mass functions, probability density functions, distribution functions, and their fundamental properties. Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions are also developed fully.

Unit IV develops the analytical tools of mathematical expectation. Moments, conditional expectation and variance, and the three major generating functions — the moment generating function (MGF), cumulant generating function (CGF), and probability generating function (PGF) — are presented along with the characteristic function, whose role in proving limit theorems is briefly indicated.

Pedagogical Features

Each unit contains (i) a conceptual introduction accessible to first-time readers, (ii) formally stated definitions and theorems, (iii) fully worked solved problems, (iv) sets of practice problems graded in difficulty, and (v) multiple-choice questions for self-assessment. All mathematical notation follows established conventions in the statistical literature, and \(\LaTeX\)-rendered formulae are displayed throughout using MathJax.

Students are advised to work through the solved examples carefully before attempting the exercises, and to consult the suggested references for alternative treatments of any topic found difficult.

Course Syllabus

The following table presents the detailed unit-wise syllabus for STAT-M-102: Probability Theory as prescribed for Semester II.

Unit Title Topics Covered
Unit I Concepts in Probability Random experiment, trial, sample point, sample space, operations on events. Exhaustive, equally likely, mutually exclusive, and independent events. Classical, relative frequency, statistical, and axiomatic definitions of probability.
Unit II Conditional Probability Addition and multiplication laws of probability and their extension to \(n\) events. Boole's inequality. Bayes' theorem and its applications.
Unit III Random Variable & Probability Functions Definition of random variable: discrete and continuous types. Probability function, PMF and PDF. Distribution function and its properties. Functions of random variables. Joint, marginal, and conditional distribution functions.
Unit IV Mathematical Expectation Definition and properties: moments, addition and multiplication theorem of expectation. Conditional expectation and variance. Moment generating function (MGF), cumulant generating function (CGF), probability generating function (PGF) and their properties. Characteristic function.

A Note from the Author

Dear Students,

It is with great pleasure that I present these study materials for Probability Theory (STAT-M-102). Over the many years that I have taught probability and statistics at this university, I have become deeply convinced of one thing: a student who truly understands probability will find almost every subsequent course in statistics significantly more accessible.

Probability is not merely a collection of formulae to be memorised before an examination. It is a coherent way of thinking — a discipline for reasoning carefully under uncertainty — and its applications touch every field from agriculture and biology to engineering, economics, and artificial intelligence. The student who masters these four units will have acquired a set of intellectual tools that will serve them for life.

The most important questions of life are indeed, for the most part, really only problems of probability.

— Pierre-Simon Laplace, Théorie Analytique des Probabilités (1812)

How to Use These Notes

These notes have been designed to be read actively, not passively. For each unit, I recommend the following approach:

1. Read the introduction first — get an intuitive feel for what the unit is about and why the concepts matter before tackling the formal definitions.

2. Work through every definition carefully — do not rush. A blurry understanding of a definition will propagate confusion through every theorem that follows. If a definition uses a term you are unsure about, look it up immediately.

3. Prove every theorem yourself before reading the proof — or at least attempt it. Even an unsuccessful attempt trains your mathematical intuition and makes the given proof far more meaningful when you do read it.

4. Work every solved example independently first — cover the solution and attempt the problem on your own. Check your reasoning, not just your final answer.

5. Consult the textbooks — especially Gupta & Kapoor, which remains the definitive reference for this course. Different authors illuminate different aspects of the same concept, and reading multiple treatments deepens understanding enormously.

A Word on Rigour and Intuition

Mathematics lives at the intersection of rigour and intuition. In these notes I have tried to provide formal proofs wherever appropriate, but I have equally tried to explain the why behind each result — the intuition that makes the formula not just correct but inevitable. Please do not neglect either side of this coin.

My doors are always open. If any topic is unclear, please do not hesitate to come to me during office hours, or to contact the department. Understanding, not marks, is the goal.

I wish you the very best in your studies.
