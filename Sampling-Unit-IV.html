4.1 Core Principles of Stratification

In Simple Random Sampling, we treat the whole population as one big group. But what if the group is very diverse? For example, if you want to know the average pocket money of students, it might be very different between students living in hostels and those living at home.

Stratification is the process of dividing a diverse population into smaller, more "alike" groups called Strata (singular: Stratum).

The Golden Rule of Stratification:

  • Within Strata: The units should be as similar as possible (Homogeneous).
  • Between Strata: The groups should be as different as possible (Heterogeneous).

Imagine a fruit basket with 100 apples and 100 watermelons. If you pick a random sample of 5, you might get all apples by chance! In Stratified Sampling, you divide them into two strata: Apples and Watermelons. Then you pick 2 apples and 3 watermelons. Now your sample is perfectly balanced.

4.2 Estimation and Variance

In Stratified Sampling, we calculate the mean for each stratum first, and then combine them to get the overall population mean.

Overall Mean (\(\bar{y}_{st}\)) = \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i\)

Where \(W_i\) is the "weight" or importance of that group (Stratum Size / Total Population Size).

Theorem: Unbiasedness

The stratified sample mean \(\bar{y}_{st}\) is an unbiased estimator of the population mean \(\bar{Y}\).

Why is it better?

Because we have forced the sample to represent every sub-group, the Sampling Variance is usually much lower than in SRS. This means our guesses are much more accurate.

4.3 Allocation Methods

Once you've divided the population into strata, how many units should you pick from each group? There are four main methods:

1. Equal Allocation

You pick the same number from every group. (e.g., 10 from every stratum). Simple, but not always efficient.

2. Proportional Allocation

You pick more from the bigger groups. If one stratum makes up 60% of the population, 60% of your sample comes from there.

\(n_i = n \times (N_i / N)\)

3. Neyman's (Optimum) Allocation

This is even smarter. You pick more from groups that are either very large or very diverse (high standard deviation). If a group is very predictable, you don't need a large sample from it.

\(n_i = n \times \frac{W_i S_i}{\sum W_i S_i}\)

4. Allocation for Fixed Cost

If some groups are more expensive to study (e.g., people living in remote mountains), you take a smaller sample from them to save money while keeping high accuracy.

4.4 Stratified Sampling vs. Simple Random Sampling

Is Stratified Sampling always better? Almost always!

Gain in Precision

The variance of the estimate in Stratified Sampling is always less than or equal to the variance in SRS, provided the allocation is proportional.

Summary of Benefits:

  • Better Representation: No important sub-group is left out.
  • Greater Precision: You get a more accurate answer with the same sample size.
  • Administrative Convenience: Different teams can handle different strata simultaneously.

4.5 Practice Exercises

Part A: Subjective Questions

1. Explain the "Golden Rule" of stratification with a real-life example.
2. Define Proportional Allocation and give its mathematical formula.
3. When would you prefer Neyman's Allocation over Proportional Allocation?

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions

Q1. In stratified sampling, the sub-groups (strata) should be:
(a) Heterogeneous within    (b) Homogeneous within    (c) Randomly mixed    (d) Overlapping
Correct Answer: (b) Homogeneous within
Explanation: Units inside a single stratum should be as similar as possible.
Q2. Which allocation method considers both group size and internal diversity (S.D.)?
(a) Equal    (b) Proportional    (c) Neyman's    (d) SRS
Correct Answer: (c) Neyman's

Part C: Fill in the Blanks

1. Proportional allocation is used when the only information available for each stratum is its ___________.
Answer: Size (or Population)
2. Stratification increases the ___________ of the final estimate.
Answer: Precision (or Accuracy)

Part D: True or False

1. Strata must be mutually exclusive (non-overlapping). [True / False]
Answer: True
2. Stratified sampling always requires a larger sample size than SRS for the same accuracy. [True / False]
Answer: False
Explanation: It usually requires a smaller sample size for the same level of accuracy.
