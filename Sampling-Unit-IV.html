4.1 Core Principles of Stratification

Hello students! In the previous unit, we studied Simple Random Sampling (SRS), where we treated the entire population as one big, single group. SRS works wonderfully when everyone in the population is somewhat similar to each other. But what happens if the population is extremely diverse, or heterogeneous?

Imagine a fruit basket containing 100 apples and 100 watermelons. You want to estimate the average weight of a piece of fruit in the basket, so you use SRS to pick a sample of 10 fruits. Just by bad luck (random chance), your sample might contain 9 watermelons and 1 apple! Your estimated average weight will be completely wrong because it doesn't represent the true mix of the basket. This is the biggest weakness of SRS.

To solve this, we use Stratified Random Sampling. Instead of picking blindly from the whole basket, we first divide the basket into two separate groups: one for apples, and one for watermelons. These groups are called Strata (singular: Stratum). Then, we pick a random sample of apples from the apple group, and a random sample of watermelons from the watermelon group.

Stratified Random Sampling

It is a sampling procedure in which the entire heterogeneous population of size \(N\) is first divided into \(k\) non-overlapping, distinct sub-populations called Strata (of sizes \(N_1, N_2, \dots, N_k\)). Then, independent Simple Random Samples (of sizes \(n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k\)) are drawn separately from each individual stratum.

The Golden Rule of Stratification

For stratification to be successful, you must construct your strata such that the units inside a stratum are as similar as possible, while the different strata are as different from each other as possible.

  • Within Strata: Maximum Homogeneity (Low Variance \(S_i^2\)).
  • Between Strata: Maximum Heterogeneity.

Real-Life Examples of Stratification

  1. Estimating Student Pocket Money: Students living in hostels spend much more than day scholars living at home. So, we divide the university into two strata: "Hostelers" and "Day Scholars". We sample independently from both.
  2. Agricultural Crop Yield: Yields vary drastically based on water. We stratify the farms into "Irrigated Farms" and "Un-irrigated Farms".
  3. Industrial Quality Control: If a factory produces screws using 3 different machines (A, B, and C), we treat each machine's output as a separate stratum, because one machine might be older and produce more defects.

Advantages of Stratified Sampling over SRS

  • Greater Precision: Because we force the sample to include units from every single sub-group, it is far more representative. This mathematically reduces the sampling error (Variance).
  • Protection against Bad Samples: It is impossible to accidentally draw a sample containing only watermelons, because we are forced to pick some apples from the apple stratum.
  • Administrative Convenience: Different strata can be handled by different field offices. For a national survey, each State can be treated as a stratum, and the State's own local office can conduct the survey independently.
  • Sub-group Estimates: Not only do we get a highly accurate estimate for the whole country, but we also get separate, highly accurate estimates for each individual state (stratum).

4.2 Estimation & Variance (The Math of Stratification)

Now we must dive into the mathematics of how to combine the data from all our different strata to make one final, grand estimate for the entire population. First, let us clearly define our notations. In examinations, writing the correct notations is half the battle won!

Notation Guide for the \(i^{th}\) Stratum

Let the population of size \(N\) be divided into \(k\) strata. For any specific stratum (let's call it the \(i^{th}\) stratum):

  • \(N_i\): Total number of units in the \(i^{th}\) stratum population. (Note: \(N_1 + N_2 + \dots + N_k = N\)).
  • \(n_i\): Number of units selected in the sample from the \(i^{th}\) stratum. (Note: \(n_1 + n_2 + \dots + n_k = n\)).
  • \(W_i\): The "Weight" of the \(i^{th}\) stratum. It is the proportion of the total population that belongs to this stratum. \(W_i = \frac{N_i}{N}\).
  • \(\bar{Y}_i\): The true Population Mean of the \(i^{th}\) stratum.
  • \(\bar{y}_i\): The Sample Mean of the \(i^{th}\) stratum.
  • \(S_i^2\): The true Population Variance (Mean Square) inside the \(i^{th}\) stratum.

Our ultimate goal is to find the overall Population Mean, denoted by \(\bar{Y}\).

How do we estimate the overall mean? We calculate the sample mean for each stratum, and then take a Weighted Average of them, based on how big each stratum is.

The Stratified Sample Mean (\(\bar{y}_{st}\))

The estimator for the overall population mean in stratified sampling is denoted by \(\bar{y}_{st}\) (where 'st' stands for stratified).

\[ \bar{y}_{st} = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i = \sum_{i=1}^k \left( \frac{N_i}{N} \right) \bar{y}_i \]

Theorem 4.1: Unbiasedness of Stratified Mean

If a simple random sample is taken independently from each stratum, then the stratified sample mean \(\bar{y}_{st}\) is an unbiased estimator of the overall population mean \(\bar{Y}\). Mathematically: \(E(\bar{y}_{st}) = \bar{Y}\).

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 4.1

We start with the definition of the stratified mean: \[ \bar{y}_{st} = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i \]

Take the Expected Value of both sides. Since \(W_i\) (the stratum weights) are fixed constants, we can pull the Expectation operator inside the sum:

\[ E(\bar{y}_{st}) = E \left( \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i E(\bar{y}_i) \]

Now, recall what we learned in Unit III. Because we are drawing a Simple Random Sample from inside the \(i^{th}\) stratum, Theorem 3.3 guarantees that the sample mean of that stratum is an unbiased estimator of that stratum's true mean. Therefore, \(E(\bar{y}_i) = \bar{Y}_i\).

Substitute this back into our equation:

\[ E(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{Y}_i \]

By definition, the sum of the weighted stratum means is exactly the overall Population Mean \(\bar{Y}\).

\[ E(\bar{y}_{st}) = \bar{Y} \]

Hence proved! The stratified estimator perfectly targets the true population mean.

Theorem 4.2: Variance of the Stratified Mean

If samples are drawn independently from each stratum using SRSWOR, the variance of the stratified sample mean \(\bar{y}_{st}\) is given by:

\[ V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i^2 V(\bar{y}_i) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i^2 \left( \frac{N_i - n_i}{N_i n_i} \right) S_i^2 \]

(Note: If the strata populations \(N_i\) are very large, the FPC term \((N_i-n_i)/N_i \approx 1\), and the formula simplifies to \(V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum W_i^2 \frac{S_i^2}{n_i}\)).

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 4.2

We know that \(\bar{y}_{st} = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i\). We need to find its variance.

According to the laws of probability, the variance of a sum of independent variables is simply the sum of their individual variances. The constants (\(W_i\)) are squared when pulled out of a variance operator. Because we specifically designed our survey to draw samples from each stratum independently of the others, the covariance between \(\bar{y}_i\) and \(\bar{y}_j\) is absolutely zero!

\[ V(\bar{y}_{st}) = V \left( \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i^2 V(\bar{y}_i) \]

From Unit III (Theorem 3.5), we already know the exact formula for the variance of a sample mean drawn via SRSWOR for any specific stratum 'i':

\[ V(\bar{y}_i) = \left( \frac{N_i - n_i}{N_i} \right) \frac{S_i^2}{n_i} \]

Substitute this into our summation:

\[ V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i^2 \left( \frac{N_i - n_i}{N_i n_i} \right) S_i^2 \]

Proved! Notice how the variance depends entirely on the variance inside the strata (\(S_i^2\)). If we followed the Golden Rule and made the strata highly homogeneous (very low \(S_i^2\)), our total variance \(V(\bar{y}_{st})\) will be incredibly small, meaning our survey is highly accurate.

Solved Example 1: Calculating Stratified Mean and Variance

Problem: A population of \(N=1000\) farms is divided into two strata based on irrigation.
Stratum 1 (Irrigated): \(N_1 = 400\), sample drawn \(n_1 = 40\), sample mean yield \(\bar{y}_1 = 80\) qt, sample variance \(s_1^2 = 60\).
Stratum 2 (Un-irrigated): \(N_2 = 600\), sample drawn \(n_2 = 60\), sample mean yield \(\bar{y}_2 = 30\) qt, sample variance \(s_2^2 = 90\).
Calculate the Stratified Sample Mean (\(\bar{y}_{st}\)) and estimate its Variance \(V(\bar{y}_{st})\).

Solution:
Step 1: Calculate the Weights (\(W_i\))
\(W_1 = N_1 / N = 400 / 1000 = 0.4\)
\(W_2 = N_2 / N = 600 / 1000 = 0.6\)

Step 2: Calculate Stratified Mean (\(\bar{y}_{st}\))
\(\bar{y}_{st} = W_1\bar{y}_1 + W_2\bar{y}_2\)
\(\bar{y}_{st} = (0.4 \times 80) + (0.6 \times 30)\)
\(\bar{y}_{st} = 32 + 18 =\) 50 quintals.

Step 3: Estimate the Variance \(V(\bar{y}_{st})\)
Since population variances (\(S_i^2\)) are unknown, we use sample variances (\(s_i^2\)) as unbiased estimates.
Formula: \(V(\bar{y}_{st}) = W_1^2 \left( \frac{N_1 - n_1}{N_1 n_1} \right) s_1^2 + W_2^2 \left( \frac{N_2 - n_2}{N_2 n_2} \right) s_2^2\)
For Stratum 1: \((0.4)^2 \times \left( \frac{400 - 40}{400 \times 40} \right) \times 60 = 0.16 \times \left( \frac{360}{16000} \right) \times 60 = 0.16 \times 0.0225 \times 60 = 0.216\)
For Stratum 2: \((0.6)^2 \times \left( \frac{600 - 60}{600 \times 60} \right) \times 90 = 0.36 \times \left( \frac{540}{36000} \right) \times 90 = 0.36 \times 0.015 \times 90 = 0.486\)
Total Estimated Variance = \(0.216 + 0.486 =\) 0.702.

4.3 Allocation Methods (The Core Optimization Problem)

We know we need to draw a total sample of size \(n\). But how many units should we take from Stratum 1? How many from Stratum 2? The process of distributing the total sample size \(n\) among the different strata (\(n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k\)) is called Allocation.

This is the most important decision a statistician makes. A bad allocation destroys the advantages of stratification. There are four main methods.

1. Equal Allocation

This is the lazy method. We simply divide the total sample size equally among all strata. If we need 100 samples from 4 strata, we take 25 from each.

\[ n_i = \frac{n}{k} \]

When to use: Practically never, unless all strata are exactly the same size and have the same variance, or if you simply have no prior information about the strata sizes. It is highly inefficient.

2. Proportional Allocation

This is the most common method. Here, the number of units taken from a stratum is strictly proportional to the total size of that stratum. If a stratum contains 60% of the total population, then 60% of our sample must come from that stratum.

\[ n_i \propto N_i \implies n_i = n \times \frac{N_i}{N} = n \cdot W_i \]

Mathematical Magic: If we substitute \(n_i = n W_i\) into our massive Variance formula (Theorem 4.2), assuming a large population where FPC is ignored (\(N_i-n_i \approx N_i\)), the variance formula simplifies drastically to a beautiful, clean equation:

\[ V_{prop}(\bar{y}_{st}) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i^2 \]

3. Neyman's (Optimum) Allocation

Proportional allocation is good, but it is not optimal. It only considers the SIZE of the stratum. What if a stratum is very large, but all the units inside it are identical (variance \(S_i^2 = 0\))? We don't need a large sample to understand it; just picking 1 unit gives us all the information! Conversely, a small stratum might be wildly heterogeneous, demanding a massive sample.

Neyman's Allocation solves this by allocating the sample size based on BOTH the size of the stratum (\(W_i\)) and its internal variation (\(S_i\)). We take more samples from a stratum if it is large, OR if it is highly variable.

🖋️ Derivation of Neyman's Allocation

Our goal is to minimize the Variance \(V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i^2 \frac{S_i^2}{n_i}\) subject to the fixed total sample size constraint: \(\sum_{i=1}^k n_i = n\).

We use the method of Lagrange Multipliers. Let our function be \(\Phi\):

\[ \Phi = \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{W_i^2 S_i^2}{n_i} + \lambda \left( \sum_{i=1}^k n_i - n \right) \]

To find the minimum, we take the partial derivative with respect to \(n_i\) and set it to zero:

\[ \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial n_i} = - \frac{W_i^2 S_i^2}{n_i^2} + \lambda = 0 \]

\[ n_i^2 = \frac{W_i^2 S_i^2}{\lambda} \implies n_i = \frac{W_i S_i}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \]

This tells us that \(n_i \propto W_i S_i\). To find the exact value, we sum both sides over all \(k\) strata:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^k n_i = \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{W_i S_i}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \implies n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i \]

So, \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda}} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i}\). Substitute this back into our \(n_i\) equation:

\[ n_i = n \frac{W_i S_i}{\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i} \]

This is the famous Neyman Allocation formula! If we substitute this \(n_i\) back into the variance formula, we get the absolute minimum possible variance:

\[ V_{opt}(\bar{y}_{st}) = \frac{1}{n} \left( \sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i \right)^2 \]

Solved Example 2: Proportional vs. Neyman Allocation

Problem: A population is divided into 3 strata.
Stratum 1: \(N_1 = 2000, S_1 = 5\)
Stratum 2: \(N_2 = 3000, S_2 = 10\)
Stratum 3: \(N_3 = 5000, S_3 = 20\)
We need to draw a total sample of \(n = 100\). Calculate the sample allocations (\(n_1, n_2, n_3\)) under (i) Proportional Allocation and (ii) Neyman's Allocation.

Solution:
Total \(N = 2000 + 3000 + 5000 = 10,000\).
Weights: \(W_1 = 0.2\), \(W_2 = 0.3\), \(W_3 = 0.5\).

(i) Proportional Allocation: (\(n_i = n \cdot W_i\))
\(n_1 = 100 \times 0.2 =\) 20
\(n_2 = 100 \times 0.3 =\) 30
\(n_3 = 100 \times 0.5 =\) 50

(ii) Neyman's Allocation: (\(n_i = n \frac{W_i S_i}{\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i}\))
First, calculate \(W_i S_i\) for each stratum:
Stratum 1: \(0.2 \times 5 = 1.0\)
Stratum 2: \(0.3 \times 10 = 3.0\)
Stratum 3: \(0.5 \times 20 = 10.0\)
Sum (\(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i\)) = \(1.0 + 3.0 + 10.0 = 14.0\).

Now allocate:
\(n_1 = 100 \times (1.0 / 14.0) = 7.14 \approx\) 7
\(n_2 = 100 \times (3.0 / 14.0) = 21.42 \approx\) 21
\(n_3 = 100 \times (10.0 / 14.0) = 71.42 \approx\) 72

Notice the brilliance of Neyman: Stratum 3 has massive variation (\(S_3=20\)), so Neyman forces us to take 72 samples from there to control the error, completely ignoring the "proportional" rule of taking only 50!

4. Optimum Allocation for Fixed Cost

In the real world, surveying a unit in a city slum costs 10 rupees, while surveying a unit in a remote Himalayan village costs 500 rupees. The cost is not equal across strata!

Let total cost \(C = c_0 + \sum_{i=1}^k c_i n_i\), where \(c_0\) is fixed overhead cost, and \(c_i\) is the cost per unit in the \(i^{th}\) stratum. If we use Lagrange multipliers again to minimize variance subject to this cost constraint, the math tells us to allocate samples directly proportional to size and variance, and inversely proportional to the square root of the cost.

\[ n_i \propto \frac{W_i S_i}{\sqrt{c_i}} \implies n_i = n \frac{W_i S_i / \sqrt{c_i}}{\sum_{i=1}^k (W_i S_i / \sqrt{c_i})} \]

This means if a stratum is extremely expensive to survey (\(c_i\) is huge), the math automatically reduces the number of samples we take from it to save our budget.

Solved Example 3: Cost Optimization

Problem: A state has 2 strata: Urban and Rural.
Urban: \(N_1 = 40,000, S_1 = 5\). Cost per survey \(c_1 = \text{Rs. } 9\).
Rural: \(N_2 = 60,000, S_2 = 8\). Cost per survey \(c_2 = \text{Rs. } 16\).
If total sample size is \(n=100\), calculate the Optimum Cost Allocation.

Solution:
Total \(N = 100,000\). Weights: \(W_1 = 0.4\), \(W_2 = 0.6\).
Calculate the weighting factor: \(W_i S_i / \sqrt{c_i}\)
Urban: \(0.4 \times 5 / \sqrt{9} = 2.0 / 3 = 0.667\)
Rural: \(0.6 \times 8 / \sqrt{16} = 4.8 / 4 = 1.200\)
Sum of factors = \(0.667 + 1.200 = 1.867\).

Allocate \(n=100\):
\(n_1 \text{ (Urban)} = 100 \times (0.667 / 1.867) \approx\) 36
\(n_2 \text{ (Rural)} = 100 \times (1.200 / 1.867) \approx\) 64
Because the Rural survey is much more expensive (Rs. 16), the formula restricted its sample size compared to pure Neyman allocation, optimizing our budget while maintaining maximum possible precision.

4.4 Efficiency Comparison: Stratified vs. SRS

Is Stratified Random Sampling always better than Simple Random Sampling? The answer is almost always yes, provided you do the allocation correctly. We measure "better" by comparing the variances. Lower variance means higher efficiency and greater precision.

Theorem 4.3: The Hierarchy of Efficiency

For a given population and a fixed total sample size \(n\), the variance of the sample mean follows this strict hierarchy (ignoring the finite population correction for simplicity):

\[ V_{opt}(\bar{y}_{st}) \le V_{prop}(\bar{y}_{st}) \le V_{srs}(\bar{y}) \]

This means Optimum (Neyman) Allocation is the best (lowest variance). Proportional is the second best. Pure SRS is the worst (highest variance).

🖋️ Algebraic Proof of Efficiency (Proportional vs SRS)

Why does Proportional Stratification beat SRS? Let's look at the famous Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) identity for a population divided into strata:

Total Variance = Variance WITHIN Strata + Variance BETWEEN Strata.

Mathematically: \[ S^2 \approx \sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^k W_i (\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 \]

We know \(V_{srs} = \frac{S^2}{n}\). Let's substitute the ANOVA expansion into the SRS formula:

\[ V_{srs} = \frac{1}{n} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^k W_i (\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 \right] \]

But wait! We proved earlier that the variance for Proportional Allocation is exactly the first term: \(V_{prop} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i^2\). Substitute this in:

\[ V_{srs} = V_{prop} + \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^k W_i (\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2 \]

Because a squared term \((\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2\) is always positive, we are adding a positive number to \(V_{prop}\) to get \(V_{srs}\). This rigorously proves that \(V_{srs}\) is ALWAYS greater than (or equal to) \(V_{prop}\).

The Conceptual Meaning: SRS suffers from the "Between Strata Variance" (the difference in means between the groups). Proportional Stratification mathematically eliminates the "Between Strata" error entirely! It only suffers from the "Within Strata" error. This is why the Golden Rule is "Heterogeneous between, Homogeneous within".

Solved Example 4: Gain in Precision

Problem: A researcher calculates the variances of an estimator under different designs for a population: \(V_{srs} = 85.0\), \(V_{prop} = 50.0\), and \(V_{opt} = 40.0\). Calculate the Percentage Gain in Precision when shifting from SRS to Proportional Allocation, and from SRS to Optimum Allocation.

Solution:
Gain in Precision is calculated as: \[ \frac{V_{worse} - V_{better}}{V_{better}} \times 100\% \]

1. Gain of Proportional over SRS:
Gain = \(\frac{85.0 - 50.0}{50.0} \times 100 = \frac{35.0}{50.0} \times 100 =\) 70% Gain in Precision.

2. Gain of Optimum over SRS:
Gain = \(\frac{85.0 - 40.0}{40.0} \times 100 = \frac{45.0}{40.0} \times 100 =\) 112.5% Gain in Precision!

Conclusion: By simply organizing the population into strata and using Neyman's optimum formula, the researcher more than doubled the precision of the survey without spending money to increase the sample size \(n\).

4.5 Practice Section: Subjective Questions (20)

Test your deep conceptual understanding and mathematical derivation skills. Formulate your answers before checking the detailed solutions.

Q1. Define Stratified Random Sampling and clearly state the 'Golden Rule' of forming strata.
Solution: Stratified Random Sampling is a method where a heterogeneous population of size \(N\) is sub-divided into \(k\) non-overlapping, distinct subgroups called strata. Then, an independent Simple Random Sample is drawn from each stratum. The 'Golden Rule' of stratification is to construct the strata such that the units within a single stratum are as homogeneous (similar) as possible, while the different strata are as heterogeneous (different) from each other as possible.
Q2. What is the fundamental formula for the Stratified Sample Mean (\(\bar{y}_{st}\))? Prove that it is an unbiased estimator of the Population Mean (\(\bar{Y}\)).
Solution: The formula is \(\bar{y}_{st} = \sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i\), where \(W_i = N_i/N\).
Proof: Take the expectation: \(E(\bar{y}_{st}) = E(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{y}_i)\). Since \(W_i\) are constants, we pull them out: \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i E(\bar{y}_i)\). Since samples are drawn via SRS within each stratum, Theorem 3.3 applies: \(E(\bar{y}_i) = \bar{Y}_i\). Substituting this gives: \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i \bar{Y}_i\). By definition, the weighted sum of stratum means is the overall population mean \(\bar{Y}\). Thus, \(E(\bar{y}_{st}) = \bar{Y}\).
Q3. Why is the variance of the stratified mean \(V(\bar{y}_{st})\) simply the sum of the variances of the individual strata? What property guarantees this?
Solution: The formula \(V(\sum W_i \bar{y}_i) = \sum W_i^2 V(\bar{y}_i)\) holds true entirely because of the property of Independence. By the strict definition of stratified sampling, the sample drawn from the \(i^{th}\) stratum is drawn completely independently of the sample drawn from the \(j^{th}\) stratum. Because they are independent random variables, their covariance is zero. The variance of a sum of independent variables is the sum of their individual variances.
Q4. Define Proportional Allocation. Show how it simplifies the general variance formula (assuming FPC is ignored).
Solution: Proportional Allocation means the sample size drawn from a stratum is proportional to the total size of that stratum. Formula: \(n_i = n W_i\).
The general variance formula (ignoring FPC) is \(V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum W_i^2 \frac{S_i^2}{n_i}\).
Substitute \(n_i = n W_i\) into the denominator: \(V = \sum W_i^2 \frac{S_i^2}{n W_i}\).
One \(W_i\) on top cancels the \(W_i\) on the bottom, simplifying beautifully to: \(V_{prop} = \frac{1}{n} \sum W_i S_i^2\).
Q5. Explain the logical flaw in Proportional Allocation that Neyman's Optimum Allocation fixes.
Solution: Proportional allocation allocates sample size based only on the population size of the stratum (\(N_i\)). Its logical flaw is ignoring the internal variance (\(S_i^2\)). A stratum might be huge, but if every unit inside it is identical (zero variance), we only need a sample of \(n=1\) to know everything about it! Proportional allocation would waste hundreds of samples on it. Neyman's allocation fixes this by allocating based on BOTH size and variance (\(n_i \propto W_i S_i\)), drawing more units only if the stratum is highly variable.
Q6. Write down the formula for Neyman's Optimum Allocation. What does the term \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i\) in the denominator represent?
Solution: The formula is \(n_i = n \frac{W_i S_i}{\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i}\). The denominator \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i S_i\) is simply a normalizing constant. It is the sum of the size-variance products for all strata. Dividing by this sum ensures that when you add up all the allocated \(n_i\)'s across all \(k\) strata, they perfectly sum up to the total allowed sample size \(n\).
Q7. Under what specific condition does Optimum Allocation (Neyman) yield the exact same sample sizes as Proportional Allocation?
Solution: Neyman's allocation simplifies to Proportional allocation if and only if the variance is exactly the same across all strata. If \(S_1 = S_2 = \dots = S_k = S\) (a constant), then in the formula \(n_i = n \frac{W_i S}{\sum W_i S}\), the constant \(S\) can be factored out of the sum. It cancels with the \(S\) in the numerator. Since \(\sum W_i = 1\), the formula collapses to \(n_i = n W_i\), which is Proportional Allocation.
Q8. Outline the algebraic proof showing that \(V_{srs} \ge V_{prop}\). What is the conceptual meaning of this result?
Solution: Using the ANOVA identity, Total Variance \(S^2 \approx \sum W_i S_i^2 + \sum W_i (\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2\). Substituting this into the SRS variance formula \(V_{srs} = S^2/n\) yields: \(V_{srs} = \frac{1}{n}[\sum W_i S_i^2 + \sum W_i (\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2]\). We recognize the first term as exactly \(V_{prop}\). Thus, \(V_{srs} = V_{prop} + \frac{1}{n}\sum W_i (\bar{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2\). Since the second term is a square (always positive), \(V_{srs}\) must be greater than \(V_{prop}\). Conceptually, it proves that proportional stratification mathematically eliminates the error caused by the difference between strata means.
Q9. How does the introduction of varying survey costs (\(c_i\)) change the optimum allocation formula? Explain the logic.
Solution: When costs vary, the allocation formula becomes \(n_i \propto \frac{W_i S_i}{\sqrt{c_i}}\). The logic is driven by mathematical optimization (Lagrange multipliers) to get the most information for a fixed budget. The \(\sqrt{c_i}\) is in the denominator, meaning if a stratum is extremely expensive to survey, the formula heavily penalizes it and drastically reduces the number of samples drawn from it, reallocating budget to cheaper strata to maximize overall precision.
Q10. Discuss three main practical advantages of Stratified Random Sampling over Simple Random Sampling.
Solution: 1. Higher Precision: By ensuring all subgroups are represented, it drastically lowers the sampling variance. 2. Administrative Efficiency: A massive population (like a country) can be divided into strata (states), allowing local field offices to handle their own strata independently. 3. Sub-population Data: It provides highly accurate, standalone estimates for each specific stratum, whereas SRS only provides a reliable estimate for the country as a whole.
Q11. Explain how the method of Lagrange multipliers is used to derive Neyman's optimum allocation formula.
Solution: The goal is to minimize the variance function \(V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum (W_i^2 S_i^2)/n_i\) subject to the constraint that the sum of sample sizes equals \(n\), i.e., \(\sum n_i - n = 0\). We form the Lagrange function \(\Phi = \sum (W_i^2 S_i^2)/n_i + \lambda (\sum n_i - n)\). By taking the partial derivative of \(\Phi\) with respect to \(n_i\) and setting it to zero, we get \(-W_i^2 S_i^2 / n_i^2 + \lambda = 0\). Solving for \(n_i\) yields \(n_i = (W_i S_i) / \sqrt{\lambda}\), which proves that \(n_i\) is directly proportional to \(W_i S_i\). Summing this across all strata gives the normalizing constant to find the exact formula.
Q12. What happens to the efficiency of Stratified Random Sampling if the strata are formed poorly (i.e., heterogeneous within, homogeneous between)?
Solution: If strata are formed poorly, the within-strata variance (\(S_i^2\)) will be very high, and the between-strata variance will be very low. Since the variance of the stratified estimator \(V(\bar{y}_{st})\) depends almost entirely on the within-strata variance, a poorly formed stratification will result in a very high variance. In extreme cases of bad stratification, stratified sampling can actually become less efficient than simple random sampling.
Q13. Show algebraically that \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i = 1\). Why is this important?
Solution: By definition, the weight of the \(i^{th}\) stratum is \(W_i = N_i / N\). When we sum these weights across all \(k\) strata, we get: \(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i = \sum_{i=1}^k (N_i / N)\). Since \(N\) is a constant denominator, we can write this as \((\sum N_i) / N\). By the definition of stratification, the sum of the sizes of all non-overlapping strata exactly equals the total population size \(N\). Therefore, \(N / N = 1\). This is important because it mathematically ensures that our weighted averages are true proportional averages.
Q14. In cost optimization, if the overhead cost \(c_0\) increases, how does it affect the allocation of \(n_i\)?
Solution: The overhead cost \(c_0\) is a fixed constant that does not depend on the sample sizes \(n_i\). In the optimization process (taking derivatives with respect to \(n_i\)), the constant \(c_0\) drops out entirely. Therefore, a change in the fixed overhead cost does not change the optimal *proportion* or *allocation* of the sample across the strata, though it may reduce the total sample size \(n\) you can afford if your overall budget is strictly fixed.
Q15. Define 'Gain in Precision' and explain how to calculate it when comparing Stratified Sampling to SRS.
Solution: Gain in Precision measures how much more efficient a new sampling design is compared to a baseline design (usually SRS). It is calculated using the variances of the estimators. The formula is: Gain = \(((V_{srs} - V_{st}) / V_{st}) \times 100\%\). A positive percentage indicates that the stratified design is more precise (has less variance) than the simple random design for the same sample size.
Q16. What is the impact of ignoring the Finite Population Correction (FPC) when calculating \(V(\bar{y}_{st})\) for a relatively small population?
Solution: If the population is small (or the sampling fraction \(n/N\) is large, typically > 5%), ignoring the FPC will cause you to significantly over-estimate the variance. The FPC, calculated as \((N-n)/N\), reduces the variance because drawing a large portion of a finite population without replacement leaves less uncertainty about the un-sampled portion. Ignoring it makes your results look much worse (less precise) than they actually are.
Q17. Explain the difference between 'Estimation' and 'Allocation' in the context of Stratified Sampling.
Solution: Allocation is the planning step: it is the mathematical process of deciding *how many* sample units to draw from each stratum (\(n_1, n_2\), etc.) before the survey begins, using methods like Proportional or Neyman. Estimation is the analysis step: it is the mathematical process of combining the data *after* the survey is completed to calculate the final Stratified Mean (\(\bar{y}_{st}\)) and its Variance to make inferences about the whole population.
Q18. If a population has extremely skewed income distribution (a few billionaires and many poor people), why is Stratification vastly superior to SRS?
Solution: Income is highly skewed, meaning the population variance (\(S^2\)) is massive. In SRS, a random sample might accidentally include too many billionaires (drastically overestimating the average) or none at all (underestimating). By stratifying into "High Income", "Middle", and "Low", we force the sample to represent the billionaires exactly according to their true proportion. This captures the extreme between-strata variance, reducing the sampling error immensely.
Q19. Can Proportional Allocation ever result in a higher variance than Simple Random Sampling? Justify your answer.
Solution: No, practically and mathematically, Proportional Allocation cannot result in a higher variance than SRS (assuming we ignore the minor effects of finite population correction in small samples). As proven by the ANOVA identity, \(V_{srs} = V_{prop} + \text{positive term}\). Since the added term (representing between-strata variance) must be zero or positive, \(V_{prop}\) is always less than or equal to \(V_{srs}\). The worst-case scenario is that they are exactly equal (if there is no difference between strata means).
Q20. In the cost optimization formula \(n_i \propto \frac{W_i S_i}{\sqrt{c_i}}\), what happens if the cost is identical across all strata (\(c_1 = c_2 = \dots = c_k\))?
Solution: If the cost \(c_i\) is a constant \(c\) for all strata, then the term \(\sqrt{c}\) can be factored out as a constant denominator in the proportionality equation. It will cancel out entirely when we divide by the sum to find the exact \(n_i\). The formula will mathematically collapse into \(n_i \propto W_i S_i\), which is exactly Neyman's Optimum Allocation formula! This proves Neyman's is just a special case of cost optimization where costs are equal.

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Qs)

Q1. The fundamental "Golden Rule" of constructing strata is that units should be:
Correct Answer: (B) Homogeneous within, Heterogeneous between
Reason: This minimizes the within-strata variance \(S_i^2\), which drives down the total stratified variance.
Q2. In stratified sampling, the samples are drawn from the different strata:
Correct Answer: (C) Independently
Reason: Independence guarantees that the covariance between strata means is zero, allowing us to simply sum their variances.
Q3. The symbol \(W_i\) represents the 'Weight' of the \(i^{th}\) stratum. It is calculated as:
Correct Answer: (B) \(N_i / N\)
Reason: Weight is strictly the proportion of the total population that belongs to the stratum.
Q4. Which allocation method ensures that \(n_i\) is directly proportional to \(N_i\)?
Correct Answer: (D) Proportional Allocation
Q5. Neyman's Optimum Allocation minimizes the variance subject to which constraint?
Correct Answer: (B) Fixed total sample size (\(n\))
Reason: It minimizes variance assuming we can only draw \(n\) total units, regardless of cost.
Q6. According to Neyman's allocation (\(n_i \propto W_i S_i\)), we should draw a larger sample from a stratum if it is:
Correct Answer: (A) Large in size and highly variable
Reason: Both high \(W_i\) and high \(S_i\) increase the allocation \(n_i\).
Q7. The Stratified Sample Mean \(\bar{y}_{st}\) is an unbiased estimator of \(\bar{Y}\). This mathematically means:
Correct Answer: (B) \(E(\bar{y}_{st}) = \bar{Y}\)
Q8. In the formula \(n_i \propto \frac{W_i S_i}{\sqrt{c_i}}\), what happens to \(n_i\) if the cost \(c_i\) of surveying that stratum quadruples (x4)?
Correct Answer: (C) It is cut in half
Reason: The square root of 4 is 2. Since cost is in the denominator, dividing by 2 halves the sample size.
Q9. Which of the following inequalities correctly ranks the variance (efficiency) of the estimators?
Correct Answer: (B) \(V_{opt} \le V_{prop} \le V_{srs}\)
Reason: Optimum (Neyman) is the best (lowest variance). SRS is the worst.
Q10. Proportional allocation completely eliminates which component of variance?
Correct Answer: (B) Between-strata variance
Reason: The ANOVA proof shows that \(V_{srs} = V_{prop} + \text{Between Strata Variance}\). Thus, proportional removes it.
Q11. The sum of the weights of all strata (\(\sum_{i=1}^k W_i\)) is always equal to:
Correct Answer: (D) 1
Reason: Because \(W_i = N_i/N\), and the sum of all parts equals the whole (\(N/N = 1\)).
Q12. If strata are formed such that they are highly heterogeneous *within* themselves, the efficiency of stratified sampling will:
Correct Answer: (B) Decrease
Reason: This violates the Golden Rule. High within-strata variance (\(S_i^2\)) directly increases the total variance of the estimator.
Q13. To derive Neyman's optimum allocation, the mathematical technique used to minimize variance under a constraint is called:
Correct Answer: (C) Method of Lagrange Multipliers
Reason: It is the standard calculus method for finding local maxima/minima subject to equality constraints.
Q14. In equal allocation, the sample size \(n_i\) drawn from each stratum is calculated as:
Correct Answer: (B) \(n / k\)
Reason: Total sample size \(n\) is divided equally among the \(k\) strata.
Q15. The formula \(V(\bar{y}_{st}) = \sum W_i^2 V(\bar{y}_i)\) requires that samples from different strata are drawn:
Correct Answer: (C) Independently
Reason: Independence ensures the covariance between sample means of different strata is zero.
Q16. Cost optimization allocation penalizes (reduces sample size from) strata that are:
Correct Answer: (B) Very expensive to survey
Reason: Because cost \(c_i\) is in the denominator of the allocation formula.
Q17. The 'Gain in Precision' when moving from SRS to Stratified Sampling is usually expressed as a:
Correct Answer: (B) Percentage
Reason: It is calculated as \([(V_{srs} - V_{st})/V_{st}] \times 100\%\).
Q18. If a population is extremely skewed (e.g., highly unequal wealth distribution), which method is best?
Correct Answer: (C) Stratified Random Sampling
Reason: It prevents drawing a sample that accidentally contains only the extreme outliers or misses them entirely.
Q19. In Neyman's allocation, the sample size \(n_i\) is directly proportional to:
Correct Answer: (C) The product \(W_i S_i\)
Reason: It considers both the size (\(W_i\)) and the internal variation (\(S_i\)) of the stratum.
Q20. If \(S_1 = S_2 = \dots = S_k\) (constant variance across all strata), then Neyman's allocation mathematically reduces to:
Correct Answer: (D) Proportional Allocation
Reason: The constant \(S\) cancels out of the formula \(n_i = n (W_i S) / \sum(W_i S)\), leaving \(n_i = n W_i\).

Part D: True or False (20 Qs)

1. Stratified random sampling is generally less precise than simple random sampling.
False. It is almost always MORE precise because it ensures all heterogeneous subgroups are represented properly.
2. Strata must be mutually exclusive and non-overlapping.
True. A single unit cannot belong to two different strata; otherwise, probabilities of selection are corrupted.
3. Equal allocation is the most scientifically efficient method of drawing a stratified sample.
False. Equal allocation is highly inefficient unless all strata are identical in size and variance. Neyman's is the most scientifically efficient.
4. Neyman's allocation requires prior knowledge of the standard deviation (\(S_i\)) of each stratum.
True. Because the formula contains \(S_i\), you must estimate it using a pilot survey before applying the formula.
5. If \(S_1 = S_2 = \dots = S_k\), then Neyman's allocation gives the exact same result as Proportional allocation.
True. If variances are constant across all strata, the \(S\) cancels out of the formula, collapsing it to \(n_i = n W_i\).
6. The variance of the stratified mean is calculated by multiplying the variances of the individual strata.
False. Because the samples are drawn independently, we ADD their weighted variances, we do not multiply them.
7. In cost optimization, if a stratum is very cheap to survey, we should draw a smaller sample from it.
False. Because cost \(c_i\) is in the denominator (\(1/\sqrt{c_i}\)), a smaller cost means the fraction becomes LARGER, so we draw MORE samples from cheap areas to maximize information for our budget.
8. \(V_{prop} \le V_{srs}\) is a mathematically proven theorem in sampling.
True. Proportional stratification mathematically guarantees a variance less than or equal to SRS by eliminating between-strata error.
9. Stratification completely eliminates Sampling Error.
False. It reduces it significantly, but sampling error always exists unless you conduct a complete census.
10. The sum of all strata weights (\(\sum W_i\)) must always exactly equal 1.
True. Since \(W_i = N_i/N\), summing all \(N_i\) gives \(N\). Therefore \(N/N = 1\).
11. Independence of drawing samples from different strata is necessary to ensure the covariance between their means is zero.
True. This independence allows us to add the individual variances algebraically without complicated cross-terms.
12. The mathematical formula for optimum allocation under variable cost contains \(c_i\) directly in the numerator.
False. The square root of the cost (\(\sqrt{c_i}\)) is located in the denominator, not the numerator.
13. Proportional allocation implies that \(n_1/N_1 = n_2/N_2 = \dots = n_k/N_k\).
True. This states that the sampling fraction is constant across all strata, which is the definition of proportional allocation.
14. The "Golden Rule" suggests that between-strata variance should be minimized.
False. The Golden Rule says between-strata variance should be MAXIMIZED (heterogeneous between), while within-strata variance should be minimized (homogeneous within).
15. The Lagrange multiplier method is a calculus technique used to minimize variance under constraints.
True. It is the standard mathematical proof method for deriving Neyman's optimum allocation.
16. A Stratified Sample Mean \(\bar{y}_{st}\) is only unbiased if proportional allocation is used.
False. Theorem 4.1 proves it is unbiased REGARDLESS of the allocation method used, as long as SRS is used within each stratum.
17. In the cost function \(C = c_0 + \sum c_i n_i\), the term \(c_0\) affects the optimum ratio of \(n_i\) to \(n_j\).
False. Since \(c_0\) is a constant overhead, its derivative is zero during optimization and does not affect the allocation ratios.
18. The "Gain in Precision" percentage can be negative if stratification is done very poorly.
True. If you group completely dissimilar units together (high within-variance), the stratified variance can actually exceed SRS variance, resulting in a negative gain (a loss of precision).
19. Stratified sampling allows for reliable sub-population estimates, which is impossible to guarantee with pure SRS.
True. SRS might accidentally select zero people from a specific minority group, while stratification guarantees a predetermined sample size from every subgroup.
20. The inequality \(V_{opt} \le V_{prop} \le V_{srs}\) is known as the hierarchy of efficiency.
True. This mathematically ranks the methods from best (Optimum) to worst (SRS) in terms of sampling variance.
