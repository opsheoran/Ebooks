2.1 Understanding Errors in Surveys

In a perfect world, our sample results would match the population exactly. However, in reality, there is always some difference. This difference is called an Error.

Think of it like target practice. If you aim for the bullseye (the population parameter) but hit slightly to the left, that gap is your error. In sampling, these errors come from two main sources:

  1. Sampling Error: Due to the fact that we only looked at a small part of the group.
  2. Non-Sampling Error: Due to mistakes in how we collected, recorded, or processed the data.

2.2 Sampling Error

This error happens because we are using a sample instead of a census. It is naturally built into every sampling plan.

Sampling Error

The difference between the estimate obtained from a sample and the true value that would be obtained if the entire population were studied using the same procedures.

How to reduce it?

The most effective way to reduce sampling error is to increase the sample size. As you study more people, your results get closer to the true population value.

Sampling Error \(\propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\)

This formula tells us that if you want to cut your error in half, you need to make your sample 4 times larger!

2.3 Non-Sampling Error

These errors are much more dangerous because they can happen in both samples and full census counts. They are usually caused by human mistakes.

Common Sources of Non-Sampling Error

  • Non-Response: A person picked for the sample refuses to answer.
  • Measurement Error: The weighing scale is broken, or a student lies about their age.
  • Processing Error: A data entry clerk types "90" instead of "09".

The Danger of Frame Error

If your list of people (Sampling Frame) is old or incorrect (e.g., using a 2010 phone book in 2026), you will have a massive non-sampling error because you are missing everyone who moved or got a new number!

2.4 Bias and Mean Square Error (MSE)

Bias

Bias is a consistent mistake in one direction. If your scale always shows 2kg extra, your results are Biased.

\(Bias = E(t) - \theta\)

If \(Bias = 0\), the estimator is called Unbiased.

Mean Square Error (MSE)

MSE is the best way to measure the total quality of an estimator. It combines both the Variance (how spread out the guesses are) and the Bias (how far the average guess is from the truth).

\(MSE(t) = Variance(t) + (Bias)^2\)

An ideal estimator has low variance (consistent) and zero bias (accurate).

2.5 Practice Exercises

Part A: Subjective Questions

1. Why can non-sampling errors occur even in a census?
2. Explain the relationship between sample size and sampling error.
3. Define Mean Square Error and state its components.

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions

Q1. Which error can be reduced by increasing the sample size?
(a) Non-sampling error    (b) Sampling error    (c) Human error    (d) All of these
Correct Answer: (b) Sampling error
Q2. If the expected value of an estimator equals the population parameter, the bias is:
(a) Positive    (b) Negative    (c) Zero    (d) Infinite
Correct Answer: (c) Zero

Part C: Fill in the Blanks

1. Non-sampling error ___________ as the sample size increases.
Answer: Increases (because there are more opportunities for human mistakes in a larger study).
2. MSE is equal to Variance plus the square of ___________.
Answer: Bias

Part D: True or False

1. Sampling error is always present in a census. [True / False]
Answer: False
Explanation: Sampling error is 0 in a census because n=N.
2. A biased estimator can still be useful if its MSE is very low. [True / False]
Answer: True
