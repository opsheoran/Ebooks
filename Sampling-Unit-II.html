2.1 Introduction: Why Do Errors Happen?

Hello students! In the previous unit, we learned what sampling is and why we do it. But here is a universal truth: No survey is perfect. Whenever you collect data, process it, and analyze it, some mistakes will always happen. In statistics, we call these mistakes Errors.

An "Error" in statistics does not necessarily mean someone did a bad job. Simply put, an error is the gap or difference between the guess we make from our sample, and the true reality of the whole population.

The Two Main Buckets of Error

Every error in a survey can be put into one of two buckets:

  1. Sampling Error: This happens simply because we are looking at a small part (sample) instead of the whole group (population). It is natural and unavoidable.
  2. Non-Sampling Error: This happens because humans make mistakes. Bad planning, wrong answers, or typing errors fall here.

2.2 Sampling Error: The Mathematical Cost

Imagine you have a large bag of mixed pulses (dal). If you take just one handful, you might accidentally pick up more of one type of dal than the other. If you make a guess about the whole bag based on that handful, your guess will be slightly off. This is exactly what a Sampling Error is.

Sampling Error

The error which arises only because we are using a sample to estimate the population parameter is called Sampling Error (or sampling fluctuations). It is inherent in any sampling scheme.

Because we don't study the whole population, our sample estimate will never perfectly match the true population parameter. However, a good sampling plan tries to keep this error as small as possible. A sample with the smallest sampling error is considered a good representative of the population.

How to Reduce Sampling Error?

The most direct way to reduce sampling error is to increase the size of the sample (make 'n' bigger). If you take two handfuls of dal instead of one, your guess becomes better.

Mathematical Rule:
Sampling Error \(\propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\)

This formula is very important. It says that the sampling error is inversely proportional to the square root of the sample size. This means if you want to cut your error in half, you cannot just double the sample size; you have to make your sample four times larger! (Because \(\sqrt{4} = 2\)).

2.3 Non-Sampling Error: The Hidden Danger

Many students think that if they do a complete Census (studying 100% of the population), all errors will disappear. This is completely false! While a census removes sampling error, it creates a massive amount of Non-Sampling Error.

Non-Sampling Error

Errors that occur at any stage of the survey (planning, field work, or tabulation) that are NOT due to the sampling process itself. These errors are mostly human mistakes or equipment failures.

Some main sources of non-sampling errors are:

  • Failure to measure some units (e.g., people not at home).
  • Defective measurement techniques (e.g., using a broken weighing machine).
  • Errors in editing, coding, and tabulating the results (e.g., typing a wrong number in the computer).

The X-Graph Concept

In a small sample, sampling error is high, but non-sampling error is low (because you can closely supervise the small team). As you increase the sample size towards a Census, the sampling error drops to zero, but the non-sampling error rises sharply because it is impossible to properly supervise thousands of workers. Therefore, a large sample survey is often more accurate than a poorly managed census.

2.4 Bias vs. Unbiasedness: A Deep Mathematical Look

To talk about the quality of our guesses, we need mathematical terms. Let us define them simply.

  • Let the true population parameter (the real answer we want) be \(\theta\) (Theta).
  • Let our estimator (the formula we use on our sample to guess \(\theta\)) be \(t\).

What is Bias?

Bias is a consistent mistake in one direction. For example, if a shopkeeper's weighing scale is rigged to always show 100 grams extra, the scale is biased. It doesn't matter how many times you weigh the item; it will always be wrong by 100 grams.

Mathematical Definition of Bias

The difference between the expected value of our estimator \(E(t)\) and the true parameter \(\theta\) is called bias.

Bias \(B(t) = E(t) - \theta\)

Unbiased Estimator

An estimator \(t\) is said to be an unbiased estimator for the parameter \(\theta\) if its expected value exactly equals the parameter. In this case, the Bias is zero.

\(E(t) = \theta\)

If \(E(t) \neq \theta\), then the estimator is called a biased estimator.

2.5 Mean Square Error (MSE): The Ultimate Measure of Quality

How do we know if our estimator \(t\) is good? A good estimator should have two qualities:

  1. It should be very close to the true target \(\theta\) (Low Bias).
  2. If we repeat the sample many times, the answers shouldn't jump around too much (Low Variance).

To measure the total quality of an estimator, we combine both variance and bias into one master formula called the Mean Square Error (MSE).

Mean Square Error (MSE)

The MSE is defined as the expected value of the square of the error (the difference between the estimator and the true parameter).

\(MSE(t) = E[(t - \theta)^2]\)

🖋️ Detailed Derivation: Proving \(MSE(t) = Variance(t) + [Bias(t)]^2\)

Let's do the math step-by-step. Don't worry, it's just simple algebra!

We start with the definition of MSE:

\(MSE(t) = E[(t - \theta)^2]\)

Let's add and subtract \(E(t)\) inside the bracket. This doesn't change the value.

\(MSE(t) = E[\{ (t - E(t)) + (E(t) - \theta) \}^2]\)

Now, let's look at the parts. The term \((E(t) - \theta)\) is exactly our definition of Bias \(B(t)\). Let's expand the square using the formula \((A + B)^2 = A^2 + B^2 + 2AB\).

\(MSE(t) = E[ (t - E(t))^2 + (E(t) - \theta)^2 + 2(t - E(t))(E(t) - \theta) ]\)

We know that Expected Value \(E\) is a linear operator, so we can apply it to each part separately:

\(= E[(t - E(t))^2] + E[(E(t) - \theta)^2] + 2(E(t) - \theta) \cdot E[t - E(t)]\)

Now, let's simplify each part:

  1. \(E[(t - E(t))^2]\) is the exact mathematical definition of Variance, \(V(t)\).
  2. \((E(t) - \theta)\) is a constant value (Bias). The expected value of a constant is just the constant squared. So it becomes \(B^2(t)\).
  3. Look at the last part: \(E[t - E(t)]\). This means \(E(t) - E(E(t))\). Since \(E(t)\) is a constant, \(E(E(t)) = E(t)\). So, \(E(t) - E(t) = 0\). Thus, the entire third term becomes zero!

Putting it all together, we get our beautiful final theorem:

\(MSE(t) = V(t) + B^2(t)\)
Mean Square Error = Variance + (Bias)²

Important Note: If an estimator is perfectly unbiased, then Bias \(B(t) = 0\). In that special case, \(MSE(t) = V(t)\). This is why statisticians love unbiased estimators!

2.6 Detailed Breakdown: Types of Non-Sampling Errors

Non-sampling errors are bad because they distort the data in hidden ways. They occur at any stage: planning, field work, or tabulation. We classify them into three main types.

Type 1: Non-Response Error

These errors happen when we cannot get data from the people we selected. They result from inadequate preparation.

  • Faulty Frame: If you use an old list (like a 10-year-old voter list), you might select dead people, or miss new voters. This is an omission error.
  • Uncooperative Respondents: Sometimes people refuse to answer, or they are not at home. If we just skip them, our data becomes biased because the people who stay home might have different opinions than the people who go to work!
  • Inadequate Schedule: The questionnaire might be too long or confusing, causing people to leave it half empty.

Type 2: Response (Observational) Error

These errors happen during the actual collection of data. The person answers, but the answer recorded is wrong.

  • Bad Supervision: If field workers are not watched, they might fake the data while sitting in a coffee shop!
  • Untrained Staff: An inexperienced worker might ask a question in a way that forces the respondent to lie out of embarrassment.
  • Defective Devices: If you are measuring the height of plants with a stretched or broken tape measure, every single reading will be incorrect.

Type 3: Tabulation (Processing) Error

These errors happen after the data is successfully collected and brought back to the office.

  • Coding Errors: Translating text answers into numbers wrongly (e.g., marking "Male" as 2 instead of 1).
  • Punching/Data Entry Errors: A computer operator typing "5000" instead of "500".
  • Publication Errors: Making mistakes while printing the final results or drawing graphs wrongly.

2.7 Practice Section: Subjective Questions (20)

Test your deep conceptual understanding. Formulate your answers before checking the detailed solutions.

Q1. Define 'Error' in the context of statistics. Does it always mean someone made a mistake?
Solution: In statistics, an 'Error' does not necessarily imply a mistake, fraud, or blunder. It simply refers to the mathematical difference or gap between the estimated value (derived from a sample) and the true, actual value of the population parameter.
Q2. What is Sampling Error? Why is it considered an unavoidable part of sampling?
Solution: Sampling Error is the discrepancy that arises purely because we are examining a fraction (sample) of the population rather than the entire population. It is unavoidable because no matter how perfectly you select a sample, a part cannot perfectly represent the whole in every tiny detail. It is inherent to the process.
Q3. State the mathematical relationship between Sampling Error and Sample Size. Explain what it means practically.
Solution: Sampling Error is inversely proportional to the square root of the sample size ($n$). Formula: Sampling Error $\propto 1/\sqrt{n}$. Practically, this means that to reduce the error to half, you cannot merely double your sample size; you must increase the sample size by four times. It shows diminishing returns on large samples.
Q4. What is Non-Sampling Error? Give two broad reasons why it occurs.
Solution: Non-Sampling Errors are all the errors that occur during the survey process that are not related to the act of sampling itself. They are human or systemic faults. Two broad reasons: 1. Observational errors (defective measurement tools or bad interviewing). 2. Processing errors (wrong data entry or coding mistakes in the office).
Q5. Why does a complete Census often suffer from massive Non-Sampling Errors?
Solution: A census requires gathering data from millions of units. This demands hiring thousands of temporary, often untrained workers. It is administratively impossible to supervise all of them closely. Consequently, workers make mistakes, fake interviews, or rush through questions, leading to massive human (non-sampling) errors.
Q6. Define 'Bias' mathematically. What does a Bias of zero indicate?
Solution: Bias is defined as the difference between the expected value of an estimator ($E(t)$) and the true population parameter ($\theta$). Mathematically: $Bias = B(t) = E(t) - \theta$. A bias of zero indicates that the estimator is 'Unbiased', meaning on average, it perfectly hits the true population target.
Q7. What is Mean Square Error (MSE)? Write its basic definition formula.
Solution: Mean Square Error (MSE) is a comprehensive measure of the quality of an estimator. It measures the average squared difference between the estimated values and the true parameter. The definition formula is: $MSE(t) = E[(t - \theta)^2]$.
Q8. Write down the theorem that connects MSE, Variance, and Bias. Under what condition does MSE equal Variance?
Solution: The theorem states that Mean Square Error is equal to the Variance of the estimator plus the square of its Bias. Formula: $MSE(t) = Variance(t) + [Bias(t)]^2$. MSE equals Variance only when the Bias is exactly zero (i.e., for an unbiased estimator).
Q9. Briefly prove that $E[t - E(t)] = 0$.
Solution: Using the linear properties of expectation: $E[t - E(t)] = E(t) - E[E(t)]$. Since the expected value of an estimator, $E(t)$, is a constant (it's a fixed number representing the long-run average), the expected value of a constant is just the constant itself. So, $E[E(t)] = E(t)$. Therefore, $E(t) - E(t) = 0$.
Q10. Classify Non-Sampling errors into three main types.
Solution: The three main types are: 1. Type 1: Non-Response Error (failure to get data from selected units). 2. Type 2: Response or Observational Error (getting wrong data from the units). 3. Type 3: Tabulation or Processing Error (making mistakes while entering or calculating the collected data).
Q11. What is a 'Type 1: Non-Response Error'? Give a real-life example.
Solution: Non-response error occurs when data cannot be collected from a selected sample unit. Example: An investigator goes to a farmer's house to ask about crop yields, but the farmer is out of town, or simply refuses to answer the door. The missing data creates a bias.
Q12. How does using an old or 'faulty sampling frame' lead to Non-Response errors?
Solution: If a frame is old, it might contain addresses of houses that have been demolished or people who have moved away or died. When investigators try to find these units, they fail, resulting in non-response. Also, new people are completely omitted from the study, causing severe bias.
Q13. Explain 'Response Error' with respect to defective measuring devices.
Solution: Response error happens when the data recorded is factually incorrect. If a scientist uses a thermometer that always reads 2 degrees too high, every single temperature reading recorded will be wrong. This is an observational error caused by defective equipment, totally independent of the sampling method.
Q14. How does the personal bias of an enumerator (interviewer) cause observational errors?
Solution: If an enumerator is untrained or has personal prejudices, they might ask questions in a leading way (e.g., "You don't smoke, do you?"). The respondent feels pressured to agree, giving a false answer. The enumerator might also guess answers instead of asking properly.
Q15. What constitutes a 'Type 3: Tabulation Error'? Provide two examples.
Solution: Tabulation errors occur in the office during data processing, after field work is done. Examples: 1. Data Entry/Punching Error: A clerk typing '100' instead of '10' into the computer. 2. Coding Error: Categorizing a 25-year-old respondent into the "Senior Citizen" age code by mistake.
Q16. If we plot 'Error' against 'Sample Size (n)', what is the behavior of Sampling Error vs Non-Sampling Error?
Solution: As Sample Size (n) increases, the Sampling Error curve drops sharply downward towards zero (at a census). However, as Sample Size increases, the Non-Sampling Error curve rises upward, because larger teams are harder to manage and make more human mistakes. They form an 'X' shape.
Q17. Why is Mean Square Error a better measure of estimator quality than just Variance?
Solution: Variance only measures how consistent or spread out the estimates are, ignoring if they are actually hitting the true target. MSE is better because it accounts for BOTH consistency (Variance) and accuracy (Bias). A highly consistent estimator is useless if it is heavily biased away from the truth.
Q18. Describe a situation where a respondent might intentionally give incorrect information, causing a Response Error.
Solution: If a survey asks highly sensitive, personal, or illegal questions (like illegal drug use, or tax evasion), the respondent is likely to lie to protect themselves from embarrassment or legal trouble. This intentional deception results in a severe Response Error.
Q19. How can proper training of field staff reduce total survey error?
Solution: Proper training ensures enumerators understand the questions, use measuring devices correctly, and know how to politely encourage respondents to answer without leading them. This drastically cuts down Type 1 (Non-response) and Type 2 (Observational) errors, bringing the total error down significantly.
Q20. In the derivation $MSE(t) = E[ (t - E(t))^2 + (E(t) - \theta)^2 + 2(t - E(t))(E(t) - \theta) ]$, why does the middle term $(E(t) - \theta)^2$ become $B^2(t)$?
Solution: By definition, Bias $B(t) = E(t) - \theta$. Therefore, the square of this term is simply the Bias squared, $B^2(t)$. Furthermore, because $E(t)$ is a constant expected value and $\theta$ is a constant parameter, their difference is a constant. The expected value of a constant is the constant itself.

Reason: Formula states Error $\propto 1/\sqrt{n}$.
Q2. Which error is inherently present in every sampling scheme?
Correct Answer: (B) Sampling error
Reason: Because a sample is only a part of the population, mathematical sampling error is unavoidable.
Q3. A mistake made by a clerk while typing survey data into a computer is classified as a:
Correct Answer: (C) Tabulation (Processing) Error
Reason: It happens in the office after field work is completed.
Q4. An estimator $t$ is said to be unbiased for parameter $\theta$ if:
Correct Answer: (D) $E(t) = \theta$
Q5. Bias is mathematically calculated as:
Correct Answer: (B) $E(t) - \theta$
Q6. Mean Square Error (MSE) equals Variance only when:
Correct Answer: (B) The bias is zero
Reason: Because $MSE = Variance + Bias^2$.
Q7. If respondents are not at home when the investigator visits, it causes:
Correct Answer: (A) Non-response Error
Q8. As sample size increases, which of the following typically increases?
Correct Answer: (C) Non-Sampling Error
Reason: Managing a larger team leads to more human mistakes.
Q9. Using a defective weighing scale in a field experiment leads to:
Correct Answer: (D) Observational (Response) Error
Q10. The sum of Sampling Error and Non-Sampling Error is called:
Correct Answer: (B) Total Survey Error
Q11. The term $(t - E(t))^2$ inside an expected value operator defines:
Correct Answer: (C) Variance
Reason: The expected squared deviation from the mean is the definition of variance.
Q12. What happens to sampling error in a complete Census?
Correct Answer: (D) It drops to zero
Reason: When $n=N$, you are no longer taking a sample; there is no sampling error.
Q13. Inadequate preparation and a faulty frame primarily cause:
Correct Answer: (A) Type 1 Non-Response Error
Q14. If a respondent lies about their income because they are embarrassed, it creates a:
Correct Answer: (B) Response (Observational) Error
Q15. The expected value of a constant $C$ is:
Correct Answer: (C) C
Reason: Used in the MSE proof where $E(Bias) = Bias$.
Q16. To cut the sampling error in half, the sample size must be:
Correct Answer: (D) Quadrupled (x4)
Reason: Because error is proportional to $1/\sqrt{n}$. $\sqrt{4} = 2$.
Q17. Which error is generally more dangerous because it's hard to measure mathematically?
Correct Answer: (B) Non-Sampling Error
Reason: It stems from human mistakes which are unpredictable and cannot be formulaically modeled easily.
Q18. $E[t - E(t)]$ always equals:
Correct Answer: (C) 0
Reason: $E(t) - E(E(t)) = E(t) - E(t) = 0$.
Q19. Non-sampling errors can be controlled effectively by:
Correct Answer: (C) Employing trained and qualified personnel
Q20. A highly consistent but highly biased estimator will have:
Correct Answer: (A) Low Variance, High MSE
Reason: Consistency means low variance. High bias drives the MSE up.

Part D: True or False (20 Qs)

1. Sampling error can be completely eliminated by taking a better random sample.
False. Sampling error is inherent. It can be reduced by increasing size, but never completely eliminated unless you do a census.
2. Non-sampling errors occur only in sample surveys, not in census surveys.
False. Non-sampling errors (human mistakes) occur in both. In fact, they are much higher in a large census.
3. If $E(t) \neq \theta$, the estimator is biased.
True. This is the exact mathematical definition of a biased estimator.
4. Mean Square Error is simply another name for Variance.
False. MSE = Variance + Bias². They are only the same if the bias is zero.
5. Doubling the sample size reduces the sampling error by half.
False. Because error is inversely proportional to the square root of $n$, you must quadruple (multiply by 4) the size to cut the error in half.
6. Using an old voter list from 10 years ago as a sampling frame will lead to non-response errors.
True. Because many people on the list will have moved or died, the investigators will fail to measure them.
7. Observational errors (Type 2) are caused by the mathematical formula used for estimation.
False. Observational errors are caused by human mistakes during data collection, like asking bad questions or using broken measuring tapes.
8. Tabulation errors happen in the office after the fieldwork is finished.
True. Errors like typing mistakes (data entry) or wrong coding happen during processing.
9. A biased estimator is completely useless in statistics.
False. If an estimator has a very tiny bias but an extremely low variance, it might have a smaller total MSE than an unbiased estimator. It can still be useful.
10. Bias is a random error that changes direction (positive or negative) with every sample.
False. Bias is a systematic, consistent error in one direction (like a rigged weighing scale that always reads heavy).
11. A sample can have a smaller Total Error than a complete Census.
True. The massive reduction in human (non-sampling) errors from using a small, expert team often makes the sample more accurate overall.
12. The mathematical expression $E[t - E(t)]$ always evaluates to zero.
True. Since $E(t)$ is a constant, $E(E(t)) = E(t)$, making $E(t) - E(t) = 0$.
13. In the derivation of MSE, the cross-product term $2(t - E(t))(E(t) - \theta)$ becomes zero upon taking expectation.
True. Because $(E(t) - \theta)$ is a constant, we pull it out, leaving the expectation of $(t - E(t))$, which is zero.
14. Increasing the sample size ($n$) will usually decrease Non-Sampling Error.
False. Increasing the sample size usually INCREASES non-sampling error because a larger team is harder to supervise.
15. A respondent lying out of embarrassment causes a Sampling Error.
False. This is a classic example of a Response (Observational) Error, which is a type of Non-Sampling Error.
16. The expected value of a constant is the constant itself.
True. This is a basic rule of mathematical expectation used in the MSE proof.
17. Bias is calculated by adding the expected value of the estimator to the true parameter.
False. Bias is calculated by SUBTRACTING the true parameter from the expected value: $E(t) - \theta$.
18. The symbol $\propto$ stands for "is proportional to".
True. It is used in the relationship between error and sample size.
19. Type 1 Non-Response errors can be completely ignored if the sample is very large.
False. Ignoring non-respondents creates severe bias because they might have systematically different characteristics from those who responded.
20. The "X-Graph Concept" shows that as sample size increases, sampling error goes down, but non-sampling error goes up.
True. This crossing behavior creates an 'X' shape on a graph, illustrating the trade-off in survey design.
