3.1 Introduction: The "Fair Lottery" of Sampling

Hello students! Now that we understand what populations, samples, and errors are, we need to learn exactly how to pick our sample. The most fundamental, pure, and simple method in all of statistics is called Simple Random Sampling (SRS).

You can think of SRS as a perfectly fair lottery. In a fair lottery, every single ticket has the exact same chance of winning. Nobody gets special treatment. In statistics, this means every single unit in our population has an equal mathematical probability of being selected for the sample.

Simple Random Sampling (SRS)

Simple random sampling is a method of selecting \(n\) units out of a population of size \(N\) by giving equal probability of selection to all units. It ensures that every possible combination of \(n\) units that can be formed from the population has the exact same chance of being chosen.

There are two slightly different ways to conduct this fair lottery. The difference depends on what you do with the slip of paper after you pick it.

1. Simple Random Sampling With Replacement (SRSWR)

Imagine you have a bowl with the names of all 100 students in your class. You close your eyes, pick a slip of paper, write down the name, and then—this is the important part—you put the slip back into the bowl before picking the next name. You mix it up again and pick the second name.

Because you put the slip back (replaced it), the bowl always has 100 names in it for every single draw. This means the exact same student could be picked twice, three times, or even ten times in the same sample!

2. Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement (SRSWOR)

Now, imagine the same bowl. You pick a name, write it down, but this time you keep the slip outside the bowl (do not replace it). Now the bowl only has 99 names left for the second draw. The third draw has 98 names, and so on.

In this method, a student can only be picked once. Every person in your final sample of 10 students will be uniquely different.

Which one is better in real life?

In practical field surveys (like interviewing farmers or checking blood samples), we almost always use SRSWOR (Without Replacement). Why waste our limited budget asking the exact same farmer the exact same questions twice? We want fresh, new information with every draw.

Solved Example 1: Possible Samples (SRSWR vs SRSWOR)

Problem: Consider a small population of 4 units, say \( \{A, B, C, D\} \). We want to draw a sample of size \(n=2\). Calculate the total number of possible samples and list them for both SRSWR and SRSWOR.

Solution:

Case 1: SRSWR (With Replacement)
Number of possible samples = \(N^n = 4^2 = 16\).
The samples are:
(A,A), (A,B), (A,C), (A,D)
(B,A), (B,B), (B,C), (B,D)
(C,A), (C,B), (C,C), (C,D)
(D,A), (D,B), (D,C), (D,D)

Case 2: SRSWOR (Without Replacement)
Number of possible samples = \(\binom{N}{n} = \binom{4}{2} = \frac{4!}{2!(4-2)!} = \frac{4 \times 3}{2 \times 1} = 6\).
The samples are:
(A,B), (A,C), (A,D), (B,C), (B,D), (C,D)
Notice how (A,A) is impossible, and order doesn't matter so (A,B) is the same as (B,A).

3.2 Core Theorems: Probabilities of Selection

We said SRS is a "fair lottery". But as students of statistics, we must prove it mathematically. Let's look at two beautiful theorems that form the heart of Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement (SRSWOR).

Theorem 3.1: Probability of Inclusion

In SRSWOR, the probability of a specific unit being included in the sample of size \(n\) is equal to \(\frac{n}{N}\).

🖋️ Detailed Proof of Theorem 3.1

Let the population size be \(N\) and the sample size be \(n\). Let's focus on one specific unit, say Mr. Sharma.

In SRSWOR, how many total possible samples of size \(n\) can we make from \(N\) people? Using combinations, the total number of possible samples is \(\binom{N}{n}\).

Now, how many of those samples will actually contain Mr. Sharma? If we force Mr. Sharma to be in the sample, we have already picked 1 person. Now we only need to pick the remaining \((n-1)\) people from the remaining \((N-1)\) population.

So, the number of samples containing our specific unit is \(\binom{N-1}{n-1}\).

Therefore, the Probability = (Favorable Outcomes) / (Total Outcomes):

\[ P = \frac{\binom{N-1}{n-1}}{\binom{N}{n}} \] \[ P = \frac{\frac{(N-1)!}{(n-1)!(N-n)!}}{\frac{N!}{n!(N-n)!}} \] \[ P = \frac{(N-1)!}{(n-1)!} \times \frac{n(n-1)!}{N(N-1)!} = \frac{n}{N} \]

This proves that the chance is completely fair and proportional to the sample size!

Solved Example 2: Verifying Theorem 3.1

Problem: From our previous example (Population: A, B, C, D; \(N=4\)), we drew a sample of \(n=2\) using SRSWOR. The total samples were 6: {(A,B), (A,C), (A,D), (B,C), (B,D), (C,D)}. Calculate the empirical probability that unit 'C' is included in the sample, and verify Theorem 3.1.

Solution:
Total possible samples = 6.
Count how many samples contain 'C': (A,C), (B,C), (C,D). There are exactly 3 favorable samples.
Empirical Probability = Favorable / Total = \(3 / 6 = 1/2\).

Using Theorem 3.1 formula: Probability = \(n/N = 2/4 = 1/2\).
Conclusion: The empirical result matches the theorem perfectly. The probability of inclusion is indeed \(n/N\).

Theorem 3.2: Probability at Any Draw

The probability that a specified unit of the population is selected at any given draw is equal to the probability of its being selected at the very first draw (which is \(1/N\)).

🖋️ Detailed Proof of Theorem 3.2

Let's say we want to find the probability that Mr. Sharma is selected exactly on the \(r^{th}\) draw. For this to happen, he must not be selected in the first \((r-1)\) draws, and then he must be selected on the \(r^{th}\) draw.

Let \(E_r\) be the event that the specified unit is selected at the \(r^{th}\) draw.

\[ P(E_r) = P(\text{Not selected in 1st}) \times P(\text{Not selected in 2nd} \mid \text{not in 1st}) \times \dots \times P(\text{Selected in } r^{th} \mid \text{not in previous}) \]

\[ P(E_r) = \left( \frac{N-1}{N} \right) \times \left( \frac{N-2}{N-1} \right) \times \dots \times \left( \frac{N-(r-1)}{N-(r-2)} \right) \times \left( \frac{1}{N-(r-1)} \right) \]

Notice the beautiful mathematical pattern here! The numerator of one fraction cancels out the denominator of the next fraction. \((N-1)\) cancels \((N-1)\), \((N-2)\) cancels \((N-2)\), and finally \((N-(r-1))\) cancels \((N-(r-1))\).

When everything cancels out, what is left on top? Just the number 1. What is left on the bottom? Just \(N\).

\[ P(E_r) = \frac{1}{N} \]

Thus, \(P(E_r) = P(E_1)\). The chance of being picked on the 5th draw is exactly the same as being picked on the 1st draw. This proves the method is perfectly random and fair at every single step.

3.3 Practical Procedures: How to Actually Do It

The math is beautiful, but how does a researcher sitting in a village actually pick a Simple Random Sample? Since the whole theory is based on "randomness," we cannot just pick numbers from our head (humans are terrible at being truly random). We use two main procedures.

1. The Lottery Method

This is the oldest and simplest method. You associate a ticket or chit with each unit of the population. If there are 50 students, you take 50 identical slips of paper, number them 1 to 50, fold them exactly the same way, and put them in a bowl or a rotating drum. You mix them thoroughly and blindly draw out \(n\) slips.

When it fails: The lottery method is great for small populations (like 50 students). But what if your population is 10,000 farmers? Writing 10,000 slips, folding them identically, and finding a bowl large enough to mix them is physically exhausting and practically impossible. Furthermore, human bias creeps in (we don't mix perfectly). For large populations, we use Random Number Tables.

2. Random Number Tables Method

Statisticians have created huge books filled with pages of digits (0-9) generated by computers or special machines to be completely, perfectly random. These are called Random Number Tables. The most famous ones are:

  • Tippett’s Random Number Tables
  • Fisher and Yates Tables
  • Kendall and Smith Tables
  • A Million Random Digits (by the RAND Corporation)

How to use the tables (Three Approaches)

A. The Remainder Approach

If population size \(N\) is a \(d\)-digit number, we read \(d\)-digit numbers from the table. We find the highest \(d\)-digit multiple of \(N\). If a drawn number is higher than this multiple, we reject it. Otherwise, we divide the random number by \(N\) and the remainder indicates the selected unit.

Solved Example 3: The Remainder Method

Problem: A village has \(N = 75\) households. We need a sample of \(n=5\). We read the following 2-digit random numbers from a table: 48, 92, 12, 85, 77, 03, 75, 41. Use the Remainder Method to select the 5 households.

Solution:
\(N = 75\) (2 digits). The highest 2-digit multiple of 75 is \(75 \times 1 = 75\). (Note: \(75 \times 2 = 150\), which is 3 digits).
Because the highest multiple is 75, any random number greater than 75 must be rejected.
Let's evaluate the drawn numbers:
- 48: Less than 75. \(48 \div 75 = 0\) with remainder 48. (Select House 48).
- 92: Greater than 75. Reject.
- 12: Less than 75. \(12 \div 75 = 0\) with remainder 12. (Select House 12).
- 85: Greater than 75. Reject.
- 77: Greater than 75. Reject.
- 03: Less than 75. \(03 \div 75 = 0\) with remainder 3. (Select House 3).
- 75: Exactly 75. \(75 \div 75 = 1\) with remainder 0. (Rule: Remainder 0 means select the last unit, House 75).
- 41: Less than 75. \(41 \div 75 = 0\) with remainder 41. (Select House 41).

Final Sample: Households 48, 12, 3, 75, and 41.

B. The Quotient Approach

If \(N\) is a \(d\)-digit number, let \(N'\) be the highest \(d\)-digit multiple of \(N\). Calculate the quotient \(q = N'/N\). We divide the random number by \(q\). The selected unit is the (Quotient of this division) + 1.

Solved Example 4: The Quotient Method

Problem: Population \(N = 18\). Pick a sample of \(n=3\) from the following 2-digit random numbers: 65, 99, 42, 18, 11.

Solution:
\(N = 18\). The highest 2-digit multiple is \(N' = 18 \times 5 = 90\).
The division quotient \(q = 90 / 18 = 5\).
Reject any random number greater than 90.

- 65: Valid (less than 90). Divide by \(q=5\): \(65 \div 5 = 13\). (Selection = \(13 + 1 =\) 14).
- 99: Greater than 90. Reject.
- 42: Valid. \(42 \div 5 = 8\) (with remainder 2). We only care about the quotient (8). (Selection = \(8 + 1 =\) 9).
- 18: Valid. \(18 \div 5 = 3\) (with remainder 3). (Selection = \(3 + 1 =\) 4).

Final Sample: Units 14, 9, and 4.

C. Independent Choice of Digits

This is the most straightforward method. If \(N = 112\) households, you just read 3-digit numbers straight from the table. If you read 086, you select house 86. If you read 345 (which is greater than 112), you simply reject it and read the next number. You keep rejecting until you get valid numbers below 112. The only problem here is you might reject a lot of numbers!

3.4 Estimation Math & Important Theorems

The ultimate goal of drawing a sample is to estimate the population's true parameters. Let's establish our notations first. Remember, Capital letters are for the Population, and small letters are for the Sample.

Notation Guide

  • \(N\): Total population size.
  • \(n\): Sample size.
  • \(Y_i\): The value of the characteristic for the \(i^{th}\) unit in the Population.
  • \(y_i\): The value of the characteristic for the \(i^{th}\) unit in the Sample.
  • \(\bar{Y}\): Population Mean \(= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} Y_i\)
  • \(\bar{y}_n\) or \(\bar{y}\): Sample Mean \(= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i\)
  • \(S^2\): Population Mean Square (Variance) \(= \frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2\)
  • \(s^2\): Sample Mean Square (Sample Variance) \(= \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \bar{y})^2\)

Now, let us prove that our sample formulas are "good" (unbiased) estimators of the population parameters. This is the heavy math, but I will explain it step-by-step.

Theorem 3.3: Sample Mean is Unbiased

In simple random sampling without replacement (SRSWOR), the sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean. Mathematically: \(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\).

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 3.3

We know the sample mean is \(\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i\). Let's take the expected value of both sides:

\[ E(\bar{y}) = E \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i \right) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} E(y_i) \]

By definition, the expected value of any specific sample observation \(y_i\) is just the sum of all population values multiplied by their probability of selection. Since each population unit has an equal probability \(1/N\) of being drawn at the \(i^{th}\) position (thanks to Theorem 3.2!):

\[ E(y_i) = \sum_{j=1}^{N} Y_j \cdot P_j = \sum_{j=1}^{N} Y_j \left(\frac{1}{N}\right) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} Y_j = \bar{Y} \]

Substitute this back into our first equation:

\[ E(\bar{y}) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (\bar{Y}) \]

Summing the constant \(\bar{Y}\), exactly \(n\) times gives \(n \cdot \bar{Y}\).

\[ E(\bar{y}) = \frac{1}{n} (n \cdot \bar{Y}) = \bar{Y} \]

Hence proved! The sample mean perfectly targets the population mean.

Solved Example 5: Verifying Unbiasedness of Sample Mean (UG Standard)

Problem: A population consists of 5 children with the following weights in kg: 10, 14, 18, 22, 26. A sample of size \(n=2\) is drawn without replacement (SRSWOR). Prove empirically that the sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean.

Solution:
Step 1: Calculate Population Mean (\(\bar{Y}\))
\(N = 5\). Weights: \(Y_1=10, Y_2=14, Y_3=18, Y_4=22, Y_5=26\).
\(\bar{Y} = \frac{10+14+18+22+26}{5} = \frac{90}{5} = 18\).

Step 2: List all possible samples of size \(n=2\) and their means (\(\bar{y}\))
Total samples = \(\binom{5}{2} = 10\).
1. (10, 14) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 12\)
2. (10, 18) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 14\)
3. (10, 22) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 16\)
4. (10, 26) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 18\)
5. (14, 18) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 16\)
6. (14, 22) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 18\)
7. (14, 26) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 20\)
8. (18, 22) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 20\)
9. (18, 26) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 22\)
10. (22, 26) \(\rightarrow \bar{y} = 24\)

Step 3: Calculate the Expected Value of Sample Mean (\(E(\bar{y})\))
\(E(\bar{y})\) is the average of all possible sample means.
\(E(\bar{y}) = \frac{12+14+16+18+16+18+20+20+22+24}{10} = \frac{180}{10} = 18\).

Conclusion: Since \(E(\bar{y}) = 18\) and \(\bar{Y} = 18\), we have empirically proven that \(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\). The estimator is strictly unbiased.

Theorem 3.4: Sample Variance is Unbiased

In simple random sampling without replacement (SRSWOR), the sample mean square (\(s^2\)) is an unbiased estimator of the population mean square (\(S^2\)). Mathematically: \(E(s^2) = S^2\).

🖋️ Detailed Proof of Theorem 3.4

This is a big proof, but if we go slowly, it is very logical. We start with the definition of sample variance:

\[ s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \left( \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^2 - n \bar{y}^2 \right) \]

Let's take the Expected Value of both sides:

\[ E(s^2) = \frac{1}{n-1} \left[ E\left(\sum_{i=1}^n y_i^2\right) - n E(\bar{y}^2) \right] \]

To solve this, we introduce a clever trick called an indicator variable, \(a_i\).
Let \(a_i = 1\) if the \(i^{th}\) population unit is selected in the sample, and \(0\) otherwise. Because we are picking \(n\) units from \(N\), the chance of \(a_i\) being 1 is \(\frac{n}{N}\). So, \(E(a_i) = \frac{n}{N}\).

Now we can rewrite the sum of sample squares using the population values and the indicator:

\[ E\left(\sum_{i=1}^n y_i^2\right) = E\left(\sum_{i=1}^N a_i Y_i^2\right) = \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 E(a_i) = \frac{n}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 \]

Next, we need to find \(E(\bar{y}^2)\). We know that \(\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^N a_i Y_i\). Squaring this gives:

\[ \bar{y}^2 = \frac{1}{n^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^N a_i Y_i \right)^2 = \frac{1}{n^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^N a_i^2 Y_i^2 + \sum_{i \neq j}^N a_i a_j Y_i Y_j \right) \]

Since \(a_i\) is only 0 or 1, squaring it doesn't change it (\(a_i^2 = a_i\)). Also, the chance of picking BOTH unit \(i\) and unit \(j\) is \(E(a_i a_j) = \frac{n(n-1)}{N(N-1)}\).

\[ E(\bar{y}^2) = \frac{1}{n^2} \left( \frac{n}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 + \frac{n(n-1)}{N(N-1)} \sum_{i \neq j}^N Y_i Y_j \right) \]

We use a standard algebra trick: \(\sum_{i \neq j}^N Y_i Y_j = (N\bar{Y})^2 - \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2\). Substituting this in and doing some basic fraction math gives us:

\[ E(\bar{y}^2) = \frac{N-n}{nN(N-1)} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 + \frac{(n-1)N}{n(N-1)} \bar{Y}^2 \]

Now, substitute everything back into our very first equation for \(E(s^2)\):

\[ E(s^2) = \frac{1}{n-1} \left[ \frac{n}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 - n \left( \frac{N-n}{nN(N-1)} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 + \frac{(n-1)N}{n(N-1)} \bar{Y}^2 \right) \right] \]

After multiplying the \(-n\) and factoring out \(\sum Y_i^2\), the algebra beautifully simplifies to:

\[ E(s^2) = \frac{1}{n-1} \left[ \frac{n-1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 - \frac{(n-1)N}{N-1} \bar{Y}^2 \right] \]

Cancel the \((n-1)\) from everywhere:

\[ E(s^2) = \frac{1}{N-1} \left( \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 - N\bar{Y}^2 \right) \]

And by definition, the right side is exactly the population variance \(S^2\)!

\[ E(s^2) = S^2 \]

This proves that dividing by \((n-1)\) instead of \(n\) when calculating sample variance is mathematically required to make it unbiased.

Solved Example 6: Verifying Unbiasedness of Sample Variance

Problem: Using the exact same population as Example 5 (Weights: 10, 14, 18, 22, 26 with \(\bar{Y}=18\)), verify that \(E(s^2) = S^2\).

Solution:
Step 1: Calculate Population Mean Square (\(S^2\))
\(S^2 = \frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^N (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2\)
\(S^2 = \frac{1}{5-1} [ (10-18)^2 + (14-18)^2 + (18-18)^2 + (22-18)^2 + (26-18)^2 ]\)
\(S^2 = \frac{1}{4} [ (-8)^2 + (-4)^2 + 0^2 + 4^2 + 8^2 ] = \frac{1}{4} [64 + 16 + 0 + 16 + 64] = \frac{160}{4} = 40\).

Step 2: Calculate Sample Variance (\(s^2\)) for all 10 possible samples
Recall \(s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2\). Since \(n=2\), the denominator is 1. The formula simplifies to \(s^2 = (y_1 - \bar{y})^2 + (y_2 - \bar{y})^2\).
1. (10, 14), \(\bar{y}=12 \rightarrow s^2 = (10-12)^2 + (14-12)^2 = 4 + 4 = 8\)
2. (10, 18), \(\bar{y}=14 \rightarrow s^2 = (10-14)^2 + (18-14)^2 = 16 + 16 = 32\)
3. (10, 22), \(\bar{y}=16 \rightarrow s^2 = (10-16)^2 + (22-16)^2 = 36 + 36 = 72\)
4. (10, 26), \(\bar{y}=18 \rightarrow s^2 = (10-18)^2 + (26-18)^2 = 64 + 64 = 128\)
5. (14, 18), \(\bar{y}=16 \rightarrow s^2 = (14-16)^2 + (18-16)^2 = 4 + 4 = 8\)
6. (14, 22), \(\bar{y}=18 \rightarrow s^2 = (14-18)^2 + (22-18)^2 = 16 + 16 = 32\)
7. (14, 26), \(\bar{y}=20 \rightarrow s^2 = (14-20)^2 + (26-20)^2 = 36 + 36 = 72\)
8. (18, 22), \(\bar{y}=20 \rightarrow s^2 = (18-20)^2 + (22-20)^2 = 4 + 4 = 8\)
9. (18, 26), \(\bar{y}=22 \rightarrow s^2 = (18-22)^2 + (26-22)^2 = 16 + 16 = 32\)
10. (22, 26), \(\bar{y}=24 \rightarrow s^2 = (22-24)^2 + (26-24)^2 = 4 + 4 = 8\)

Step 3: Calculate the Expected Value of Sample Variance (\(E(s^2)\))
\(E(s^2) = \frac{8+32+72+128+8+32+72+8+32+8}{10} = \frac{400}{10} = 40\).

Conclusion: Since \(E(s^2) = 40\) and \(S^2 = 40\), we have numerically proven that \(E(s^2) = S^2\). The estimator is unbiased.

Theorem 3.5: Variance of the Sample Mean

How much does the sample mean jump around from sample to sample? In SRSWOR, the variance of the sample mean is given by:

\[ V(\bar{y}_n) = \left( \frac{N-n}{Nn} \right) S^2 = \left( 1 - \frac{n}{N} \right) \frac{S^2}{n} \]

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 3.5

We know that Variance is defined as: \(V(\bar{y}) = E(\bar{y}^2) - [E(\bar{y})]^2\).

We just found \(E(\bar{y}^2)\) in the previous proof, and we know \(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\). Let's plug them in:

\[ V(\bar{y}) = \left[ \frac{N-n}{nN(N-1)} \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 + \frac{(n-1)N}{n(N-1)} \bar{Y}^2 \right] - \bar{Y}^2 \]

Combine the \(\bar{Y}^2\) terms using a common denominator:

\[ \frac{(n-1)N - n(N-1)}{n(N-1)} \bar{Y}^2 = \frac{nN - N - nN + n}{n(N-1)} \bar{Y}^2 = \frac{-(N-n)}{n(N-1)} \bar{Y}^2 \]

Now factor out the common \(\frac{N-n}{nN(N-1)}\) term:

\[ V(\bar{y}) = \frac{N-n}{nN(N-1)} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 - N \bar{Y}^2 \right] \]

Since the term in the brackets divided by \((N-1)\) is exactly \(S^2\), we get:

\[ V(\bar{y}) = \frac{N-n}{nN} S^2 \]

Which is often written as \((1 - f)\frac{S^2}{n}\). Proved!

Crucial Remarks on Theorem 3.5:

  • Sampling Fraction (\(f\)): The ratio \(n/N\) is called the sampling fraction. It tells you what percentage of the population you sampled.
  • Finite Population Correction (FPC): The term \((1 - f)\) or \(\frac{N-n}{N}\) is called the FPC.
  • If the population \(N\) is very, very large (infinite) compared to the sample \(n\), then \(n/N \to 0\). The FPC becomes \((1 - 0) = 1\). The formula simplifies beautifully to just \(\frac{S^2}{n}\). This is the standard formula taught in basic statistics! The FPC is only used when we sample a large chunk of a small, finite population.

Solved Example 7: Estimating Totals and Standard Error

Problem: In a village of \(N=1000\) farms, a random sample of \(n=50\) farms is surveyed for wheat yield. The sample mean yield is \(\bar{y} = 120\) quintals with a sample variance of \(s^2 = 400\).
(i) Estimate the total wheat yield of the village.
(ii) Calculate the Standard Error (S.E.) of the sample mean.

Solution:
(i) Estimating the Total Yield
The estimated total \(\hat{Y} = N \times \bar{y}\).
\(\hat{Y} = 1000 \times 120 = 1,20,000\) quintals.

(ii) Standard Error of Sample Mean
First, we need the Variance of the mean: \(V(\bar{y}) = \left( \frac{N-n}{N} \right) \frac{S^2}{n}\).
Since we don't know the population \(S^2\), we use our unbiased estimator \(s^2\) instead.
\(V(\bar{y}) = \left( \frac{1000-50}{1000} \right) \times \frac{400}{50}\)
\(V(\bar{y}) = \left( \frac{950}{1000} \right) \times 8 = 0.95 \times 8 = 7.6\).
Standard Error is the square root of Variance: \(S.E.(\bar{y}) = \sqrt{7.6} \approx 2.76\) quintals.

3.5 Simple Random Sampling for Attributes (Proportions)

So far, we talked about measuring things with numbers (like crop yield in kg, or height in cm). These are called Variables. But what if we are counting qualities that don't have a numerical measurement? For example, whether an item is "Defective" or "Not Defective", or if a person is "Employed" or "Unemployed". These qualitative characteristics are called Attributes.

We cannot calculate an average height for attributes. Instead, we calculate a Proportion (a percentage).

Notation for Proportions

Let Population \(N\) be divided into two classes: Class A (has the attribute, e.g., Defective) and Class A' (does not have it). Let the total number of items in Class A be \(A\).

  • Population Proportion of Success (\(P\)): \(P = A / N\)
  • Population Proportion of Failure (\(Q\)): \(Q = 1 - P\)

We draw a sample of size \(n\). Let \(a\) be the number of items in the sample that belong to Class A.

  • Sample Proportion of Success (\(p\)): \(p = a / n\)
  • Sample Proportion of Failure (\(q\)): \(q = 1 - p\)

Theorem 3.6: Unbiasedness of Sample Proportion

The sample proportion '\(p\)' is an unbiased estimate of the population proportion '\(P\)'. Mathematically: \(E(p) = P\).

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 3.6

We can use a clever trick. Let's assign a numerical value to our attributes. Let \(y_i = 1\) if the unit has the attribute (success), and \(y_i = 0\) if it doesn't (failure).

Then the sum of all population values is exactly the number of successes: \(\sum_{i=1}^N Y_i = A\).

The population mean is \(\bar{Y} = A/N = P\).

Similarly, the sample sum is \(\sum_{i=1}^n y_i = a\). The sample mean is \(\bar{y} = a/n = p\).

Since we already proved in Theorem 3.3 that the sample mean is unbiased (\(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\)), it directly follows that \(E(p) = P\). Proved!

Theorem 3.7: Variance of Sample Proportion

In simple random sampling without replacement (SRSWOR), the variance of the sample proportion \(p\) is:

\[ V(p) = \frac{N-n}{N-1} \cdot \frac{PQ}{n} \]

🖋️ Proof of Theorem 3.7

We know from Theorem 3.5 that \(V(\bar{y}) = \frac{N-n}{nN} S^2\).

Let's calculate \(S^2\) when our variables are only 0s and 1s. We know \(S^2 = \frac{1}{N-1} (\sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 - N\bar{Y}^2)\).

Since \(Y_i\) is either 0 or 1, squaring it doesn't change it: \(Y_i^2 = Y_i\). Therefore, \(\sum_{i=1}^N Y_i^2 = \sum_{i=1}^N Y_i = A = NP\).

Substitute this back: \(S^2 = \frac{1}{N-1} (NP - N P^2) = \frac{N}{N-1} P(1-P)\).

Since \((1-P) = Q\), we get \(S^2 = \frac{N}{N-1} PQ\).

Now, substitute this special \(S^2\) back into our variance formula:

\[ V(p) = \frac{N-n}{nN} \left( \frac{N}{N-1} PQ \right) = \frac{N-n}{N-1} \frac{PQ}{n} \]

If the population \(N\) is very large, the FPC \(\frac{N-n}{N-1}\) becomes almost 1, and the formula simplifies to the famous basic statistics formula: \(V(p) \approx \frac{PQ}{n}\). Proved!

Solved Example 8: Estimating Proportion and Error

Problem: In a manufacturing batch of \(N=500\) bulbs, a quality inspector draws a random sample of \(n=50\) bulbs without replacement. He finds that 4 bulbs are defective. Estimate the true proportion of defective bulbs in the population and calculate the standard error of this proportion.

Solution:
Step 1: Estimate Population Proportion
Sample size \(n=50\). Defective items in sample \(a=4\).
Sample Proportion \(p = a/n = 4/50 = 0.08\) (or 8%).
Since \(E(p) = P\), our best estimate for the true population proportion \(P\) is 0.08.

Step 2: Calculate Standard Error
We use the variance formula: \(V(p) = \frac{N-n}{N-1} \cdot \frac{pq}{n-1}\) (using sample estimates \(p\) and \(q\) for unknown parameters).
We know \(p = 0.08\), so \(q = 1 - 0.08 = 0.92\).
\(V(p) = \left( \frac{500 - 50}{500 - 1} \right) \times \frac{0.08 \times 0.92}{50 - 1}\)
\(V(p) = \left( \frac{450}{499} \right) \times \frac{0.0736}{49}\)
\(V(p) = 0.9018 \times 0.0015 = 0.00135\)
Standard Error = \(\sqrt{V(p)} = \sqrt{0.00135} \approx 0.0367\) (or 3.67%).

Solved Example 9: Estimating Total Attributes

Problem: A survey of 200 households in a village of 1200 shows that 150 own a tractor. Estimate the total number of households owning a tractor in the entire village.

Solution:
\(N = 1200\), \(n = 200\), \(a = 150\).
Sample Proportion \(p = 150/200 = 0.75\) (75%).
Estimated Total possessing attribute = \(N \times p = 1200 \times 0.75 = 900\) households.

3.6 Determination of Sample Size

The most ticklish and frequently asked question in survey planning is: "How large should the sample 'n' be?"

If the sample is too small, your estimates will be wild and inaccurate. If it is too large, you are wasting huge amounts of money and resources. A researcher must calculate an optimum sample size using a logical, mathematical process.

Factors Influencing Sample Size:

  • Nature of universe: If the population is highly homogenous (all units are very similar), a very small sample is enough. If it is heterogeneous (widely varying), a large sample is required to capture the diversity.
  • Number of classes proposed: If you want to break down your final report into many small subgroups (e.g., analyzing income not just by state, but by district and village), you need a much larger total sample to ensure each small subgroup has enough people in it.
  • Standard of accuracy (Precision): How much error is acceptable? If you need pinpoint accuracy, you must pay for a massive sample.
  • Availability of finance: Ultimately, the size is constrained by the budget. High accuracy costs high money.

The Mathematical Approach (Based on Precision & Confidence)

A researcher must specify two things to the statistician:

  1. Acceptable Error (\(e\)): The maximum difference allowed between the sample mean and true mean (e.g., "I want my guess to be within \(\pm 3\) rupees of the true mean").
  2. Confidence Level (\(z\)): How sure do you want to be? Usually, we use 95% confidence, which gives a standard normal variate value of \(z = 1.96\) from the Z-tables.

The confidence interval formula is: \(\bar{x} \pm z \frac{\sigma_p}{\sqrt{n}}\)

The error '\(e\)' is the part added/subtracted: \(e = z \frac{\sigma_p}{\sqrt{n}}\)

By squaring both sides and rearranging to solve for \(n\), we get the master formula for an infinite population:

\[ n = \frac{z^2 \sigma_p^2}{e^2} \] Where \(\sigma_p\) is the population standard deviation (usually guessed from a small pilot survey).

Solved Example 10: Infinite Population Sample Size

Problem: A researcher wants to estimate the average yield of wheat in a massive agricultural zone (assume infinite population). The estimated standard deviation is 10 quintals. The researcher wants to be 95% confident (\(z=1.96\)) that his estimate is within 2 quintals of the true average. Calculate the required sample size.

Solution:
Given: \(\sigma_p = 10\), \(e = 2\), \(z = 1.96\).
Formula: \(n = \frac{z^2 \sigma_p^2}{e^2}\)
\(n = \frac{(1.96)^2 \times (10)^2}{(2)^2}\)
\(n = \frac{3.8416 \times 100}{4} = \frac{384.16}{4} = 96.04\).
We always round up for safety. Therefore, the required sample size is 97 farms.

If the population is finite (size \(N\)), we must include the Finite Population Multiplier. The formula adjusts to:

\[ n = \frac{z^2 \cdot N \cdot \sigma_p^2}{(N-1)e^2 + z^2 \sigma_p^2} \]

This ensures you don't accidentally calculate a sample size that is larger than the actual population itself!

Solved Example 11: Finite Population Sample Size

Problem: Recalculate Example 10, but assume the agricultural zone is actually a very small, specific village containing only \(N=200\) farms.

Solution:
Given: \(N = 200\), \(\sigma_p = 10\), \(e = 2\), \(z = 1.96\).
Formula: \(n = \frac{z^2 \cdot N \cdot \sigma_p^2}{(N-1)e^2 + z^2 \sigma_p^2}\)
Numerator: \((1.96)^2 \times 200 \times 100 = 3.8416 \times 20000 = 76832\).
Denominator: \((199 \times 4) + (3.8416 \times 100) = 796 + 384.16 = 1180.16\).
\(n = 76832 / 1180.16 = 65.10\).
Round up. The required sample size is 66 farms.
(Notice how the finite population correction drastically reduced our required work from 97 down to 66!)

Solved Example 12: Sample Size for Proportions

Problem: A political surveyor wants to estimate the proportion of voters supporting candidate A with a margin of error of 4% (\(e=0.04\)) at 95% confidence (\(z=1.96\)). Since she has no idea what the proportion is, she assumes maximum variance (where \(P=0.5\) and \(Q=0.5\)). What sample size is required from a very large population?

Solution:
For proportions, the variance term \(\sigma_p^2\) is replaced by \(PQ\).
Formula: \(n = \frac{z^2 PQ}{e^2}\)
\(n = \frac{(1.96)^2 \times 0.5 \times 0.5}{(0.04)^2}\)
\(n = \frac{3.8416 \times 0.25}{0.0016} = \frac{0.9604}{0.0016} = 600.25\).
Round up. The required sample size is 601 voters.

3.7 Practice Section: Subjective Questions (20)

Test your deep conceptual understanding. Formulate your answers before checking the detailed solutions.

Q1. Clearly explain the difference between Simple Random Sampling With Replacement (SRSWR) and Without Replacement (SRSWOR).
Solution: In SRSWR, after a unit is selected and recorded, it is returned to the population before the next draw. This means the population size remains constant (\(N\)) at every draw, and a single unit can be selected multiple times in the same sample. In SRSWOR, a selected unit is kept out of the population for subsequent draws. The population shrinks by one at each step (\(N\), \(N-1\), \(N-2\)...), ensuring that all \(n\) units in the final sample are unique and distinct. SRSWOR is practically preferred as it yields more information for the same sample size.
Q2. Define Simple Random Sampling (SRS). Why is it compared to a 'fair lottery'?
Solution: SRS is a sampling method where every possible sample of size 'n' drawn from a population of size 'N' has an identical, equal probability of being selected. It is compared to a fair lottery because, just like every lottery ticket has an equal mathematical chance of being pulled from a well-mixed drum, every individual unit in the population has an equal mathematical chance (\(n/N\)) of being included in an SRS, eliminating all human bias.
Q3. State Theorem 3.1 regarding the probability of a specific unit being included in a sample.
Solution: Theorem 3.1 states that in Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement (SRSWOR), the probability that any specific, specified unit from the population is included in a sample of size \(n\) is equal to the sampling fraction, which is \(n/N\).
Q4. Briefly prove Theorem 3.2: The probability of a unit being selected at the \(r^{th}\) draw is \(1/N\).
Solution: Let \(E_r\) be the event of selection at the \(r^{th}\) draw. The unit must NOT be selected in the first \((r-1)\) draws, and then selected on the \(r^{th}\) draw.
\(P(E_r) = (\frac{N-1}{N}) \times (\frac{N-2}{N-1}) \times \dots \times (\frac{N-(r-1)}{N-(r-2)}) \times (\frac{1}{N-(r-1)})\).
Through sequential algebraic cancellation, every numerator cancels with the subsequent denominator, leaving only \(1\) in the numerator and \(N\) in the denominator. Thus, \(P(E_r) = 1/N\), exactly the same as the first draw.
Q5. Describe the Lottery Method of selecting an SRS. What are its major practical limitations?
Solution: The Lottery Method involves assigning a number to each of the \(N\) population units, writing these numbers on identical slips of paper, folding them identically, placing them in a bowl, mixing thoroughly, and blindly drawing \(n\) slips.
Limitations: It is practically impossible and physically exhausting to use for very large populations (e.g., \(N=10,000\)). Furthermore, human bias easily creeps in because it is very difficult to thoroughly mix thousands of slips of paper, leading to non-random clustering.
Q6. Name four standard Random Number Tables commonly used in statistics.
Solution: 1. Tippett’s Random Number Tables. 2. Fisher and Yates Tables. 3. Kendall and Smith Tables. 4. A Million Random Digits (published by the RAND Corporation).
Q7. Explain the 'Remainder Approach' for using random number tables with an example where \(N=123\).
Solution: Since \(N=123\) is a 3-digit number, we look at 3-digit numbers in the table. The highest multiple of 123 below 999 is \(984\). We pick a random 3-digit number from the table. If we pick \(287\), we divide it by \(N\) (123). The quotient is 2, and the remainder is \(41\). Therefore, we select the population unit with serial number 41. (If the remainder is 0, we select unit 123. If we pick a number above 984, like 985, we reject it).
Q8. How does the 'Quotient Approach' differ from the Remainder Approach? Give an example for \(N=16\).
Solution: In the Quotient Approach, we divide by the quotient \(q\), not the population size \(N\). For \(N=16\) (2 digits), the highest multiple is 96. So \(N' = 96\), and quotient \(q = 96/16 = 6\). We pick a random 2-digit number, say 65. We divide 65 by the quotient \(q=6\). \(65 \div 6 = 10\) with remainder 5. We take the quotient of this division (10) and add 1. So, \(10+1=11\). Unit number 11 is selected.
Q9. What do the notations \(\bar{Y}\), \(\bar{y}\), \(S^2\), and \(s^2\) represent?
Solution:
\(\bar{Y}\) = Population Mean (Parameter).
\(\bar{y}\) = Sample Mean (Statistic).
\(S^2\) = Population Mean Square / Variance (Parameter).
\(s^2\) = Sample Mean Square / Variance (Statistic). Capital letters denote the entire population, while lowercase letters denote the sample.
Q10. State Theorem 3.3 regarding the Sample Mean. Why is this theorem foundational to inferential statistics?
Solution: Theorem 3.3 states that in Simple Random Sampling Without Replacement (SRSWOR), the sample mean \(\bar{y}\) is an unbiased estimator of the population mean \(\bar{Y}\). Mathematically, \(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\). This is foundational because it guarantees that our sample averages, over the long run, perfectly center on the true population average without systematic bias.
Q11. Explain what the Finite Population Correction (FPC) is. When can it be safely ignored?
Solution: The FPC is the term \((1 - f)\) or \(\frac{N-n}{N}\) found in the variance formula \(V(\bar{y}) = (1 - f) \frac{S^2}{n}\). It adjusts the variance downward because sampling without replacement from a finite population leaves less uncertainty. It can be safely ignored when the population \(N\) is infinitely large or extremely large compared to sample \(n\), making the sampling fraction \(f = n/N\) approach zero, so the FPC becomes \((1-0) = 1\).
Q12. Write the formula for the Variance of the Sample Mean in SRSWOR and clearly identify all terms.
Solution: \(V(\bar{y}_n) = \frac{N-n}{Nn}S^2\) or \(V(\bar{y}_n) = (1-f)\frac{S^2}{n}\).
Terms: \(N\) is population size, \(n\) is sample size. \(S^2\) is the population variance. \(\frac{N-n}{N}\) is the Finite Population Correction factor.
Q13. Differentiate between a 'Variable' and an 'Attribute' in statistics with examples.
Solution: A Variable is a characteristic that can be measured numerically and quantitatively (e.g., crop yield in kg, height in cm, income in rupees). An Attribute is a qualitative characteristic that cannot be measured numerically but only classified into categories (e.g., gender, literacy status (literate/illiterate), quality status (defective/non-defective)).
Q14. In sampling for attributes, how do we define the sample proportion of success '\(p\)'? What is it an unbiased estimator of?
Solution: If a sample of size \(n\) contains \(a\) items possessing the desired attribute (success), the sample proportion is \(p = a/n\). According to Theorem 3.6, the sample proportion \(p\) is an unbiased estimator of the true population proportion \(P\) (where \(P = A/N\)). Thus, \(E(p) = P\).
Q15. Write the formula for the variance of the sample proportion \(V(p)\) under SRSWOR.
Solution: Under SRSWOR, the variance of the proportion estimator is \(Var(p) = \frac{N-n}{N-1} \cdot \frac{PQ}{n}\). Here, \(\frac{N-n}{N-1}\) acts as the finite population correction, \(P\) is population proportion of success, \(Q\) is proportion of failure (\(1-P\)), and \(n\) is sample size.
Q16. What are the two primary mathematical inputs required from a researcher to determine the required sample size (\(n\))?
Solution: 1. Acceptable Error / Precision (\(e\)): The maximum tolerable deviation between the sample estimate and the true parameter (e.g., \(\pm 5\) units).
2. Confidence Level (\(z\)): The degree of certainty required that the estimate falls within that error margin, expressed as a standard normal variate (e.g., 95% confidence gives \(z = 1.96\)).
Q17. Write the formula to determine the sample size \(n\) for estimating a mean in an infinite population.
Solution: For an infinite population, the required sample size is given by: \(n = \frac{z^2 \sigma_p^2}{e^2}\). Where \(z\) is the standard normal variate for the confidence level, \(\sigma_p\) is the estimated population standard deviation, and \(e\) is the acceptable margin of error.
Q18. How does the nature of the universe (Homogeneous vs Heterogeneous) affect the required sample size?
Solution: The nature of the universe is represented by its variance (\(\sigma^2\)). If the universe is highly homogeneous (all units are very similar), the variance is low, and a very small sample is sufficient to capture the characteristics accurately. If the universe is highly heterogeneous (widely varying), the variance is large, demanding a much larger sample size to ensure representation and desired precision.
Q19. Why must the formula for sample size determination be adjusted when dealing with a finite population?
Solution: For a finite population of size \(N\), sampling without replacement significantly reduces the variance. If we use the infinite population formula, it might result in calculating a required sample size \(n\) that is absurdly large—perhaps even larger than the entire population \(N\) itself! We must apply the finite population multiplier to adjust the calculation downward to realistic bounds.
Q20. Prove algebraically that the expected value of a sample observation \(E(y_i)\) equals the population mean \(\bar{Y}\).
Solution: By definition of expectation in probability, \(E(y_i) = \sum (Value) \times Probability\). The possible values that the \(i^{th}\) sample draw can take are the population values \(Y_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_N\). By Theorem 3.2, the probability of selecting any specific unit \(Y_j\) at the \(i^{th}\) draw is exactly \(1/N\). Therefore, \(E(y_i) = \sum_{j=1}^{N} Y_j \cdot (\frac{1}{N}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} Y_j\). By definition, this sum divided by \(N\) is the population mean \(\bar{Y}\). Thus, \(E(y_i) = \bar{Y}\).

True. This is a crucial mathematical step used to prove that the sample mean is an unbiased estimator.

18. The sample variance \(s^2\) calculated using denominator \(n-1\) is a biased estimator of \(S^2\).
False. Theorem 3.4 proves that using \(n-1\) makes \(s^2\) an EXACTLY unbiased estimator. \(E(s^2) = S^2\).
19. If budget constraints are tight, a researcher should just reduce the sample size without worrying about the precision formulas.
False. Reducing \(n\) arbitrarily will destroy the precision and confidence level of the study, rendering the entire survey mathematically useless.
20. The primary advantage of SRS over Subjective Sampling is the complete elimination of investigator bias.
True. By letting mathematical random tables choose the units, human bias is removed, making the results scientifically valid.
