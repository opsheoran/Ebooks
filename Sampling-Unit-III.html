3.1 Basic Concepts of SRS

Simple Random Sampling (SRS) is the most fundamental and commonly used method of selecting a sample. In this method, every unit in the population has an equal chance of being selected.

Think of it as a fair lottery. If you have 100 students and you want to pick 10, each student should have exactly a 10% chance of being chosen. No one is preferred over anyone else.

SRS with Replacement (SRSWR)

Imagine you pick a numbered slip from a hat, write down the number, and then put the slip back into the hat before picking the next one. This means the same unit could be picked more than once!

SRS without Replacement (SRSWOR)

Here, once a slip is picked, it is kept out. You only pick from the remaining slips. In this method, every unit in the sample is unique.

In practice, SRSWOR is almost always preferred because it provides more information for the same sample size (since you don't repeat units).

3.2 Practical Selection Methods

How do we actually perform SRS in the field? There are two main ways:

1. The Lottery Method

This is the classic way. You assign a number to every unit in the population, write these numbers on identical slips of paper, mix them in a bowl, and blindly draw 'n' slips.

2. Random Number Tables

For large populations (like 10,000 people), slips of paper are impractical. Instead, we use standard tables of random digits (like Tippett’s or Fisher’s tables).

How to use a Random Table:

  1. Assign numbers 001 to 500 to your population of 500 farmers.
  2. Pick a starting point in the random table.
  3. Read three-digit numbers (since 500 has 3 digits).
  4. If the number is between 001 and 500, pick that farmer. If it's over 500, skip it and move to the next.

3.3 Estimation of Population Mean

Our main goal is to use the sample mean (\(\bar{y}\)) to guess the true population mean (\(\bar{Y}\)).

Theorem: Unbiasedness of Sample Mean

In SRSWOR, the sample mean \(\bar{y}\) is an unbiased estimator of the population mean \(\bar{Y}\).

\(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\)

Simplified Logic: Because every unit has an equal chance (\(n/N\)) of being in the sample, if we were to take every possible sample of size 'n' and average their means, the final answer would be exactly the population mean.

3.4 Sampling Variance

How much does our sample mean vary from sample to sample? This is measured by the Sampling Variance.

\(V(\bar{y}) = \left(1 - f\right) \frac{S^2}{n}\)

Where:

  • \(f = n/N\) is called the Sampling Fraction.
  • \((1 - f)\) is called the Finite Population Correction (FPC).
  • \(S^2\) is the population mean square.

If the population is very large (N is huge), the FPC \((1-f)\) becomes close to 1 and can be ignored. This simplifies our formula to \(V(\bar{y}) = S^2/n\).

3.5 Determining Sample Size

The most common question from researchers is: "How many people do I need to study?"

To answer this, you must decide two things:

  1. Precision (e): How much error can you tolerate? (e.g., \(\pm 5\) units).
  2. Confidence (z): How sure do you want to be? (Usually 95%, where \(z = 1.96\)).
\(n = \frac{z^2 \sigma^2}{e^2}\)

This formula gives the required size for an infinite population. For finite populations, we adjust this value downwards.

Part A: Subjective Questions

1. Compare SRSWR and SRSWOR. Which one is more efficient and why?
2. Explain the step-by-step procedure of picking a sample using Random Number Tables.
3. What is the role of the Finite Population Correction (FPC) in variance calculation?

Part B: Multiple Choice Questions

Q1. In SRSWOR of size 'n' from 'N', the probability of a specific unit being in the sample is:
(a) \(1/N\)    (b) \(n/N\)    (c) \(1/n\)    (d) \((N-1)/N\)
Correct Answer: (b) \(n/N\)
Q2. The ratio \(n/N\) is known as the:
(a) Sampling Correction    (b) Sampling Interval    (c) Sampling Fraction    (d) Bias
Correct Answer: (c) Sampling Fraction

Part C: Fill in the Blanks

1. In SRS, the sample mean is an ___________ estimator of the population mean.
Answer: Unbiased
2. As the sample size 'n' increases, the sampling variance ___________.
Answer: Decreases

Part D: True or False

1. In SRSWR, a single population unit can appear multiple times in the sample. [True / False]
Answer: True
2. The lottery method is very convenient for very large populations. [True / False]
Answer: False
Explanation: For large populations, random number tables or computers are much better.
