7.1 Introduction

"At thirty man suspects himself a fool;
Knows it at forty, and reforms his plan:
At fifty chides his infamous delay,
Pushes his prudent purpose to resolve,
In all the magnanimity of thought
Resolves, and re-resolves: then dies the same."
-- J. Young

Hello students! Just like ratio estimators, linear regression estimators use extra information (auxiliary variable) to make our results more accurate. We have already seen that the ratio estimator gives a precise estimate of the population mean if the relationship is linear and the straight line passes through the origin (starting point). But what happens when the regression is linear and the line does not pass through the origin? In that case, it is much better to use estimators based on linear regression.

In simple words, if our study variable (\( y \)) is roughly equal to a constant plus a multiple of the auxiliary variable (\( x \)), fitting a linear regression gives a more precise estimate of the population mean or total. We call such an estimator a regression estimator. Now, let's discuss its main features and see how it compares with the mean per unit and ratio estimators.

Hello students! Ratio estimators ki tarah hi, linear regression estimators bhi hamare results ko zyada accurate banane ke liye extra information (auxiliary variable) ka use karte hain. Humne dekha tha ki agar relationship linear ho aur line origin (starting point) se pass kare, to ratio estimator population mean ka bahut precise estimate deta hai. Lekin jab regression linear ho aur line origin se pass nahi karti, to linear regression par based estimators use karna behtar hota hai.

Simple words mein samjhein to, agar hamara study variable (\( y \)) extra variable (\( x \)) ke multiple aur ek constant ke barabar hai, to linear regression fit karne se population mean ya total ka zyada precise estimate milta hai. Aise estimator ko hum regression estimator kehte hain. Aaiye iske main features discuss karein aur iski tulna mean per unit aur ratio estimators se karein.

7.2 Difference Estimator

Suppose we have two related variables, \( y \) and \( x \). We want to estimate the population mean \( \bar{Y} \). If we take a simple random sample and get the unbiased sample estimators \( \bar{y} \) and \( \bar{x} \) (for \( \bar{Y} \) and \( \bar{X} \)), we can improve our estimate of \( \bar{y} \) by introducing a difference function. So, a simple difference estimator is defined as:

We adjust the simple sample mean by the difference between the known population mean and sample mean of x:
$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$

This formula assumes that for a 1 unit change in \( x \), there is exactly a 1 unit change in \( y \) (meaning they have equal variances). But this assumption isn't valid if the relationship looks like \( y = cx + k \), where \( c \) and \( k \) are constants. In that situation, we use a more general difference estimator:

We multiply the difference by a known constant 'c':
$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$

Here, \( c \) and \( \bar{X} \) are known quantities. Let's look at some important theorems about this difference estimator in simple random sampling.

Theorem 7.2.1

In simple random sampling without replacement (wor), the difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \) is unbiased, and its sampling variance is given by:

The variance combines the variances of both variables and their covariance:
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{y}) + c^2V(\bar{x}) - 2c\text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x}) $$
We can express this using the correlation coefficient \( \rho \):
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{y})(1 + \alpha^2 - 2\alpha\rho) $$

Where \( \rho \) is the correlation coefficient between \( y \) and \( x \), and \( \alpha = cS_x/S_y \).

From this result, we can clearly see that \( \bar{y}_D \) has a smaller variance than the simple mean \( \bar{y} \) as long as \( \rho > \alpha/2 \).

Corollary

We want to find the value of \( c \) that gives the minimum variance. The optimum value is found by differentiating the variance with respect to \( c \).

The variance is minimum when \( c \) is equal to the regression coefficient \( \beta \):
$$ c = \frac{\text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x})}{V(\bar{x})} = \beta $$

This proves that the best value for \( c \) is \( \beta \), the regression coefficient of \( y \) on \( x \). When we use \( \beta \), our minimum value of variance becomes \( V(\bar{y})(1 - \rho^2) \).

Theorem 7.2.2

In simple random sampling (wor), let's formally calculate the sampling variance of \( \bar{y}_D \) for some specified quantity \( c \):

$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + c^2S_x^2 - 2c \rho S_y S_x) $$

Proof:

First, let's show it is unbiased. Since \( c \) is a given constant and we know \( E(\bar{x}) = \bar{X} \), the expectation of \( \bar{y}_D \) is simply \( \bar{Y} \). Therefore, it is unbiased. Now, to find the variance, let's denote a new variable for each unit:

Let's define \( u_i \):
$$ u_i = y_i - cx_i $$
Then the sample mean is:
$$ \bar{u} = \bar{y} - c\bar{x} $$
So our estimator becomes:
$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{u} + c\bar{X} $$
Since \( c\bar{X} \) is a constant, the variance of \( \bar{y}_D \) is just the variance of \( \bar{u} \):
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{u}) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_u^2 $$
Now, let's expand \( S_u^2 \):
$$ S_u^2 = \frac{1}{N - 1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(u_i - \bar{U})^2 = \frac{1}{N - 1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}[(y_i - \bar{Y}) - c(x_i - \bar{X})]^2 $$
Expanding the square, we get:
$$ S_u^2 = S_y^2 + c^2S_x^2 - 2c\rho S_yS_x $$

This proves the theorem!

Corollary 1 & 2

For the special case where \( c = 1 \), the variance of the difference estimator is exactly the same as the first order approximation of the ratio estimator's variance. Also, the difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \) will be more precise than the mean per unit estimator \( \bar{y} \) if \( \rho > cS_x / 2S_y \).

This means if we can guess \( c \) correctly from a pilot survey, the difference estimator is very simple and efficient to apply because its results are exact.

Corollary 3

In simple random sampling (wor), we can estimate this variance from our sample using an unbiased estimator:

We replace the population parameters with sample statistics:
$$ v(\bar{y}_D) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(s_y^2 + c^2s_x^2 - 2cs_{yx}) $$

7.2 Difference Estimator (अंतर अनुमानक)

Maan lijiye hamare paas do related variables hain, \( y \) aur \( x \). Hum population mean \( \bar{Y} \) estimate karna chahte hain. Agar hum simple random sample lein aur unbiased estimators \( \bar{y} \) aur \( \bar{x} \) calculate karein, to hum ek difference function use karke \( \bar{y} \) ko improve kar sakte hain. Ek simple difference estimator ko is tarah define kiya jata hai:

$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$

Ye formula assume karta hai ki \( x \) mein 1 unit change aane par \( y \) mein bhi exactly 1 unit change aayega. Lekin ye assumption tab valid nahi hota jab relationship \( y = cx + k \) jaisa ho, jahan \( c \) aur \( k \) constants hain. Is situation mein hum ek generalized difference estimator use karte hain:

$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$

Yahan \( c \) aur \( \bar{X} \) known (pehle se pata) values hain.

Theorem 7.2.1

Simple random sampling (wor) mein, difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \) unbiased hota hai, aur iska sampling variance hota hai:

$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{y}) + c^2V(\bar{x}) - 2c\text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x}) $$
Correlation coefficient \( \rho \) ke terms mein:
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{y})(1 + \alpha^2 - 2\alpha\rho) $$

Jahan \( \alpha = cS_x/S_y \).

Is result se hum dekh sakte hain ki \( \bar{y}_D \) ka variance \( \bar{y} \) se kam hoga agar \( \rho > \alpha/2 \) ho.

Corollary (उपप्रमेय)

\( V(\bar{y}_D) \) ki minimum value tab aayegi jab \( c \) ko regression coefficient \( \beta \) ke barabar rakha jaye:

$$ c = \frac{\text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x})}{V(\bar{x})} = \beta $$

Tab minimum variance ki value \( V(\bar{y})(1 - \rho^2) \) ho jayegi.

Theorem 7.2.2 & Proof

Sampling variance ka calculation aur proof bilkul waise hi kiya jata hai jaise English section mein bataya gaya hai. Hum ek naya variable \( u_i = y_i - cx_i \) assume karke variance nikalte hain. Agar hum population values ki jagah sample values (\( s_y^2, s_x^2, s_{yx} \)) rakh dein, to humein unbiased sample variance estimator mil jata hai (Corollary 3).

7.3 Regression Estimator

While discussing the difference estimator, we saw that the best value for \( c \) is \( \beta \) (the regression coefficient of \( y \) on \( x \)). But generally, \( \beta \) is not known in advance! So we estimate its value from the sample itself. If we have values of \( y \) and \( x \) for each unit in the sample, a least square estimate of \( \beta \) is denoted by \( b \):

The sample regression coefficient is:
$$ b = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Using this, the linear regression estimators for the population mean \( \bar{Y} \) and population total \( Y \) are given by:

Regression estimator for mean:
$$ \bar{y}_l = \bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$
Regression estimator for total:
$$ \widehat{Y}_l = N[\bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x})] $$

Since \( b \) is a random variable calculated from the sample, exact mathematical expressions for the bias and variance are difficult to derive. Instead, we use large sample approximations.

7.3.1 Bias of Regression Estimator

The regression estimator is consistent (as sample size grows to population size, it hits the exact value). However, it is slightly biased because we estimate \( b \) taking the ratio of two estimates, and it involves multiplying two random variables (\( b \) and \( \bar{x} \)). But don't worry, the bias is usually tiny and decreases as the sample gets bigger.

Theorem 7.3.1

In simple random sampling, the bias of \( \bar{y}_l \) is approximated by:

The bias relates to the covariance of the sample mean and the regression coefficient:
$$ B(\bar{y}_l) \cong -\text{Cov}(\bar{x},b) $$

Proof Logic: Let's define \( \bar{x} = \bar{X} + \epsilon_1 \) and \( b = \beta + \epsilon_2 \), where expectations of \( \epsilon_1 \) and \( \epsilon_2 \) are zero. If we substitute these into our estimator equation, we find the expected value of \( \bar{y}_l - \bar{Y} \) is \( -E(\epsilon_1 \epsilon_2) \), which is exactly \( -\text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b) \). For large samples, this covariance drops towards zero.

7.3.2 Sampling Variance of Regression Estimator

When the sample size is large enough, the bias becomes practically zero. We can then derive the sampling variance by ignoring higher-order terms.

Theorem 7.3.2

In simple random sampling, the large sample variance of the regression estimator is given by:

Just like the difference estimator with optimal \( c \):
$$ V(\bar{y}_l) = V(\bar{y}) + \beta^2 V(\bar{x}) - 2\beta \text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x}) $$

This proves that if regression is linear and \( b \) is the least square estimate, the regression estimator \( \bar{y}_l \) is more precise than any simple difference estimator. In fact, we can simplify this using correlation \( \rho \):

$$ V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) $$

If \( y \) and \( x \) are perfectly linearly related (\( \rho = 1 \) or \( -1 \)), the variance becomes zero! If they are completely uncorrelated (\( \rho = 0 \)), it just reduces to the variance of the simple mean per unit estimator. To estimate this variance from the sample data, we use the following almost unbiased formula:

Sample estimator of variance:
$$ v(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n(n - 2)}\left[ \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 - \frac{(\sum (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x}))^2}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \right] $$

(Note: Standard regression theory suggests using \( n - 2 \) as the divisor instead of \( n - 1 \) for a better estimate).

7.3 Regression Estimator (प्रतिगमन अनुमानक)

Difference estimator padhte time humne dekha ki \( c \) ki best value \( \beta \) hoti hai. Lekin real life mein \( \beta \) pehle se nahi pata hota! Isliye hum sample se iski value estimate karte hain, jise least square estimate \( b \) kehte hain:

$$ b = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Ise use karke population mean \( \bar{Y} \) ka regression estimator hota hai:

$$ \bar{y}_l = \bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$

Kyunki \( b \) ek sample-based random variable hai, iske exact bias aur variance nikalna thoda mushkil hota hai. Isliye hum large sample approximations use karte hain.

7.3.1 Bias (पूर्वाग्रह)

Regression estimator consistent hota hai, lekin slightly biased hota hai kyunki isme do variables ka product use hota hai. Par achhi baat ye hai ki large samples mein ye bias bahut chhota ho jata hai. Bias ka formula lagbhag ye hota hai:

$$ B(\bar{y}_l) \cong -\text{Cov}(\bar{x},b) $$

7.3.2 Sampling Variance

Large samples mein jab bias zero ho jata hai, tab regression estimator ka variance ye hota hai:

$$ V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) $$

Ye formula clear dikhata hai ki agar variables perfectly correlated hon (\( \rho = 1 \)), to variance zero ho jayega! Aur agar bilkul correlation nahi hai (\( \rho = 0 \)), to ye simple mean estimator ke barabar ho jayega. Sample data se ise estimate karne ke liye hum ek formula ka use karte hain, jahan denominator mein \( n - 2 \) lagana behtar mana jata hai.

7.4 Comparison with the Mean per unit and Ratio Estimators

Let's do a quick battle of the estimators! For a large sample size, here are the variances we are comparing:

  • Mean per unit: \( V(\bar{y}) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2 \)
  • Ratio: \( V(\bar{y}_R) \cong \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + R^2S_x^2 - 2R\rho S_yS_x) \)
  • Regression: \( V(\bar{y}_l) \cong \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \)

Compared to Mean per unit: The regression estimator's variance is always smaller, unless \( \rho = 0 \) (where they become equal). The higher the correlation \( \rho \), the bigger the reduction in variance.

Compared to Ratio Estimator: The variance of the regression estimator is less than that of the ratio estimator if \( (\rho S_y - RS_x)^2 > 0 \). This is mathematically ALWAYS true unless \( \rho S_y = RS_x \), which means \( \beta = R \). In that one special situation, both have the same variance, and this only happens when the true regression line is a straight line passing exactly through the origin.

Rules for choosing the right estimator:

  1. If you know an appropriate value of \( c \) in advance, the difference estimator gives good results with very simple calculations.
  2. If correlation \( \rho \cong \frac{S_x}{S_y} \), the difference estimator with \( c=1 \) works just as precisely as the regression estimator.
  3. If correlation \( \rho \cong \frac{C_x}{C_y} \), the ratio estimator will give equally precise results as the regression estimator.
  4. If the regression line does not pass through the origin (\( \beta \neq R \)), ALWAYS prefer the regression estimator.
  5. Only avoid the regression estimator if the computations are too heavy, time-consuming, or expensive, and the small gains aren't worth the cost.

Example 7.1

Using data to estimate the total number of milch animals in 117 villages, we had: \(\bar{X} = 1032, \bar{x} = 1007, \bar{y} = 899, b = 0.81\).

Regression Estimate of Total: \( \widehat{Y}_l = 117[899 + 0.81(1032 - 1007)] = 107554 \)

When we calculate the relative precision of the regression estimate over the simple mean per unit estimate, we get an impressive 416.74%! And its precision over the ratio estimate is 102.58%. This proves regression is the most efficient!

7.4 Comparison (तुलना)

Chaliye sabhi estimators ki tulna karte hain. Large sample size ke liye:

Mean per unit se tulna: Regression estimator ka variance hamesha kam hota hai, siwaye tab jab \( \rho = 0 \) ho (tab dono barabar hote hain). Correlation jitni zyada hogi, variance utna kam hoga.

Ratio Estimator se tulna: Regression estimator ratio se hamesha behtar hota hai, siwaye ek case ke: jab regression line seedhe origin (zero) se pass kare (\( \beta = R \)). Uss case mein dono same result dete hain.

Sahi Estimator chunne ke Rules:

  1. Agar \( c \) ki value pehle se pata ho, to simple calculations ke sath difference estimator use karein.
  2. Agar \( \rho \cong \frac{C_x}{C_y} \) ho, to ratio estimator bilkul regression jitna precise result dega.
  3. Agar line origin se pass nahi hoti (\( \beta \neq R \)), to hamesha regression estimator choose karein.
  4. Agar regression ke calculations bahut complex lag rahe hon aur time zyada lag raha ho, tabhi ise avoid karein agar accuracy ka fayda cost se kam ho.

Example 7.1

Agar hum ek data set mein milch animals ka total estimate karein jahan \( b = 0.81 \) ho, to Regression Estimate aayega 107554. Jab hum iski relative precision nikalte hain, to ye simple mean ke mukable 416.74% behtar hota hai, aur ratio estimator ke mukable 102.58% behtar hota hai! Ye sabit karta hai ki regression sabse efficient hai.

7.5 Regression Estimators in Stratified Sampling

When our population is divided into \( L \) strata (groups), and we draw a simple random sample from each, we can apply regression estimators in two ways, just like we did with ratio estimators: the Separate Regression Estimator and the Combined Regression Estimator.

7.5.1 Separate Regression Estimator

In this method, we estimate the regression coefficient \( b_h \) separately for every single stratum. The separate regression estimator (\( \bar{y}_{ls} \)) is defined as:

We apply the regression adjustment inside each stratum individually and sum them up:
$$ \bar{y}_{ls} = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h [\bar{y}_h + b_h(\bar{X}_h - \bar{x}_h)] $$

Theorem 7.5.1: If sampling is independent in different strata and the sample size is large enough in each stratum, this is an almost unbiased estimator. Its variance is simply the sum of variances across strata:

$$ V(\bar{y}_{ls}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_h S_{hyx} + \beta_h^2 S_{hx}^2] $$

Drawback: Since the bias terms from each stratum can add up with the same sign, the overall bias in this separate estimator might become quite large if the sample size in each individual stratum is small.

7.5.2 Combined Regression Estimator

In this method, instead of calculating a separate slope for each group, we calculate one common (pooled) regression coefficient \( b_c \) for all the strata combined.

The pooled estimator \( b_c \) is used with the overall stratified means:
$$ \bar{y}_{lc} = \bar{y}_{st} + b_c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}_{st}) $$

Theorem 7.5.2: If sampling is independent and sample sizes are large, the variance of the combined estimator is:

$$ V(\bar{y}_{lc}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_c S_{hyx} + \beta_c^2 S_{hx}^2] $$

Drawback: This combined estimator is not appropriate when there is reason to believe that the true regression coefficient varies considerably from one stratum to another.

7.5.3 Comparison of Combined and Separate Estimators

Which one should you pick? You need to be cautious:

  • If the regression relationships are linear but the slopes (coefficients) appear to be the SAME in all strata, prefer the Combined Regression Estimator (it has a smaller bias).
  • If the regressions are linear but the coefficients DIFFER a lot from stratum to stratum, it is better to adopt the Separate Regression Estimator (it will be more efficient, though you must watch out for bias if sample sizes are small).

Example 7.2

Estimating the total number of trees using stratified sampling data.
Separate Regression Estimate: gives \( \widehat{Y}_{ls} = 18641 \). The efficiency over ratio estimate is 95.8%.
Combined Regression Estimate: gives \( \widehat{Y}_{lc} = 18635 \). The efficiency over ratio estimate is 90.3%.
The efficiency of the separate over the combined estimate is 106.06%, showing that in this specific case, calculating slopes separately gave slightly better precision.

7.5 Regression Estimators in Stratified Sampling (स्तरीकृत प्रतिचयन में प्रतिगमन अनुमानक)

Jab population \( L \) strata (groups) mein bati ho, to hum regression estimators ko do tarike se apply kar sakte hain:

7.5.1 Separate Regression Estimator (अलग प्रतिगमन आकलक)

Is method mein hum har stratum ke liye alag regression coefficient \( b_h \) nikalte hain.

$$ \bar{y}_{ls} = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h [\bar{y}_h + b_h(\bar{X}_h - \bar{x}_h)] $$

Ye estimator large samples mein almost unbiased hota hai. Lekin iska ek bada Drawback hai: Agar har stratum mein sample size chhota ho, to har stratum ka thoda-thoda bias judkar overall bias bahut bada ho sakta hai.

7.5.2 Combined Regression Estimator (संयुक्त प्रतिगमन आकलक)

Is method mein hum alag-alag slope nikalne ki bajay, sabhi strata ke liye ek common (pooled) regression coefficient \( b_c \) nikalte hain.

$$ \bar{y}_{lc} = \bar{y}_{st} + b_c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}_{st}) $$

Iska Drawback ye hai ki agar alag-alag strata mein regression slope wakai mein alag-alag ho, to ye estimator achha result nahi dega aur iska variance badh jayega.

7.5.3 Comparison (तुलना)

  • Agar sabhi strata mein regression line ka slope lagbhag same ho, to Combined Regression Estimator use karein (iska bias kam hota hai).
  • Agar strata ke beech slopes alag-alag hon, to Separate Regression Estimator behtar hai (ye zyada efficient hoga, bas dhyan rahe ki sample size bahut chhota na ho).

Example 7.2

Stratified sampling data use karke trees ka total nikalne par, Separate estimate 18641 aaya (efficiency 95.8%) aur Combined estimate 18635 aaya (efficiency 90.3%). Is case mein Separate estimate Combined wale se 106.06% behtar sabit hua.

7.6 Multi-variate Regression Estimator

Often, we have data on more than just one extra variable, and we want to use all available information! For instance, if we want to estimate crop yield, we might know both the area of the farm (\( x_1 \)) and the amount of fertilizer used (\( x_2 \)). In this case, we define a multi-variate estimator:

We sum the adjustments for all 'k' auxiliary variables:
$$ \bar{y}_{lr} = \bar{y} + \sum_{i = 1}^k \beta_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{x}_i) $$

Des Raj (1965) proposed a difference estimator for two \( x \)-variates using weights that add up to 1 (\( w_1 + w_2 = 1 \)):

$$ \bar{y}_w = w_1[\bar{y} + \beta_1(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{x}_1)] + w_2[\bar{y} + \beta_2(\bar{X}_2 - \bar{x}_2)] $$

By finding the best weights (\( w_1 \) and \( w_2 \)) by differentiating the variance expression and equating to zero, we minimize the variance. The key takeaway is: If you have two auxiliary variables, it is always better to use both of them instead of just one, as long as they are not perfectly correlated with each other. They each bring fresh, valuable information to make your estimate incredibly precise!

7.6 Multi-variate Regression Estimator (बहुभिन्नरूपी प्रतिगमन आकलक)

Kai baar hamare paas ek se zyada auxiliary variables ki jankari hoti hai, aur hum sabka use karna chahte hain! Jaise fasal ki paidaish estimate karne ke liye khet ka area (\( x_1 \)) aur fertilizer ki matra (\( x_2 \)) dono pata hon. Tab hum multi-variate estimator use karte hain:

$$ \bar{y}_{lr} = \bar{y} + \sum_{i = 1}^k \beta_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{x}_i) $$

Des Raj ne do variables ke liye weights (\( w_1, w_2 \)) ka use karke ek naya estimator banaya. Iska main conclusion ye hai ki: Agar aapke paas do auxiliary variables hain, to ek ki bajay dono ka use karna hamesha behtar hota hai (bas shart ye hai ki wo aapas mein perfectly correlated na hon). Har variable ek nayi information lata hai jisse estimate ki precision kafi badh jati hai!

  • a) \( \rho = 0 \) (No correlation)
  • b) \( \rho \neq 0 \) (Some correlation exists)
  • c) \( \rho \) is negative only
  • d) Both a and c
Correct Answer: b) \( \rho \neq 0 \). (As long as there is some relationship, regression helps!)
2. If the true regression line passes exactly through the origin (\( \beta = R \)), which estimator gives the same variance as the regression estimator?
(यदि प्रतिगमन रेखा मूल बिंदु से होकर गुजरती है, तो कौन सा अनुमानक प्रतिगमन के समान प्रसरण प्रदान करता है?)
  • a) Mean per unit estimator
  • b) Difference estimator
  • c) Ratio estimator
  • d) Product estimator
Correct Answer: c) Ratio estimator. (This is the special case where ratio and regression are equally precise!)

✏️ Fill in the Blanks (रिक्त स्थान भरें)

1. The regression estimator is slightly ____________ because we have to estimate the regression coefficient 'b' from the sample itself.
(प्रतिगमन अनुमानक थोड़ा ____________ है क्योंकि हम नमूने से 'b' का अनुमान लगाते हैं।)
Answer: biased (पक्षपाती). (But remember, this bias becomes negligible in large samples!)
2. In stratified sampling, if the regression coefficients appear to be the SAME in all strata, we should prefer the ____________ regression estimator.
(स्तरीकृत प्रतिचयन में, यदि प्रतिगमन गुणांक सभी स्तरों में समान हों, तो हमें ____________ प्रतिगमन अनुमानक का चयन करना चाहिए।)
Answer: Combined (संयुक्त).

✅ True or False (सही या गलत)

1. The difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \) gives its minimum variance when the constant 'c' is equal to the regression coefficient \( \beta \).
(अंतर अनुमानक न्यूनतम प्रसरण देता है जब स्थिरांक 'c' प्रतिगमन गुणांक \( \beta \) के बराबर होता है।)
Answer: True (सही). (This is the core concept that leads us to the regression estimator!)
2. The Separate Regression estimator is always better than the Combined Regression estimator, regardless of sample size or stratum slopes.
(अलग प्रतिगमन अनुमानक हमेशा संयुक्त प्रतिगमन अनुमानक से बेहतर होता है।)
Answer: False (गलत). (The separate estimator can have a dangerously large bias if stratum sample sizes are small. Combined is safer if slopes are roughly equal.)
