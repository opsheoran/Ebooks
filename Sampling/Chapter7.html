7.1 Introduction

"At thirty man suspects himself a fool;
Knows it at forty, and reforms his plan:
At fifty chides his infamous delay,
Pushes his prudent purpose to resolve,
In all the magnanimity of thought
Resolves, and re-resolves: then dies the same."
-- J. Young

Just like ratio estimators, linear regression estimators use extra information (auxiliary variable) to make our results more accurate. We have already seen that the ratio estimator works very well if the relationship between our main variable (\( y \)) and the extra variable (\( x \)) is a straight line passing through the starting point (origin). But what if that straight line does not pass through the origin? In such cases, regression estimators are a much better choice. Simply put, if your study variable (\( y \)) is related to the extra variable (\( x \)) in a linear way, fitting a regression line gives a more precise estimate of the population mean or total. In this chapter, we will learn how these estimators work and see why they are often better than the simple mean per unit or ratio estimators.

Ratio estimators की तरह, linear regression estimators भी precision बढ़ाने के लिए auxiliary information का उपयोग करते हैं। हमने देखा था कि ratio estimator तब सटीक होता है जब regression linear हो और line origin (मूल बिंदु) से गुजरे। लेकिन जब regression linear हो और line origin से न गुजरे, तो linear regression आधारित estimators बेहतर होते हैं। दूसरे शब्दों में, अगर study variable (\( y \)) auxiliary variable का लगभग linear function हो, तो linear regression फिट करके population parameters का अनुमान लगाना ज़्यादा सटीक होता है। ऐसे estimator को regression estimator कहा जाता. है। हम इसके मुख्य characteristics और अन्य estimators से तुलना पर चर्चा करेंगे।

7.2 Difference Estimator

Suppose we have two related variables, \( y \) and \( x \). We want to estimate the population mean \( \bar{Y} \). If we take a simple random sample and get the sample means \( \bar{y} \) and \( \bar{x} \), we can improve our estimate of \( \bar{y} \) by using the known population mean \( \bar{X} \). The idea is to adjust \( \bar{y} \) based on the 'difference' between the actual \( \bar{X} \) and our sample estimate \( \bar{x} \). This is called the simple difference estimator:

The estimator adjusts the sample mean by adding the difference between the population mean and sample mean of the auxiliary variable:
$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \quad (7.2.1) $$

This simple formula assumes that for every 1 unit change in \( x \), there is exactly 1 unit change in \( y \) (meaning they have equal variances). But in real life, the relationship is usually \( y = cx + k \), where \( c \) is a constant slope. In this case, we use a more general version of the formula:

We multiply the difference by a constant factor \( c \) to account for the scale of relationship:
$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \quad (7.2.2) $$

Here, \( c \) and \( \bar{X} \) are constants that we already know.

Now, let's look at some important theorems about this difference estimator in simple random sampling.

Theorem 7.2.1

In simple random sampling without replacement (wor), the difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \) is unbiased, and its sampling variance is given by:

The variance of a linear combination of two variables depends on their individual variances and their covariance:
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{y}) + c^2V(\bar{x}) - 2c\text{ Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x}) $$
By substituting the standard variance formulas, we can express it in terms of the correlation coefficient \( \rho \):
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{y})(1 + \alpha^2 - 2\alpha\rho) \quad (7.2.3) $$

Where \( \rho \) is the correlation between \( y \) and \( x \), and \( \alpha = cS_x/S_y \).

This result shows us something very important: the difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \) is more precise than the simple sample mean \( \bar{y} \) as long as the correlation \( \rho \) is greater than \( \alpha/2 \).

Corollary

At what value of \( c \) will the variance be the lowest? We can show that \( V(\bar{y}_D) \) is minimum when:

By differentiating the variance with respect to \( c \) and equating to zero, we find the optimum value:
$$ c = \frac{\text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x})}{V(\bar{x})} = \beta \quad (7.2.4) $$

This proves that the best value for \( c \) is \( \beta \), which is the regression coefficient of \( y \) on \( x \). In this case, our minimum variance becomes \( V(\bar{y})(1 - \rho^2) \).

Theorem 7.2.2

Let's formally calculate the sampling variance of the difference estimator \( \bar{y}_D \):

The variance for a given constant \( c \) is:
$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n}(S_y^2 + c^2S_x^2 - 2c \rho S_y S_x) \quad (7.2.5) $$

Proof:

Since \( c \) is a constant and \( E(\bar{x}) = \bar{X} \), we can easily see that \( E(\bar{y}_D) = E(\bar{y}) + c(\bar{X} - E(\bar{x})) = \bar{Y} \). This proves it is unbiased. Now, let's define a new variable for each unit:

Let \( u_i = y_i - c(x_i - \bar{X}) \). Then the sample mean is \( \bar{u} = \bar{y} - c(\bar{x} - \bar{X}) \).

The variance of \( \bar{y}_D \) is just the variance of \( \bar{u} \):

$$ V(\bar{y}_D) = V(\bar{u}) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n}S_u^2 $$
Now we calculate the population variance of our new variable \( u \):
$$ S_u^2 = \frac{\sum (u_i - \bar{U})^2}{(N - 1)} = \frac{\sum [(y_i - \bar{Y}) - c(x_i - \bar{X})]^2}{(N - 1)} $$
Expanding the square \( (A - B)^2 = A^2 + B^2 - 2AB \), we get:
$$ S_u^2 = \frac{\sum (y_i - \bar{Y})^2}{N-1} + c^2 \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{X})^2}{N-1} - 2c \frac{\sum (y_i - \bar{Y})(x_i - \bar{X})}{N-1} $$
Which simplifies to the final formula:
$$ S_u^2 = S_y^2 + c^2S_x^2 - 2c\rho S_yS_x $$

Corollary 3

To estimate this variance from a sample, we replace population values with sample values:

The unbiased estimator of the variance is:
$$ v(\bar{y}_D) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n}(s_y^2 + c^2s_x^2 - 2cs_{yx}) \quad (7.2.6) $$

7.2 Difference Estimator (Difference Estimator)

मान लीजिए \( y \) और \( x \) correlated variables हैं। हम \( Y \) का अनुमान लगाना चाहते हैं। अगर simple random sample से \( \bar{y} \) और \( \bar{x} \) (unbiased estimators) प्राप्त हों, तो difference function जोड़कर \( \bar{y} \) को सुधारा जा सकता है। एक simple difference estimator इस प्रकार है:

$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \quad (7.2.1) $$

यहाँ माना जाता है कि \( x \) में unit change होने पर \( y \) में भी unit change होता है। अगर relationship \( y = cx + k \) टाइप की हो, तो generalized difference estimator उपयोग किया जाता है:

$$ \bar{y}_D = \bar{y} + c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \quad (7.2.2) $$

जहाँ \( c \) और \( \bar{X} \) ज्ञात (known) हैं।

Theorem 7.2.1

Simple random sampling (wor) में \( \bar{y}_D \) unbiased है और इसका sampling variance Eq. (7.2.3) द्वारा दिया जाता है। \( \bar{y}_D \) का variance \( \bar{y} \) से कम होगा यदि \( \rho > \alpha/2 \) हो।

Corollary (उपप्रमेय)

\( V(\bar{y}_D) \) न्यूनतम (minimum) तब होगा जब \( c \) का मान regression coefficient \( \beta \) के बराबर हो। तब minimum variance \( V(\bar{y})(1 - \rho^2) \) होता है।

Theorem 7.2.2

Sampling variance और उसका unbiased estimator Eq. (7.2.5) और (7.2.6) द्वारा दिए गए हैं (अंग्रेजी section देखें)।

7.3 Regression Estimator

When we discussed the difference estimator, we saw that the best value for the constant \( c \) is actually \( \beta \), the regression coefficient. However, in real life, we usually don't know the value of \( \beta \) beforehand. So, what do we do? We estimate it from our sample! If we have values of \( y \) and \( x \) for every unit in our sample, we can find the 'least squares' estimate of \( \beta \), which we call \( b \):

The sample regression coefficient \( b \) is calculated as:
$$ b = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Using this estimated value \( b \), we can define the linear regression estimators for the population mean (\( \bar{Y} \)) and total (\( Y \)):

For the population mean:
$$ \bar{y}_l = \bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$
For the population total:
$$ \widehat{Y}_l = N[\bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x})] \quad (7.3.1) $$

7.3.1 Bias of Regression Estimator

Is this estimator unbiased? Well, not exactly. Since we are using an estimate \( b \) from the same sample, it introduces a small amount of bias. However, this bias is very small and decreases as our sample size gets larger. For large samples, we can practically ignore it. Mathematically, the bias is approximated as:

The bias depends on the relationship between the sample mean and the regression coefficient:
$$ B(\bar{y}_l) \cong - \text{Cov}(\bar{x},b) \quad (7.3.2) $$

7.3.2 Sampling Variance of Regression Estimator

Even though there is a tiny bias, the regression estimator is very precise. For large samples, we can calculate its variance by looking at the linear relationship.

Theorem 7.3.2

In simple random sampling, the variance of the regression estimator for a large sample is:

Just like the difference estimator with the optimum value of \( c \), the variance is:
$$ V(\bar{y}_l) = V(\bar{y}) + \beta^2V(\bar{x}) - 2\beta \text{Cov}(\bar{y},\bar{x}) $$
Which simplifies to a very elegant formula involving the correlation \( \rho \):
$$ V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \quad (7.3.7) $$

This formula tells us something great: as long as there is some correlation between our variables (\( \rho \neq 0 \)), the regression estimator will always be more precise than the simple sample mean!

Corollary 2

If we want to estimate this variance using only our sample data, we use this formula:

The sample-based estimate of the variance is:
$$ v(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n(n - 1)}\left[ \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 - \frac{\left( \sum (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x}) \right)^2}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \right] \quad (7.3.8) $$

7.3 Regression Estimator (Regression Estimator)

आमतौर पर \( \beta \) पहले से ज्ञात नहीं होता, इसलिए इसे sample से estimate किया जाता है। Least square estimate \( b \) का उपयोग करके linear regression estimators इस प्रकार हैं:

$$ \bar{y}_l = \bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) $$

7.3.1 Bias (पूर्वाग्रह)

यह estimator consistent है, लेकिन biased है क्योंकि \( b \) sample पर आधारित है। Bias नगण्य होता है यदि sample size बड़ा हो:

$$ B(\bar{y}_l) \cong - \text{Cov}(\bar{x},b) $$

7.3.2 Sampling Variance (Sampling Variance)

बड़े samples के लिए regression estimator का variance इस प्रकार है:

$$ V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \quad (7.3.7) $$

यह दर्शाता है कि अगर \( \rho \neq 0 \) हो, तो regression estimator simple mean से ज़्यादा सटीक है। इसका unbiased sample estimator Eq. (7.3.8) में दिया गया है।

7.4 Comparison with the Mean per unit and Ratio Estimators

Now, let's see which method is the best. For large samples, we compare the variances of the three methods we've learned:

  • Simple Mean: \( V(\bar{y}) = \frac{(1 - f)}{n}S_y^2 \)
  • Ratio Estimator: \( V(\widehat{Y}_R) \cong \frac{(1 - f)}{n}(S_y^2 + R^2S_x^2 - 2R\rho S_yS_x) \)
  • Regression Estimator: \( V(\bar{y}_l) \cong \frac{(1 - f)}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \)

The Conclusion: The regression estimator is the winner! It is always more efficient than the simple mean (unless the variables are not related at all, \( \rho = 0 \)). It is also better than the ratio estimator, except in the special case where the regression line passes exactly through the origin—in that case, both ratio and regression methods give the same precision.

7.4 Comparison (तुलना)

बड़े samples के लिए, regression estimator हमेशा simple mean (\( \rho \neq 0 \) होने पर) और ratio estimator (जब तक line origin से न गुजरे) से ज़्यादा efficient होता है।

  • यदि \( \rho \cong \sigma_x/\sigma_y \) हो, तो \( c=1 \) वाला difference estimator अच्छा रहता है।
  • यदि \( \rho \cong C_x/C_y \) हो, तो ratio estimator regression जितना ही सटीक होता है।

7.5 Regression Estimators in Stratified Sampling

Just like we did with ratio estimators, we can apply regression techniques to stratified sampling in two different ways: the Separate version and the Combined version.

7.5.1 Separate Regression Estimator

We calculate a separate regression coefficient for each stratum:
$$ \bar{y}_{ls} = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h [\bar{y}_h + b_h(\bar{X}_h - \bar{x}_h)] \quad (7.5.1) $$

When to use it? This method is very powerful if the regression slope (\( b_h \)) changes a lot from one stratum to another. For example, if the relationship between income and expenditure is different in urban areas compared to rural areas, a separate estimator is better. But be careful: if your sample size in each group is very small, the overall bias might increase.

7.5.2 Combined Regression Estimator

We use a single "pooled" regression coefficient \( b_c \) for the entire population:
$$ \bar{y}_{lc} = \bar{y}_{st} + b_c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}_{st}) \quad (7.5.5) $$

When to use it? We use the combined estimator when the regression slopes are roughly the same across all strata. The big advantage here is that the bias is usually much smaller than the separate estimator, especially when sample sizes within strata are not very large.

7.5 Regression Estimators in Stratified Sampling (Stratified Sampling में Regression Estimators)

इसमें दो प्रकार के estimators होते हैं:

7.5.1 Separate Regression Estimator (अलग प्रतिगमन आकलक)

इसमें हर stratum के लिए अलग \( b_m \) निकाला जाता है। यह तब बेहतर है जब strata के slopes अलग-अलग हों, लेकिन छोटे samples में bias ज़्यादा हो सकता है।

7.5.2 Combined Regression Estimator (संयुक्त प्रतिगमन आकलक)

इसमें पूरी population के लिए एक pooled \( b_c \) निकाला जाता है। अगर सभी strata के slopes लगभग समान हों, तो इसे प्राथमिकता दी जाती है क्योंकि इसका bias कम होता है।

7.6 Multi-variate Regression Estimator

What if we have data on more than one extra variable? For example, to estimate crop yield, we might know both the 'area of the farm' and the 'amount of fertilizer used'. In such cases, we use a multi-variate regression estimator:

We adjust our estimate using multiple regression coefficients (\( \beta_i \)) for each auxiliary variable:
$$ \bar{y}_{lr} = \bar{y} + \sum_{i = 1}^k \beta_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{x}_i) \quad (7.6.1) $$

Using more than one auxiliary variable usually makes our estimate much more precise. The only thing to keep in mind is that these extra variables should not be perfectly related to each other—they should each bring some new information to the table.

7.6 Multi-variate Regression Estimator (बहुभिन्नरूपी प्रतिगमन आकलक)

जब एक से ज़्यादा auxiliary variables उपलब्ध हों, तब multivariate regression estimator (Eq. 7.6.1) का उपयोग किया जाता है। एक से अधिक variables उपयोग करना हमेशा बेहतर होता है, बशर्ते वे आपस में perfectly correlated न हों।

❓ Multiple Choice Questions (बहुविकल्पीय प्रश्न)

1. In which of these situations is the linear regression estimator more efficient than the simple mean per unit estimator?
(रैखिक प्रतिगमन अनुमानक माध्य प्रति इकाई अनुमानक की तुलना में अधिक कुशल होता है जब:)
  • a) \( \rho = 0 \) (No correlation)
  • b) \( \rho \neq 0 \) (Some correlation exists)
  • c) \( \rho \) is negative only
  • d) Both a and c
Correct Answer: b) \( \rho \neq 0 \). (As long as there is some relationship, regression helps!)
2. If the relationship between \( y \) and \( x \) is a straight line passing exactly through the origin, which of these will give the same variance as the regression estimator?
(यदि \( x \) पर \( y \) का प्रतिगमन मूल बिंदु से होकर गुजरता है, तो कौन सा अनुमानक प्रतिगमन अनुमानक के समान प्रसरण प्रदान करता है?)
  • a) Mean per unit estimator
  • b) Difference estimator
  • c) Ratio estimator
  • d) Product estimator
Correct Answer: c) Ratio estimator. (This is the special case where ratio and regression are equally good!)

✅ True or False (सही या गलत)

1. If there is absolutely no correlation between the study variable and the extra variable (\( \rho = 0 \)), the regression estimator is exactly as efficient as the simple mean per unit estimator.
(यदि \( \rho = 0 \) है, तो प्रतिगमन अनुमानक माध्य प्रति इकाई अनुमानक के समान ही कुशल है।)
Answer: True (सही). (If there's no relationship, the extra information doesn't help at all.)
