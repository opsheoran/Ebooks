7.1 Introduction

"At thirty man suspects himself a fool;
Knows it at forty, and reforms his plan:
At fifty chides his infamous delay,
Pushes his prudent purpose to resolve,
In all the magnanimity of thought
Resolves, and re-resolves: then dies the same."
-- J. Young

Hello students! Just like ratio estimators, linear regression estimators also make use of auxiliary information for increasing precision. We have already seen that the ratio estimator provides a precise estimate of the population mean if regression is linear and the straight line passes through the origin. But what happens when the regression is linear and the line does not pass through the origin? In that case, it is much better to use estimators based on linear regression.

In simple words, if our study variate (\(y\)) is approximately a constant plus a multiple of the auxiliary variate (\(x\)), it is more precise to estimate the population mean or total by fitting a linear regression. Such an estimator is called a regression estimator. We shall now discuss the main characteristics of such estimators and compare them with the mean per unit estimator and ratio estimator.

7.2 Difference Estimator

Let \(y\) and \(x\) be correlated characteristics. We wish to estimate the population mean \(\bar{Y}\). If, from a simple random sample, we obtain the unbiased estimators \(\bar{y}\) and \(\bar{x}\) of \(\bar{Y}\) and \(\bar{X}\) respectively, then we can improve upon the estimator \(\bar{y}\) by introducing a difference function. A simple difference estimator is defined by:

Simple Difference Estimator:
\( \bar{y}_d = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \)

This formula assumes that for a 1 unit change in \(x\), there is exactly a 1 unit change in \(y\), meaning \(y\) and \(x\) have equal variances. This assumption will not be valid if the relationship is of the type \(y = cx + k\), where \(c\) and \(k\) are constants. In this situation, a more generalized difference estimator is defined as:

Generalized Difference Estimator:
\( \bar{y}_d = \bar{y} + \beta_0(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \)

Here, \(\beta_0\) and \(\bar{X}\) are known quantities. Let us look at the theorems for these estimators in simple random sampling.

Theorem 7.2.1

In simple random sampling, without replacement (wor), the simple difference estimator \(\bar{y}_d = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x})\) is unbiased, and its sampling variance is given by:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + S_x^2 - 2\rho S_y S_x) \)

Proof:

First, we find the expected value to prove it is unbiased. By applying the expectation operator:

\( E(\bar{y}_d) = E[\bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x})] \)
\( E(\bar{y}_d) = E(\bar{y}) + \bar{X} - E(\bar{x}) \)
Since sample means are unbiased estimators of population means (
\(E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y}\)
and
\(E(\bar{x}) = \bar{X}\)
):
\( E(\bar{y}_d) = \bar{Y} + \bar{X} - \bar{X} = \bar{Y} \)

This proves that the simple difference estimator is unbiased. Now, let's derive its variance:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = V(\bar{y} + \bar{X} - \bar{x}) \)
Since
\(\bar{X}\)
is a constant, its variance is zero:
\( V(\bar{y}_d) = V(\bar{y} - \bar{x}) \) \( V(\bar{y}_d) = V(\bar{y}) + V(\bar{x}) - 2\text{Cov}(\bar{y}, \bar{x}) \)
Substituting the standard formulas for variance and covariance in SRS wor:
\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2 + \frac{1 - f}{n}S_x^2 - 2\left(\frac{1 - f}{n}S_{yx}\right) \)
Factoring out \((1-f)/n\) and using \(S_{yx} = \rho S_y S_x\):
\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + S_x^2 - 2\rho S_y S_x) \)

From this result, it may be noted that \(\bar{y}_d\) has a smaller variance than the simple mean per unit \(\bar{y}\) if \(\rho > S_x / 2S_y\).

Corollary

Show that \(V(\bar{y}_d)\) will have its minimum value when \(c = \beta\) (or \(\beta_0 = \beta\)).

Proof: To find the optimum value to be given to \(\beta_0\), we differentiate the variance of the generalized difference estimator with respect to \(\beta_0\) and equate it to zero:

\( \frac{d}{d\beta_0} \left[ V(\bar{y}) + \beta_0^2 V(\bar{x}) - 2\beta_0 \text{Cov}(\bar{y}, \bar{x}) \right] = 0 \)
\( 2\beta_0 V(\bar{x}) - 2\text{Cov}(\bar{y}, \bar{x}) = 0 \)
\( \beta_0 = \frac{\text{Cov}(\bar{y}, \bar{x})}{V(\bar{x})} = \beta \)

This proves that the optimum value to be given to \(\beta_0\) is \(\beta\), the value of the regression coefficient of \(y\) on \(x\). In this case, \(\beta_0 = \beta\) and the minimum value of variance is \(V(\bar{y})(1 - \rho^2)\).

Theorem 7.2.2

In simple random sampling, wor, the sampling variance of the generalized difference estimator \(\bar{y}_d = \bar{y} + \beta_0(\bar{X} - \bar{x})\) is obtained by:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + \beta_0^2 S_x^2 - 2\beta_0 \rho S_y S_x) \)

Where \(\beta_0\) is some specified quantity.

Proof:

Since \(\beta_0\) is a given constant and \(E(\bar{x}) = \bar{X}\), the unbiasedness of \(\bar{y}_d\) is proved easily (\(E(\bar{y}_d) = \bar{Y}\)). Further, to derive the variance rigorously, we define a new variate:

Let us denote for each unit:
\( u_i = y_i - \beta_0 x_i \)
Then the population mean and sample mean of \(u\) are:
\( \bar{U} = \bar{Y} - \beta_0 \bar{X} \) \( \bar{u} = \bar{y} - \beta_0 \bar{x} \)
We can now express our estimator as:
\( \bar{y}_d = \bar{u} + \beta_0 \bar{X} \)
Since \(\beta_0 \bar{X}\) is a constant, the variance of \(\bar{y}_d\) is simply the variance of the sample mean \(\bar{u}\) in SRS wor:
\( V(\bar{y}_d) = V(\bar{u}) = \frac{1 - f}{n} S_u^2 \)

Now, let us expand the population variance \(S_u^2\):

\( S_u^2 = \frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^N (u_i - \bar{U})^2 \)
\( S_u^2 = \frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^N [ (y_i - \bar{Y}) - \beta_0(x_i - \bar{X}) ]^2 \)
\( S_u^2 = \frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^N [ (y_i - \bar{Y})^2 + \beta_0^2(x_i - \bar{X})^2 - 2\beta_0(y_i - \bar{Y})(x_i - \bar{X}) ] \)
\( S_u^2 = S_y^2 + \beta_0^2 S_x^2 - 2\beta_0 S_{yx} \)

By substituting \(S_{yx} = \rho S_y S_x\) and putting \(S_u^2\) back into the variance equation, we get:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + \beta_0^2 S_x^2 - 2\beta_0 \rho S_y S_x) \)

This proves the theorem comprehensively.

Corollary 1

For the case \(\beta_0 = 1\) (or \(c=1\)), the variance of the difference estimator \(\bar{y}_d\) is exactly the same as the first-order approximation of the ratio estimator \(V(\bar{y}_R)\) given in relation (6.4.4).

Corollary 2

The difference estimator \(\bar{y}_d\) will be more precise than the mean per unit estimator \(\bar{y}\) if \(\rho > \beta_0 S_x / 2S_y\). This gives the condition that the difference estimator will be more efficient than the mean per unit estimator. Hence, if one can guess \(\beta_0\) from a pilot survey, a proper decision about its use can be taken.

Corollary 3

In simple random sampling, wor, an unbiased estimator of \(V(\bar{y}_d)\) is obtained by:

\( v(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(s_y^2 + \beta_0^2 s_x^2 - 2\beta_0 s_{yx}) \)

where \(s_y^2\), \(s_x^2\), and \(s_{yx}\) are the usual unbiased sample estimators of their population counterparts.

7.2 Difference Estimator (अंतर अनुमानक)

मान लीजिए हमारे पास दो related variables हैं, \(y\) और \(x\)। हम population mean \(\bar{Y}\) estimate करना चाहते हैं। अगर हम simple random sample लें और unbiased estimators \(\bar{y}\) और \(\bar{x}\) प्राप्त करें, तो हम एक difference function introduce करके \(\bar{y}\) को improve कर सकते हैं। एक simple difference estimator को इस तरह define किया जाता है:

Simple Difference Estimator:
\( \bar{y}_d = \bar{y} + (\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \)

यह formula assume करता है कि \(x\) में 1 unit change आने पर \(y\) में भी exactly 1 unit change आएगा। लेकिन यह assumption तब valid नहीं होता जब relationship \(y = cx + k\) जैसा हो। ऐसी situation में हम एक generalized difference estimator use करते हैं:

Generalized Difference Estimator:
\( \bar{y}_d = \bar{y} + \beta_0(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \)

यहाँ \(\beta_0\) और \(\bar{X}\) known (पहले से पता) quantities हैं।

Theorem 7.2.1

Simple random sampling (wor) में, simple difference estimator \(\bar{y}_d\) unbiased होता है, और इसका sampling variance यह होता है:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + S_x^2 - 2\rho S_y S_x) \)

Proof:

सबसे पहले, expectation operator apply करके हम prove करते हैं कि यह unbiased है (\(E(\bar{y}_d) = \bar{Y}\))। उसके बाद variance निकालने के लिए हम दोनों terms का variance और covariance formula लगाते हैं:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = V(\bar{y} - \bar{x}) = V(\bar{y}) + V(\bar{x}) - 2\text{Cov}(\bar{y}, \bar{x}) \)

इसमें values put करने पर हमें required expression मिल जाता है। यह estimator तभी ज़्यादा precise होगा जब \(\rho > S_x / 2S_y\) हो।

Corollary (उपप्रमेय)

अगर हमें minimum variance चाहिए, तो हम variance expression को \(\beta_0\) के respect में differentiate करके zero के बराबर रखते हैं। इससे हमें \(\beta_0 = \beta\) मिलता है (जो कि regression coefficient है)। इस case में minimum variance \(V(\bar{y})(1 - \rho^2)\) होता है।

Theorem 7.2.2

Generalized difference estimator का variance यह होता है:

\( V(\bar{y}_d) = \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + \beta_0^2 S_x^2 - 2\beta_0 \rho S_y S_x) \)

Proof:

इसको rigorously prove करने के लिए, हम एक नया variable \(u_i = y_i - \beta_0 x_i\) define करते हैं। फिर \(\bar{y}_d = \bar{u} + \beta_0 \bar{X}\) लिख कर, हम इसका variance \(\bar{u}\) के variance के बराबर set करते हैं। Population variance \(S_u^2\) को expand करके values रखने पर हमें final theorem का result exact मिल जाता है।

Corollaries

Corollary 1: अगर \(\beta_0 = 1\) हो, तो यह variance ratio estimator के first-order approximation के बराबर होता है।

Corollary 2: यह estimator mean per unit से बेहतर होगा अगर \(\rho > \beta_0 S_x / 2S_y\)। Pilot survey से \(\beta_0\) का अंदाज़ा लगाकर हम अच्छा decision ले सकते हैं।

Corollary 3: इस estimator का unbiased sample variance estimate निकालने के लिए हम parameters को उनके sample counterparts (\(s_y^2, s_x^2, s_{yx}\)) से replace कर देते हैं।

7.3 Regression Estimator

While discussing the difference estimator, it was seen that the optimum value to be given to \(\beta_0\) is \(\beta\), where \(\beta\) is the regression coefficient of \(y\) on \(x\). Generally, \(\beta\) is not known in advance and its value is estimated from the sample. Suppose \(y\) and \(x\) are obtained for each unit in the sample, then a least square estimate of \(\beta\) is:

\( b = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \)

Thus, linear regression estimators of the population mean \(\bar{Y}\) and population total \(Y\) are given by:

\( \bar{y}_l = \bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x}) \)
\( \widehat{Y}_l = N[\bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x})] \)

Since \(b\) is a random variate, exact expressions for the bias and variance of the regression estimator are difficult to derive. In this section, large sample approximations to its bias and sampling variance will be given.

7.3.1 Bias of Regression Estimator

The regression estimator is consistent because, by increasing the sample size \(n\) to the population size \(N\), we have \(\bar{x} \to \bar{X}\). Ultimately, the regression estimator reduces to \(\bar{y}\), which is unbiased. However, the regression estimator \(\bar{y}_l\) is generally biased because it involves the product of two estimates, viz. \(b\) and \(\bar{x}\). The bias of the regression estimator will usually be trivial and will decrease as sample size increases.

Theorem 7.3.1

In simple random sampling, the bias of \(\bar{y}_l\) is approximated by \(-\text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b)\), which will be negligible if the sample size is large.

Proof:

To find the bias rigorously, we use Taylor series expansion by introducing error terms. Let:

\( \bar{y} = \bar{Y} + e_0 \implies E(e_0) = 0 \)
\( \bar{x} = \bar{X} + e_1 \implies E(e_1) = 0 \)
\( b = \beta + e_2 \implies E(e_2) = 0 \)

Now, let's substitute these error terms into our regression estimator equation:

\( \bar{y}_l = (\bar{Y} + e_0) + (\beta + e_2)[\bar{X} - (\bar{X} + e_1)] \)
\( \bar{y}_l = \bar{Y} + e_0 + (\beta + e_2)(-e_1) \)
\( \bar{y}_l = \bar{Y} + e_0 - \beta e_1 - e_1 e_2 \)

To find the bias, we take the expectation of \((\bar{y}_l - \bar{Y})\):

\( \text{Bias}(\bar{y}_l) = E(\bar{y}_l - \bar{Y}) = E(e_0 - \beta e_1 - e_1 e_2) \)
\( \text{Bias}(\bar{y}_l) = E(e_0) - \beta E(e_1) - E(e_1 e_2) \)
Since
\(E(e_0) = 0\)
and
\(E(e_1) = 0\)
:
\( \text{Bias}(\bar{y}_l) = - E(e_1 e_2) \)
By definition of covariance, \(E(e_1 e_2) = E[(\bar{x} - \bar{X})(b - \beta)] = \text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b)\). Therefore:
\( \text{Bias}(\bar{y}_l) = -\text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b) \)

This completely proves the theorem. For large samples, usually \(\text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b)\) decreases and becomes zero if the joint distribution of \(y\) and \(x\) is a bivariate normal.

Corollary 1 & 2

Corollary 1: If \(b = \bar{y}/\bar{x}\), the regression estimator \(\bar{y}_l\) reduces to the ratio estimator \(\bar{y}_R\) and its bias is similarly derived.

Corollary 2: If the sample is selected in the form of \(g\) independent sub-samples, then the bias can be unbiasedly estimated. This technique can be used to construct almost unbiased regression estimators.

7.3.2 Sampling Variance of Regression Estimator

When the sample size is large enough, the contribution of the term involving \(e_1 e_2\) is expected to be very small and the bias reduces to zero. By neglecting second and higher order terms, we derive the variance.

Theorem 7.3.2

In simple random sampling, the large sample variance of the regression estimator is given by:

\( V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \)

Proof:

Using the same Taylor expansion from the bias proof, we have:

\( \bar{y}_l - \bar{Y} = e_0 - \beta e_1 - e_1 e_2 \)

If the term involving \(e_1 e_2\) is ignored for large samples, we get the first-order approximation:

\( \bar{y}_l - \bar{Y} \cong e_0 - \beta e_1 \)

Squaring both sides and taking the expectation gives the variance:

\( V(\bar{y}_l) = E[(\bar{y}_l - \bar{Y})^2] = E[(e_0 - \beta e_1)^2] \)
\( V(\bar{y}_l) = E(e_0^2) + \beta^2 E(e_1^2) - 2\beta E(e_0 e_1) \)
Substitute the definitions of expectations:
\( V(\bar{y}_l) = V(\bar{y}) + \beta^2 V(\bar{x}) - 2\beta \text{Cov}(\bar{y}, \bar{x}) \) \( V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2 + \beta^2 \frac{1 - f}{n}S_x^2 - 2\beta \frac{1 - f}{n}\rho S_y S_x \)
Since \(\beta = \frac{S_{yx}}{S_x^2} = \rho \frac{S_y}{S_x}\), substitute \(\beta\):
\( V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n} \left[ S_y^2 + \left(\rho \frac{S_y}{S_x}\right)^2 S_x^2 - 2\left(\rho \frac{S_y}{S_x}\right) \rho S_y S_x \right] \) \( V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n} [S_y^2 + \rho^2 S_y^2 - 2\rho^2 S_y^2] \) \( V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \)

This rigorous derivation completes the proof.

Corollary 1

In simple random sampling, wor, this variance shows that if regression is linear and \(b\) is the least square estimate, then \(\bar{y}_l\) is more precise than the difference estimator. If regression is perfectly linear (\(\rho = 1\)), variance becomes zero.

Corollary 2

In large samples, an almost unbiased estimator of \(V(\bar{y}_l)\) is given by substituting sample values and using \(n-2\) as a divisor based on standard regression theory:

\( v(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n(n - 2)} \left[ \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 - \frac{(\sum (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x}))^2}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \right] \)

7.3 Regression Estimator (प्रतिगमन अनुमानक)

Difference estimator discuss करते time हमने देखा कि \(\beta_0\) की optimum value \(\beta\) (regression coefficient) होती है। लेकिन generally \(\beta\) पहले से नहीं पता होता। इसलिए इसकी value sample से estimate की जाती है। अगर sample में \(y\) और \(x\) known हैं, तो least square estimate \(b\) निकालते हैं:

\( b = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})(x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2} \)

इस \(b\) का use करके linear regression estimators \(\bar{y}_l\) और \(\widehat{Y}_l\) बनाए जाते हैं। क्योंकि \(b\) एक random variate है, bias और variance निकालने के लिए हम large sample approximations का use करते हैं।

7.3.1 Bias (पूर्वाग्रह)

Regression estimator consistent होता है, लेकिन sample में यह थोड़ा biased होता है क्योंकि इसमें \(b\) और \(\bar{x}\) का product आता है।

Theorem 7.3.1 & Proof

Bias का formula \(-\text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b)\) होता है। इसको prove करने के लिए हम error terms \(e_0, e_1, e_2\) का use करके Taylor series expansion करते हैं। जब हम इन terms को equation में रखते हैं, तो expectations लेने पर \(E(e_0)\) और \(E(e_1)\) zero हो जाते हैं, और सिर्फ cross-product term \(-E(e_1 e_2)\) बचता है, जो directly \(-\text{Cov}(\bar{x}, b)\) के बराबर होता है। यह rigorously bias को prove करता है।

7.3.2 Sampling Variance

Large samples में जब bias negligible हो जाता है, तब regression estimator का variance derive किया जा सकता है।

Theorem 7.3.2 & Proof

Variance का final result \(V(\bar{y}_l) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2)\) आता है। इसका proof bias वाले expansion से ही निकलता है। Higher order term \(e_1 e_2\) को ignore करके हम first-order approximation \((\bar{y}_l - \bar{Y}) \cong e_0 - \beta e_1\) ले लेते हैं। दोनों तरफ square करके expectation लेने से सीधे हमें variance मिल जाता है, जिसमें \(\beta = \rho S_y / S_x\) substitute करने पर final formula बन जाता है।

Corollaries

Corollary 1: यह variance difference estimator से ज़्यादा precise होता है, और perfect correlation होने पर variance zero हो जाता है।

Corollary 2: Variance का almost unbiased sample estimate निकालने के लिए formula में \((n-2)\) का divisor use किया जाता है।

7.4 Comparison with the Mean per unit and Ratio Estimators

For a large sample size, the variance for estimators of the population mean \(\bar{Y}\) can be written as:

  • \( V(\bar{y}) = \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2 \)
  • \( V(\bar{y}_R) \cong \frac{1 - f}{n}(S_y^2 + R^2S_x^2 - 2R\rho S_yS_x) \)
  • \( V(\bar{y}_l) \cong \frac{1 - f}{n}S_y^2(1 - \rho^2) \)

Firstly, comparing with the mean per unit estimator, we observe that the variance of the regression estimator is always smaller unless \(\rho = 0\). In case \(\rho = 0\), the variances for both are equal. The reduction in variance is large when \(\rho\) is high.

Comparing next with the ratio estimator, it can be seen that the variance of the regression estimator is less than that of the ratio estimator if:

\( \rho^2 S_y^2 < R^2 S_x^2 - 2R\rho S_y S_x \)
\( (\rho S_y - R S_x)^2 > 0 \)

Which is always true unless \(\rho S_y = R S_x\) (i.e. \(\beta = R\)). In this situation, both estimates have the same variance and this occurs only when the regression of \(y\) on \(x\) is a straight line through the origin.

Rules for Choice Among Alternatives

The above comparisons suggest that the regression estimator is not always a proper choice although it provides a variance equal to or less than that of others. Some rules for a choice among alternatives are as follows:

  1. When advance information on an appropriate value of \(\beta_0\) is available, then, with simple computations good results can be obtained with the difference estimator.
  2. When the correlation coefficient \(\rho \cong S_x / S_y\), the difference estimator with \(\beta_0 = 1\) will attain equally precise results as the regression estimator.
  3. When the correlation coefficient \(\rho \cong C_x / C_y\), the ratio estimator will provide equally precise results as the regression estimator.
  4. When \(\beta \neq 1\), the regression estimator should be preferred over the difference estimator.
  5. When \(\beta \neq R\), the regression estimator should be preferred over the ratio estimator.
  6. When computations for the regression estimator are heavy, its use is recommended only if the gains are much more significant than the additional costs.

Example 7.1

Using the data to estimate the total number of milch animals in 117 villages by the method of regression estimation:

Given values:

\( N = 117, \quad n = 12, \quad \bar{y} = 899, \quad \bar{X} = 1032, \quad \bar{x} = 1007, \quad b = 0.81 \)

Regression Estimate of Total:

\( \widehat{Y}_l = N [\bar{y} + b(\bar{X} - \bar{x})] \)
\( \widehat{Y}_l = 117 [899 + 0.81(1032 - 1007)] = 107554 \)

Relative Precision over Mean per unit:

\( \frac{V(\bar{y})}{V(\bar{y}_l)} = 416.74\% \)

Relative Precision over Ratio:

\( \frac{V(\bar{y}_R)}{V(\bar{y}_l)} = 102.58\% \)

This shows the regression estimate provides the highest precision.

7.4 Comparison (तुलना)

Large sample size के लिए, आइये mean per unit, ratio और regression estimators की तुलना करते हैं:

Mean per unit से तुलना: Regression estimator का variance हमेशा कम होता है, सिवाय तब जब \(\rho = 0\) हो। Correlation \(\rho\) जितना ज़्यादा होगा, precision उतनी ही अच्छी होगी。

Ratio Estimator से तुलना: Regression estimator हमेशा ratio से बेहतर होता है, सिवाय उस case के जब \((\rho S_y - R S_x) = 0\) हो, यानी \(\beta = R\)। ऐसा सिर्फ तब होता है जब regression line origin से सीधी pass करे।

सही Estimator चुनने के Rules (नियम)

  1. अगर advance में \(\beta_0\) की value पता हो, तो simple computations से difference estimator best result देता है।
  2. अगर \(\rho \cong S_x / S_y\), तो difference estimator (\(\beta_0 = 1\)) और regression बराबर precision देंगे।
  3. अगर \(\rho \cong C_x / C_y\), तो ratio और regression दोनों बराबर precision देंगे।
  4. अगर \(\beta \neq 1\), तो regression estimator difference से बेहतर होता है।
  5. अगर \(\beta \neq R\), तो regression estimator ratio से बेहतर होता है।
  6. अगर regression के calculation time consuming और महंगे लग रहे हों, तभी इसको avoid करें।

Example 7.1

Milch animals estimate करने के data में (जहाँ \(N = 117, n = 12, \bar{y} = 899, \bar{X} = 1032, \bar{x} = 1007\)):

\( b = 0.81 \)

Regression Estimate of Total:

\( \widehat{Y}_l = 117 [899 + 0.81(1032 - 1007)] = 107554 \)

Relative Precision over Mean per unit:

Relative Precision over Ratio:

\( \frac{V(\bar{y}_R)}{V(\bar{y}_l)} = 102.58\% \)

यह साफ़ दर्शाता है कि regression सबसे efficient technique है।

7.5 Regression Estimators in Stratified Sampling

When the population is stratified into \(L\) strata and, in the \(h\)-th stratum, \(n_h\) units are selected from \(N_h\) units with simple random sampling, wor, like the ratio estimators, two regression estimators are possible. One is the separate regression estimator, and another is the combined regression estimator.

7.5.1 Separate Regression Estimator

A separate regression estimator \(\bar{y}_{ls}\) in stratified sampling is defined by estimating the regression coefficients for each stratum separately:

\( \bar{y}_{ls} = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h [\bar{y}_h + b_h(\bar{X}_h - \bar{x}_h)] \)

where \(W_h, \bar{y}_h, \bar{x}_h, \bar{X}_h\) and \(b_h\) are the corresponding terms for the \(h\)-th stratum.

Theorem 7.5.1

If sampling is independent in different strata and sample size is large enough in each stratum, then \(\bar{y}_{ls}\) is an almost unbiased estimator. To the first order of approximation, its variance is given by:

\( V(\bar{y}_{ls}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_h S_{hyx} + \beta_h^2 S_{hx}^2] \)

Proof:

The separate regression estimator is the sum of independent stratum estimates:

\( \bar{y}_{ls} = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h \bar{y}_{lh} \quad \text{where} \quad \bar{y}_{lh} = \bar{y}_h + b_h(\bar{X}_h - \bar{x}_h) \)

Since the samples are drawn independently in each stratum, the variance of the sum is the sum of their variances:

\( V(\bar{y}_{ls}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 V(\bar{y}_{lh}) \)

Applying the result from Theorem 7.3.2 to the \(h\)-th stratum individually, the large-sample variance of \(\bar{y}_{lh}\) is:

\( V(\bar{y}_{lh}) = V(\bar{y}_h) + \beta_h^2 V(\bar{x}_h) - 2\beta_h \text{Cov}(\bar{y}_h, \bar{x}_h) \)
\( V(\bar{y}_{lh}) = \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} (S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_h S_{hyx} + \beta_h^2 S_{hx}^2) \)

Substituting this back into the overall variance formula, we get the rigorously derived final result:

\( V(\bar{y}_{ls}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_h S_{hyx} + \beta_h^2 S_{hx}^2] \)

Corollaries

Corollary 1: If \(b_h = \beta_h\), the true regression coefficient in the \(h\)-th stratum is satisfied and variance remains the same.

Corollary 2: Provided sample size is large in all strata, an unbiased estimator of variance is obtained by substituting sample values \(s_{hy}^2, s_{hyx}, s_{hx}^2\).

Drawback: Since the leading terms in bias come from quadratic regression, they may be of the same sign in all strata. Hence, the overall bias in \(\bar{y}_{ls}\) can accumulate and become very large if sample sizes are small.

7.5.2 Combined Regression Estimator

A combined regression estimator \(\bar{y}_{lc}\) is defined by getting a common (pooled) regression coefficient \(b_c\) for all strata:

\( \bar{y}_{lc} = \bar{y}_{st} + b_c(\bar{X} - \bar{x}_{st}) \)

Theorem 7.5.2

If sampling is independent in different strata and sample size is large enough in each stratum, the variance of \(\bar{y}_{lc}\) is given by:

\( V(\bar{y}_{lc}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_c S_{hyx} + \beta_c^2 S_{hx}^2] \)

Proof:

Using Taylor series approximation with error terms, let \(\bar{y}_{st} = \bar{Y} + e_0\), \(\bar{x}_{st} = \bar{X} + e_1\), and \(b_c = \beta_c + e_2\). Substituting these in:

\( \bar{y}_{lc} = (\bar{Y} + e_0) + (\beta_c + e_2)(-e_1) = \bar{Y} + e_0 - \beta_c e_1 - e_1 e_2 \)

Ignoring the higher-order error term \(e_1 e_2\) for large samples, the error in the estimate is \(\bar{y}_{lc} - \bar{Y} \approx e_0 - \beta_c e_1\). The variance is the expectation of the squared error:

\( V(\bar{y}_{lc}) = E[(e_0 - \beta_c e_1)^2] = E(e_0^2) + \beta_c^2 E(e_1^2) - 2\beta_c E(e_0 e_1) \)
Recognizing these as the variances and covariance of stratified means:
\( V(\bar{y}_{lc}) = V(\bar{y}_{st}) + \beta_c^2 V(\bar{x}_{st}) - 2\beta_c \text{Cov}(\bar{y}_{st}, \bar{x}_{st}) \)

In stratified sampling, the variances and covariance are \(\sum W_h^2 \frac{1-f_h}{n_h} S_{hy}^2\), etc. Substituting these back, we assemble the final variance:

\( V(\bar{y}_{lc}) = \sum_{h=1}^L W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} (S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_c S_{hyx} + \beta_c^2 S_{hx}^2) \)

This completes the explicit step-by-step derivation.

Corollaries

Corollary 1: If \(\beta_h = \beta_c\), the combined regression coefficient minimizes the variance of \(\bar{y}_{lc}\).

Corollary 2: Provided sample size is large in all strata, the estimator of variance is obtained by using sample estimates.

7.5.3 Comparison

If regressions are approximately linear and coefficients appear to be the same in all strata, prefer the Combined Regression Estimator (smaller bias). If regressions are linear but coefficients differ from stratum to stratum, adopt the Separate Regression Estimator.

Example 7.2

Using stratified data to estimate the total number of trees in the districts:

(i) Separate Regression Estimate:

\( \widehat{Y}_{ls} = 18641 \)

Efficiency over ratio estimate:

\( \frac{V(\widehat{Y}_{Rs})}{V(\widehat{Y}_{ls})} = 95.8\% \)

(ii) Combined Regression Estimate:

\( \widehat{Y}_{lc} = 18635 \)

Efficiency over ratio estimate:

\( \frac{V(\widehat{Y}_{Rc})}{V(\widehat{Y}_{lc})} = 90.3\% \)

Efficiency of Separate vs Combined:

\( \frac{V(\widehat{Y}_{lc})}{V(\widehat{Y}_{ls})} = 106.06\% \)

This indicates the separate regression estimate is slightly more efficient here.

7.5 Regression Estimators in Stratified Sampling (स्तरीकृत प्रतिचयन)

जब population को \(L\) strata में बाँट कर simple random sampling की जाती है, तो ratio estimator की तरह यहाँ भी दो तरह के estimators बनते हैं: Separate (अलग) और Combined (संयुक्त)।

7.5.1 Separate Regression Estimator

इसमें हम हर stratum के लिए अलग regression coefficient \(b_h\) estimate करते हैं।

Theorem 7.5.1 & Proof

इसका variance \(\sum W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_h S_{hyx} + \beta_h^2 S_{hx}^2]\) होता है। इसको prove करना बिल्कुल आसान है: क्योंकि हर stratum में sampling independent है, इसलिए total variance हर stratum के variances का sum होगा। हम simply हर stratum पर Theorem 7.3.2 का formula लगाते हैं और सबको जोड़ देते हैं। यह derivation पूरी तरह clear है।

Drawback: छोटे samples में हर stratum का थोड़ा-थोड़ा bias जुड़कर एक बहुत बड़ा bias बन सकता है।

7.5.2 Combined Regression Estimator

इसमें सभी strata के लिए एक common (pooled) regression coefficient \(b_c\) निकालते हैं।

Theorem 7.5.2 & Proof

इसका variance \(\sum W_h^2 \frac{1 - f_h}{n_h} [S_{hy}^2 - 2\beta_c S_{hyx} + \beta_c^2 S_{hx}^2]\) होता है। इसका rigorous proof भी Taylor series से किया जाता है। Error terms \(e_0, e_1\) को मान कर हम expectation of squared error निकालते हैं। उसके बाद हम stratified means के variances को उनके standard formulas से replace करते हैं तो हमें exact result प्राप्त होता है।

7.5.3 Comparison (तुलना)

अगर सभी strata में regression line का slope (coefficient) same लग रहा हो, तो Combined Regression Estimator use करें। अगर slopes अलग-अलग हों, तो Separate Estimator ज़्यादा efficient रहेगा, जैसे कि Example 7.2 में देखा गया जहाँ Separate की efficiency 106.06% ज़्यादा थी।

Example 7.2

Districts में पेड़ों (trees) की कुल संख्या estimate करने के लिए stratified data का उपयोग:

(i) Separate Regression Estimate:

\( \widehat{Y}_{ls} = 18641 \)

Ratio estimate पर इसकी efficiency:

\( \frac{V(\widehat{Y}_{Rs})}{V(\widehat{Y}_{ls})} = 95.8\% \)

(ii) Combined Regression Estimate:

\( \widehat{Y}_{lc} = 18635 \)

Ratio estimate पर इसकी efficiency:

\( \frac{V(\widehat{Y}_{Rc})}{V(\widehat{Y}_{lc})} = 90.3\% \)

Separate vs Combined की Efficiency:

\( \frac{V(\widehat{Y}_{lc})}{V(\widehat{Y}_{ls})} = 106.06\% \)

यह दर्शाता है कि यहाँ separate regression estimate थोड़ा ज़्यादा efficient है।

7.6 Multi-variate Regression Estimator

Frequently, data on more than one auxiliary variate are available and it is important to make use of all available information. If \(\bar{y}\) is the usual estimator of the main variate, and we have multiple auxiliary variables, we use a multi-variate estimator:

\( \bar{y}_{lr} = \bar{y} + \sum_{i = 1}^k \beta_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{x}_i) \)

Des Raj (1965) proposed another difference estimator of the form:

\( \bar{y}_w = \sum_{i=1}^k w_i[\bar{y} + \beta_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{x}_i)] \)

where \(\sum w_i = 1\). By putting these in the variance expression, differentiating with respect to weights, and equating to zero, we get the optimum best weights. When comparing variances, we find that it is always better to use the second variate if they are not perfectly correlated. Similarly, it is always better to use one auxiliary variate than none.

7.6 Multi-variate Regression Estimator (बहुभिन्नरूपी प्रतिगमन आकलक)

अक्सर, एक से ज़्यादा auxiliary variates का data available होता है और सारी जानकारी का use करना फायदेमंद होता है। तब हम multi-variate estimator use करते हैं:

\( \bar{y}_{lr} = \bar{y} + \sum_{i = 1}^k \beta_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{x}_i) \)

Des Raj ने एक difference estimator प्रस्तुत किया जिसमें weights का use होता है और उनका sum 1 होता है। Optimum weights पाने के लिए variance को differentiate करके zero के बराबर रखा जाता है। Result यह मिलता है कि अगर दो variables perfectly correlated ना हों, तो एक की बजाय दोनों का use करना हमेशा बेहतर होता है जिससे variance कम हो जाता है।
