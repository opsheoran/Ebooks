6.1 Introduction

"There is repetition everywhere, and nothing is found only once in the world." -- Goethe

In many surveys, we often have information about an extra variable (called an auxiliary variable) that is closely related to the main variable we are studying. This extra information can help us make better sampling designs. We have already seen this in Stratified sampling (Chapter 3) and PPS sampling (Chapter 5). In those methods, we needed to know the auxiliary data for every single unit before even starting the survey.

But what if we only know the total sum of the auxiliary variable for the whole population, and not for each unit separately? In that case, those previous methods won't work. However, we can still use this total sum during the estimation stage to get better results. This leads us to two important methods: the ratio method and the regression method. In this chapter, we will focus on the ratio method of estimation.

6.1 परिचय (Introduction)

"हर जगह दोहराव है, और दुनिया में कोई भी चीज़ केवल एक बार नहीं पाई जाती है।" -- Goethe

कई surveys में, एक auxiliary variate की जानकारी जो study variable के साथ highly correlated है, आसानी से उपलब्ध होती है और इसका उपयोग sampling design सुधारने के लिए किया जा सकता है। Stratified sampling और pps schemes बेहतर sampling designs के दो उदाहरण हैं, जिनमें auxiliary variate के data का उपयोग Chapters 3 और 5 में किया गया है। हालाँकि, इन दोनों schemes में यह माना जाता है कि sampling design बनाने से पहले individual sampling units पर auxiliary variate की जानकारी उपलब्ध है। यदि individual sampling units के लिए data उपलब्ध नहीं है, बल्कि केवल aggregate value उपलब्ध है, तो इन दोनों schemes का उपयोग नहीं किया जा सकता।

ऐसी स्थिति में, parameters estimate करते समय auxiliary variate के aggregated data का उपयोग फिर भी किया जा सकता है, बशर्ते sampled units के लिए auxiliary data आसानी से प्राप्त किया जा सके। Estimation की ऐसी दो विधियों को ratio method of estimation और regression method of estimation कहा जाता है। इस chapter में, हम ratio method of estimation पर चर्चा करेंगे।

6.2 Definitions and Notations

Before we look at the formulas, let's define the symbols we will use. Imagine we are studying a characteristic \( y \) (like crop yield), and we have an auxiliary variable \( x \) (like the area of the farm):

  • \( y_i \) = the value of the main variable for the \( i \)-th unit in the population.
  • \( x_i \) = the value of the auxiliary variable for that same unit.
  • \( Y = \sum y_i \) = the total sum of the main variable for the entire population.
  • \( X = \sum x_i \) = the total sum of the auxiliary variable for the entire population.
  • \( R = Y/X = \bar{Y}/\bar{X} \) = the actual ratio of the population totals or means.
  • \( \rho \) = the correlation coefficient showing how closely \( x \) and \( y \) are related.

Now, suppose we take a simple random sample of size \( n \). From this sample, we calculate the sample means \( \bar{y} \) and \( \bar{x} \). If we know the true population value of \( X \) or \( \bar{X} \), we can estimate the population ratio, total, and mean using these ratio estimators:

First, we estimate the ratio \( R \) using our sample means:

$$ \widehat{R} = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}} \quad (6.2.1) $$

Then, we use this estimated ratio to find the population total \( Y \) and the mean \( \bar{Y} \):

$$ \widehat{Y}_R = \widehat{R}X = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}}X $$ $$ \bar{y}_R = \widehat{R}\bar{X} = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}}\bar{X} $$

Here, \( \bar{y} \) and \( \bar{x} \) are the sample means we calculated from our survey.

6.2 Definitions and Notations (परिभाषाएं और संकेतन)

आइए हम दर्शाते हैं:

  • \( y_i \) = population की \( i \)-th unit के लिए study variable का value
  • \( x_i \) = उसी unit पर auxiliary characteristic का value
  • \( Y = \sum y_i \) = population के \( y \) characteristic का कुल योग (total)
  • \( X = \sum x_i \) = population के \( x \) characteristic का कुल योग
  • \( R = Y/X = \bar{Y}/\bar{X} \) = characteristics \( y \) और \( x \) के population totals या means का ratio
  • \( \rho \) = population में \( x \) और \( y \) के बीच correlation coefficient

मान लीजिए population से \( n \) units का simple random sample निकालकर \( Y \), \( \bar{Y} \) या \( R \) का अनुमान लगाना है। मान लें कि \( n \) observations के आधार पर, \( \bar{y} \) और \( \bar{x} \) क्रमशः \( y \) और \( x \) के sample means हैं, और population total \( X \) या mean \( \bar{X} \) ज्ञात है। Ratio \( R \), total \( Y \) और mean \( \bar{Y} \) के ratio estimators इस प्रकार परिभाषित किए जा सकते हैं:

$$ \widehat{R} = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}} \quad (6.2.1) $$

और

$$ \widehat{Y}_R = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}}X = \widehat{R}X, \quad \bar{y}_R = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}}\bar{X} = \widehat{R}\bar{X}, \quad \text{क्रमशः} $$

जहाँ \( \bar{y} \) और \( \bar{x} \) क्रमशः \( y \) और \( x \) के sample totals हैं।

6.3 Bias of Ratio Estimators

Usually, we prefer estimators that are "unbiased" (meaning their average value is exactly the population value). However, in many cases, a "biased" estimator might be more precise or efficient. The ratio estimator is one such example—it has a small bias, but it often gives much more accurate results than simple estimators. In this section, we will calculate exactly how much bias exists in these estimators.

Theorem 6.3.1

In simple random sampling, the bias of the ratio estimator \( \widehat{R} \) is related to the covariance between the estimator and the sample mean of the auxiliary variable:

$$ B(\widehat{R}) = - \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} \quad (6.3.1) $$

Proof:

We know that the covariance between any two variables \( u \) and \( v \) is defined as the expected value of their product minus the product of their expected values:

$$ \text{Cov}(u,v) = E(uv) - E(u)E(v) $$

Let's apply this formula to our ratio estimator \( \widehat{R} \) and the sample mean \( \bar{x} \):

$$ \text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x}) = E(\widehat{R}\bar{x}) - E(\bar{x})E(\widehat{R}) $$

Recall that \( \widehat{R} = \bar{y}/\bar{x} \). If we multiply it by \( \bar{x} \), we simply get \( \bar{y} \). So the first term \( E(\widehat{R}\bar{x}) \) is actually \( E(\bar{y}) \):

$$ \text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x}) = E(\bar{y}) - E(\bar{x})E(\widehat{R}) $$

In simple random sampling, the expected value of a sample mean is the true population mean. Therefore, \( E(\bar{y}) = \bar{Y} \) and \( E(\bar{x}) = \bar{X} \):

$$ \text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x}) = \bar{Y} - \bar{X}E(\widehat{R}) $$

Now, let's divide the entire equation by the population mean \( \bar{X} \):

$$ \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} = \frac{\bar{Y}}{\bar{X}} - E(\widehat{R}) $$

Since the population ratio \( R \) is defined as \( \bar{Y}/\bar{X} \), we have:

$$ \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} = R - E(\widehat{R}) $$

By definition, Bias is the difference between the expected value of an estimator and the true value, i.e., \( B(\widehat{R}) = E(\widehat{R}) - R \). So, \( R - E(\widehat{R}) \) is simply the negative bias:

$$ \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} = - B(\widehat{R}) $$

Rearranging this gives us our result:

$$ B(\widehat{R}) = - \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} $$

Corollary 1

The absolute bias is always smaller than or equal to the standard error of the ratio estimator multiplied by the coefficient of variation of \( \bar{x} \):

$$ |B(\widehat{R})| \leq \sigma_{\widehat{R}} \cdot CV(\bar{x}) $$

Theorem 6.3.2

For a large sample, the relative bias (bias divided by the true value) of the ratio estimator can be approximated as:

$$ \frac{B(\widehat{R})}{R} \approx \frac{1 - f}{n}(C_x^2 - \rho C_x C_y) \quad (6.3.2) $$

Proof:

Let's look at the error in our ratio estimator:

$$ \widehat{R} - R = \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}} - R = \frac{\bar{y} - R\bar{x}}{\bar{x}} $$

We can rewrite this by introducing the population mean \( \bar{X} \) in the denominator:

$$ \widehat{R} - R = \frac{\bar{y} - R\bar{x}}{\bar{X} + (\bar{x} - \bar{X})} = \frac{\bar{y} - R\bar{x}}{\bar{X}} \left( 1 + \frac{\bar{x} - \bar{X}}{\bar{X}} \right)^{-1} $$

Using the Taylor series expansion \( (1+z)^{-1} \approx 1 - z \), we get a first approximation:

$$ \widehat{R} - R \approx \frac{\bar{y} - R\bar{x}}{\bar{X}} \left( 1 - \frac{\bar{x} - \bar{X}}{\bar{X}} \right) = \frac{\bar{y} - R\bar{x}}{\bar{X}} - \frac{(\bar{y} - R\bar{x})(\bar{x} - \bar{X})}{\bar{X}^2} $$

Now, let's take the expected value to find the bias. Note that \( E(\bar{y} - R\bar{x}) = \bar{Y} - R\bar{X} = 0 \), so the first term vanishes:

$$ E(\widehat{R} - R) \approx - \frac{1}{\bar{X}^2} E [ (\bar{y} - R\bar{x})(\bar{x} - \bar{X}) ] $$

Expanding the term inside the brackets:

$$ E [ (\bar{y} - R\bar{x})(\bar{x} - \bar{X}) ] = E [ (\bar{y} - \bar{Y} - R(\bar{x} - \bar{X})) (\bar{x} - \bar{X}) ] = E [ (\bar{y} - \bar{Y})(\bar{x} - \bar{X}) ] - R E [ (\bar{x} - \bar{X})^2 ] $$

In SRSWOR, these are simply the formulas for sample covariance and variance of means:

$$ = \frac{1 - f}{n} S_{xy} - R \frac{1 - f}{n} S_x^2 = \frac{1 - f}{n} (\rho S_x S_y - R S_x^2) $$

Substituting this back, we get the Bias:

$$ B(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n \bar{X}^2} (R S_x^2 - \rho S_x S_y) $$

Finally, we divide by \( R \) and use the definitions of coefficients of variation (\( C_x = S_x/\bar{X} \) and \( C_y = S_y/\bar{Y} \)):

$$ \frac{B(\widehat{R})}{R} \approx \frac{1 - f}{n} \left( \frac{S_x^2}{\bar{X}^2} - \rho \frac{S_x S_y}{R \bar{X}^2} \right) = \frac{1 - f}{n} (C_x^2 - \rho C_x C_y) $$

6.3 Bias of Ratio Estimators (Ratio Estimators का Bias)

पिछले chapters में हमने unbiased estimators की बात की, लेकिन कई बार biased estimators का उपयोग तब किया जाता है जब वे अधिक efficient हों। कई स्थितियों में precision बढ़ाने के लिए cost बदले बिना ऐसे estimators का उपयोग किया जाता है। यहाँ यह दिखाया जाएगा कि \( \widehat{R} \), \( \widehat{Y}_R \) और \( \bar{y}_R \) सभी biased estimators हैं, लेकिन कुछ conditions के तहत इन्हें unbiased estimators से बेहतर माना जाता है। चूंकि थ्योरी समान है, इसलिए ज़्यादातर results ratio estimate करने से जुड़े होंगे।

Theorem 6.3.1

Simple random sampling में, ratio estimator \( \widehat{R} \) का bias इस प्रकार है:

$$ B(\widehat{R}) = - \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} \quad (6.3.1) $$

Proof:

हम जानते हैं कि \( \text{Cov}(u,v) = E(uv) - E(u)E(v) \)

$$ \text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x}) = E(\widehat{R}\bar{x}) - E(\bar{x})E(\widehat{R}) $$

अतः,

$$ = E(\bar{y}) - \bar{X}E(\widehat{R}) $$ $$ = \bar{Y} - \bar{X}E(\widehat{R}) $$
\( \because \) \( \bar{y} \) का expected value population mean \( \bar{Y} \) है।
$$ \frac{\text{Cov}(\widehat{R},\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} = \frac{\bar{Y}}{\bar{X}} - E(\widehat{R}) = R - E(\widehat{R}) = - B(\widehat{R}) $$
\( \because \) bias की mathematical definition \( E(\widehat{R}) - R \) है।

Corollary 1

सिद्ध कीजिए कि

$$ |B(\widehat{R})| \leq CV(\bar{x}) $$

जहाँ \( CV \), coefficient of variation है।

Corollary 2

दिखाइए कि

$$ B(\widehat{Y}_R) = XB(\widehat{R}) \quad तथा \quad B(\bar{y}_R) = \bar{X}B(\widehat{R}) $$

Corollary 3

सिद्ध कीजिए कि \( \widehat{R} \) unbiased है यदि \( \rho = 0 \) हो।

Theorem 6.3.2

दिखाइए कि simple random sampling (wor) में relative bias का first approximation इस प्रकार है:

$$ \frac{B(\widehat{R})}{R} \approx \frac{1 - f}{n}(C_x^2 - \rho C_x C_y) \quad (6.3.2) $$

Proof:

Taylor series expansion का उपयोग करके और second एवं higher orders के terms को ignore करने पर:

$$ E(\widehat{R} - R) = - \frac{1}{\bar{X}^2}E[(\bar{y} - R\bar{x})(\bar{x} - \bar{X})] $$

Standard definitions substitute करने पर हमें result मिलता है।

बड़े \( n \) और \( \rho \) के उच्च मान के लिए bias नगण्य होगा। Ratio estimator में bias तब शून्य होता है जब regression line origin से गुजरती है। सन्निकटन के दूसरे क्रम (second order) तक relative bias इस प्रकार है:

$$ B_2 = B_1 + 3C_x(C_x^2 - \rho C_x C_y) \quad (6.3.3) $$

Corollary 1

Bias और standard error के ratio का upper bound:

$$ \left| \frac{B(\widehat{R})}{\text{S.E.}(\widehat{R})} \right| \leq C_x \quad (6.3.4) $$

Corollary 2

Bias \( B(\widehat{R}) \) का estimator:

$$ \widehat{B}(\widehat{R}) = \frac{1}{\bar{X}}\left[ \widehat{R}v(\bar{x}) - \text{cov}(\bar{x},\bar{y}) \right] \quad (6.3.5) $$

6.4 Approximate Variance of Ratio Estimator

Since the ratio estimator is slightly biased, we usually compare it with other estimators using the "Mean Square Error" (MSE). However, for larger samples, this bias becomes so small that we can just focus on the variance. In this section, we will derive the formulas for the variance of our ratio estimators.

Theorem 6.4.1

In simple random sampling (without replacement), for a large sample size \( n \), the variance of the ratio estimator \( \widehat{R} \) is approximately:

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2} \frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - Rx_i)^2 \quad (6.4.1) $$

Proof:

The variance of an estimator is the average squared error. For a large sample, we can approximate this using the true population ratio \( R \):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) = E[\widehat{R} - E(\widehat{R})]^2 \approx E(\widehat{R} - R)^2 $$

Now, let's substitute the formula for \( \widehat{R} = \bar{y}/\bar{x} \):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx E \left[ \frac{\bar{y}}{\bar{x}} - R \right]^2 = E \left[ \frac{\bar{y} - R\bar{x}}{\bar{x}} \right]^2 $$

For a large sample, the sample mean \( \bar{x} \) will be very close to the true population mean \( \bar{X} \). So we can replace \( \bar{x} \) in the denominator with \( \bar{X} \):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1}{\bar{X}^2} E (\bar{y} - R\bar{x})^2 $$

Let's define a new variable \( u_i = y_i - Rx_i \). The sample mean of this variable is \( \bar{u} = \bar{y} - R\bar{x} \), and its population mean is \( \bar{U} = \bar{Y} - R\bar{X} = 0 \). This simplifies our expression to:

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1}{\bar{X}^2} E (\bar{u} - \bar{U})^2 = \frac{1}{\bar{X}^2} V(\bar{u}) $$

From Chapter 2, we know that the variance of a sample mean is \( \frac{1 - f}{n} S_u^2 \):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1}{\bar{X}^2} \frac{1 - f}{n} \left[ \frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^N (u_i - \bar{U})^2 \right] $$

Substituting back \( u_i = y_i - Rx_i \) and \( \bar{U} = 0 \), we get our result:

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2} \frac{1}{N - 1} \sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - Rx_i)^2 $$

Theorem 6.4.2

We can also express the variance of \( \widehat{R} \) using the variances and correlation of \( x \) and \( y \):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2} [S_y^2 + R^2 S_x^2 - 2R\rho S_x S_y] \quad (6.4.2) $$ $$ = \frac{1 - f}{n} R^2 [C_y^2 + C_x^2 - 2\rho C_x C_y] $$

Proof:

Let's expand the squared term inside the summation from formula (6.4.1):

$$ (y_i - Rx_i)^2 = [ (y_i - \bar{Y}) - R(x_i - \bar{X}) ]^2 $$

Expanding the square gives us three terms:

$$ (y_i - Rx_i)^2 = (y_i - \bar{Y})^2 + R^2 (x_i - \bar{X})^2 - 2R(y_i - \bar{Y})(x_i - \bar{X}) $$

Now, let's sum this over the entire population and divide by \( N - 1 \):

$$ \frac{\sum (y_i - Rx_i)^2}{N - 1} = \frac{\sum (y_i - \bar{Y})^2}{N - 1} + R^2 \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{X})^2}{N - 1} - \frac{2R \sum (y_i - \bar{Y})(x_i - \bar{X})}{N - 1} $$

These terms are exactly the definitions of population variances \( S_y^2, S_x^2 \) and population covariance \( S_{xy} \):

$$ \frac{\sum (y_i - Rx_i)^2}{N - 1} = S_y^2 + R^2 S_x^2 - 2R S_{xy} $$

Since \( S_{xy} = \rho S_x S_y \), we can substitute this into formula (6.4.1) to get the first form:

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2} [ S_y^2 + R^2 S_x^2 - 2R \rho S_x S_y ] $$

To get the second form, we divide everything by \( R^2 \) and use the coefficients of variation (\( C_y = S_y/\bar{Y} \) and \( C_x = S_x/\bar{X} \)):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n} R^2 \left[ \frac{S_y^2}{R^2 \bar{X}^2} + \frac{S_x^2}{\bar{X}^2} - \frac{2 \rho S_x S_y}{R \bar{X}^2} \right] $$

Since \( \bar{Y} = R\bar{X} \), the first term inside the brackets becomes \( S_y^2/\bar{Y}^2 \), which is \( C_y^2 \):

$$ V(\widehat{R}) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n} R^2 [ C_y^2 + C_x^2 - 2 \rho C_x C_y ] $$

Corollary 1: Variance of Estimators of Total and Mean

The variances for the population mean and total estimators are found by multiplying the variance of \( \widehat{R} \) by \( \bar{X}^2 \) or \( X^2 \):

$$ V(\bar{y}_R) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n} \bar{Y}^2 [C_y^2 + C_x^2 - 2\rho C_x C_y] $$ $$ V(\widehat{Y}_R) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n} Y^2 [C_y^2 + C_x^2 - 2\rho C_x C_y] $$

Corollary 2: Sample Estimate of Variance

In practice, we don't know the population values, so we use sample estimates \( s_y^2, s_x^2, r \) and \( \widehat{R} \):

$$ v(\widehat{R}) = \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2} [s_y^2 + \widehat{R}^2 s_x^2 - 2\widehat{R}r s_y s_x] \quad (6.4.7) $$

6.4 Approximate Variance of Ratio Estimator (अनुमानित Variance)

चूंकि ratio estimators biased होते हैं, इसलिए comparison के लिए mean square errors पर विचार करना चाहिए। हालाँकि ये biased हैं, पर ये consistent हैं, और moderate samples के लिए bias नगण्य होता है। व्यावहारिक उद्देश्यों के लिए approximate variance precision comparison हेतु मान्य है।

Theorem 6.4.1

Simple random sampling (wor) में बड़े \( n \) के लिए, \( \widehat{R} \) का approximate variance इस प्रकार है:

$$ V(\widehat{R}) = \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2}\frac{1}{N - 1}\sum (y_i - Rx_i)^2 \quad (6.4.1) $$

Proof:

\( u_i = y_i - Rx_i \) मानकर और Theorem 2.3.1 का उपयोग करके हमें result मिलता है।

Corollary 1 & 2

\( V(\bar{y}_R) \) और \( V(\widehat{Y}_R) \) के expressions (अंग्रेजी section देखें) इसी प्रकार प्राप्त किए जाते हैं।

Theorem 6.4.2

First order of approximation तक \( V(\widehat{R}) \) को इस प्रकार व्यक्त किया जा सकता है:

$$ V(\widehat{R}) = \frac{1 - f}{n\bar{X}^2}[S_y^2 + R^2 S_x^2 - 2R\rho S_x S_y] \quad (6.4.2) $$ $$ = \frac{1 - f}{n}R^2[C_y^2 + C_x^2 - 2\rho C_x C_y] $$

Corollary 3 & 4

Relative variance \( (CV)^2 \) को Hansen et al. (1953) ने relative variance कहा है। यदि \( C_x = C_y = C \) हो, तो:

$$ (CV)^2 = \frac{1 - f}{n}2C^2(1 - \rho) \quad (6.4.6) $$

Corollary 5

Sample estimate of variance:

$$ v(\widehat{R}) = \frac{1 - f}{n(n - 1)\bar{X}^2}\sum (\bar{y}_i - \widehat{R}\bar{x}_i)^2 \quad (6.4.7) $$

6.5 Comparison of Ratio Estimator with Mean per Unit

When is the ratio estimator better than the simple sample mean? To answer this, we compare their variances. We know that in simple random sampling, the variance of the sample mean is:

$$ V(\bar{y}) = \frac{1 - f}{n} S_y^2 $$

We also found that the variance of the ratio estimator (to the first order) is:

$$ V(\bar{y}_R) \approx \frac{1 - f}{n} (S_y^2 + R^2 S_x^2 - 2R \rho S_x S_y) $$

The ratio estimator is more efficient if its variance is smaller than the simple mean's variance:

Setting up the inequality:

$$ V(\bar{y}_R) < V(\bar{y}) $$ $$ \frac{1 - f}{n} (S_y^2 + R^2 S_x^2 - 2R \rho S_x S_y) < \frac{1 - f}{n} S_y^2 $$

We can cancel out the common factor \( \frac{1 - f}{n} \) and subtract \( S_y^2 \) from both sides:

$$ R^2 S_x^2 - 2R \rho S_x S_y < 0 $$

Moving the negative term to the right and dividing by \( 2R S_x S_y \):

$$ \rho > \frac{R S_x}{2 S_y} $$

Since \( R = \bar{Y} / \bar{X} \), we can rewrite this using the coefficients of variation (\( C_x = S_x / \bar{X} \) and \( C_y = S_y / \bar{Y} \)):

$$ \rho > \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\bar{Y}}{\bar{X}} \right) \frac{S_x}{S_y} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{S_x / \bar{X}}{S_y / \bar{Y}} $$ $$ \rho > \frac{1}{2} \frac{C_x}{C_y} \quad (6.5.1) $$

Conclusion: The ratio estimator is superior if the correlation \( \rho \) is high enough. If \( C_x \approx C_y \), then the ratio estimator is better if \( \rho > 0.5 \). However, if the auxiliary variable is very spread out (if \( C_x > 2C_y \)), the ratio estimator will always be less precise than the simple mean.

6.6 Confidence Limits

For large samples, we can assume that our estimates (the mean or total) follow a normal distribution. This allows us to calculate "confidence limits," which is the range where we are fairly sure the true population value lies. For the population total, the limits are:

$$ \widehat{Y}_R \pm z \cdot \sigma(\widehat{Y}_R) \quad (6.6.1) $$

where \( z \) is a value from the normal distribution table (like 1.96 for 95% confidence).

Similarly, the range for the population ratio \( R \) is:

$$ \widehat{R} \pm z \cdot \sigma(\widehat{R}) \quad (6.6.2) $$

For very small samples, calculating these limits is a bit more complex and involves solving a quadratic equation. A simpler version for small samples is:

$$ \widehat{R} \pm \frac{t}{\sqrt{n - 1}} \left[ (1 - g)(c_y^2 + c_x^2) - 2rc_xc_y \right]^{1/2} \quad (6.6.4) $$

6.6 Confidence Limits (विश्वास सीमाएं)

बड़े samples के लिए normal distribution मानकर confidence limits इस प्रकार होंगी:

$$ \widehat{Y}_R \pm z\sigma(\widehat{Y}_R) $$

छोटे samples के लिए bivariate normal distribution मानकर limits निकाली जाती हैं। \( R \) की limits को Eq. (6.6.4) द्वारा सरल रूप में दिखाया जा सकता है (अंग्रेजी section देखें)।

6.7 Ratio Estimators in Stratified Sampling

If your population is divided into different groups (strata), you have two main ways to use the ratio method to estimate the total population value \( Y \):

6.7.1 Separate Ratio Estimator

In this method, we calculate a separate ratio for *every single stratum* and then add them up. This is useful if the relationship between \( x \) and \( y \) is different in each group.

$$ \widehat{Y}_{Rs} = \sum_{m} \frac{\bar{y}_m}{\bar{x}_m} X_m = \sum_{m} \widehat{R}_m X_m \quad (6.7.1) $$

When to use: Use this if you have a large sample in *each* stratum.

6.7.2 Combined Ratio Estimator

In this method, we first find the overall stratified means (\( \bar{y}_{st} \) and \( \bar{x}_{st} \)) and then calculate one single ratio for the whole population.

$$ \widehat{Y}_{Rc} = \frac{\bar{y}_{st}}{\bar{x}_{st}} X \quad (6.7.4) $$

When to use: Use this if your sample sizes in individual strata are small. It helps keep the bias low.

6.7.3 Which one is better?

The **Separate estimator** is usually more accurate if your strata are very different from each other. However, if your samples are small, the **Combined estimator** is safer because it has less bias.

6.7 Ratio Estimators in Stratified Sampling (Stratified Sampling में Ratio Estimators)

Stratified population के लिए ratio estimate के दो तरीके हैं:

6.7.1 Separate Ratio Estimator (अलग अनुपात अनुमानक)

इसमें प्रत्येक stratum के लिए अलग ratio निकाला जाता है:

$$ \widehat{Y}_{Rs} = \sum \widehat{R}_mX_m $$

यह तब ज़्यादा सटीक है जब प्रत्येक stratum में sample size काफी बड़ा हो।

6.7.2 Combined Ratio Estimator (संयुक्त अनुपात अनुमानक)

इसमें पहले strata के means का average लिया जाता है, फिर ratio निकाला जाता है:

$$ \widehat{Y}_{Rc} = \frac{\bar{y}_{st}}{\bar{x}_{st}}X $$

जब individual stratum के sample sizes छोटे हों, तब इसे प्राथमिकता दी जाती है क्योंकि इसका bias कम होता है।

6.7.3 Comparison (तुलना)

अगर अलग-अलग strata में \( R_m \) बहुत ज़्यादा vary करता है, तो separate ratio estimator बेहतर है। अन्यथा bias कम रखने के लिए combined estimator अच्छा रहता है।

6.8 Unbiased Ratio Estimator and its Variance

As we saw earlier, the standard ratio estimator is usually biased. However, there are special sampling tricks to make it completely unbiased. One famous method was suggested by Midzuno (1951).

The Midzuno Scheme: Instead of picking all units with equal chance, we pick the *first* unit with probability proportional to its \( x_i \) value. Then, we pick the remaining units normally (with equal probability). If we use this method, the simple ratio estimator becomes perfectly unbiased!

The probability of picking a specific sample \( s \) is:

$$ P(s) = \frac{\sum_{i \in s} x_i}{X \binom{N - 1}{n - 1}} \quad (6.8.1) $$

Under this scheme, the expected value of our ratio estimator is exactly the population total:

$$ E(\widehat{Y}_R) = Y $$

6.8 Unbiased Ratio Estimator and its Variance (Unbiased Ratio Estimator)

Simple random sampling में ratio estimator biased होता है। Midzuno (1951) ने एक sampling scheme सुझाई जिससे यह unbiased हो जाता है। इसमें पहली unit \( x_i \) के proportional probability से चुनी जाती है और बाकी units equal probability से। इस scheme में \( E(\widehat{Y}_R) = Y \) होता है।

6.9 Unbiased Ratio-type Estimator

Another way to remove bias is to change the estimator itself. Hartley and Ross (1954) proposed a modified version. Instead of taking the ratio of means, they took the average of individual ratios (\( r_i = y_i / x_i \)) and applied a correction factor.

The Hartley-Ross unbiased estimator is:

$$ \widehat{R}_1 = \bar{r} + \frac{n(N - 1)}{N(n - 1)} \frac{(\bar{y} - \bar{r}\bar{x})}{\bar{X}} \quad (6.9.1) $$

where \( \bar{r} \) is the average of the ratios in your sample.

For large samples, the accuracy of this estimator is almost the same as the standard ratio estimator, but it has the advantage of being unbiased.

6.9 Unbiased Ratio-type Estimator (हार्टले-रॉस अनुमानक)

Hartley और Ross (1954) ने ratios के average (\( \bar{r} \)) को bias के लिए correct करके एक unbiased estimator दिया। बड़े samples के लिए इसका variance सामान्य ratio estimator जैसा ही होता है।

6.10 Product Estimator

What if your study variable \( y \) and auxiliary variable \( x \) are **negatively** related? (For example, as the price of a product goes up, the number of sales goes down). In this case, the ratio method won't work well. Instead, we use the **Product Estimator**.

The product estimator for the population mean is:

$$ \bar{y}_P = \bar{y} \frac{\bar{x}}{\bar{X}} \quad (6.10.1) $$

This method is more efficient than the simple mean if the correlation \( \rho \) is very negative (specifically, if \( \rho < - \frac{1}{2} \frac{C_x}{C_y} \)).

6.10 Product Estimator (Product Estimator)

अगर \( y \) और \( x \) के बीच correlation negative हो, तो ratio estimator काम नहीं आता। ऐसे में product estimator (\( \bar{y}_P = \bar{y}\bar{x}/\bar{X} \)) का उपयोग किया जाता है। यह तब ज़्यादा efficient है जब \( \rho \) काफी negative हो।

6.11 Multivariate Ratio Estimator

Sometimes, we have more than one auxiliary variable (like knowing both the farm area *and* the amount of fertilizer used). In these cases, we can use all of them together to get an even better estimate. This is called the **Multivariate Ratio Estimator**, proposed by Olkin (1958).

The multivariate estimator is a weighted average of individual ratio estimators:

$$ \widehat{Y}_{MR} = W_1 \widehat{Y}_{R1} + W_2 \widehat{Y}_{R2} + \dots + W_k \widehat{Y}_{Rk} \quad (6.11.1) $$

where the weights \( W_i \) are chosen carefully to make the final result as precise as possible.

6.11 Multivariate Ratio Estimator (Multivariate Ratio Estimator)

जब एक से ज़्यादा auxiliary variables (\( x_1, x_2, \ldots \)) उपलब्ध हों, तब Olkin (1958) का multivariate estimator उपयोग किया जाता है। इसमें अलग-अलग ratio estimators को weights \( W_i \) देकर जोड़ा जाता है। weights इस तरह चुने जाते हैं कि precision maximize हो सके।
